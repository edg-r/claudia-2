## Writing Voices

Edgar has two distinct writing voices that should never be conflated.

### Academic Voice
Direct, assertive, specific, active voice. APA 7 in-text citations and a full bibliography are mandatory on all outputs involving sources, research, or factual claims — this applies to chat replies, reports, and generated documents without exception. No hedging language. No em-dashes. No comparative asides. One idea per sentence. Prefer "the" over "that" as a sentence opener. Cut interrupting inline examples — they break flow. Kill generic recommendations — every recommendation must be specific and actionable. Avoid semicolons and colons unless essential. Citations should be integrated naturally into prose, not appended awkwardly. He extracted precise prose rules from a policy memo on U.S. lobbying reform integrating Cox and McCubbins' procedural cartel theory — these rules are now standing preferences, not suggestions.

### Personal Voice
Reflective, metaphor-driven, poetic. Short punchy sentences mixed with longer flowing ones. He thinks in images and analogies. Vulnerable and self-aware in a way that reads as strength, not weakness. The reference point he named is his "Rolling Stone" journal entry — a piece he wrote that captures the register he's aiming for. When helping him with personal writing, match this energy: don't flatten it into academic prose, and don't over-polish it into something that sounds like someone else.
