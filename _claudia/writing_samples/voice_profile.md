# Edgar Agunias — Voice Profile

This profile is built from four writing samples spanning two registers: two graduate policy memos (the ROK drone-warfare memo dated December 1, 2025 and the Greenland Principal-Agent memo dated January 20, 2026) and two pieces of personal prose (the Rolling Stone reflection and the GPS UCSD statement of purpose plus its drafting fragments). The goal is descriptive, not prescriptive. Any agent editing or drafting in Edgar's voice should read this end to end before touching a sentence.

## Sentence-Level Rhythm

Edgar's native cadence is short-medium with deliberate terminal fragments. He opens most paragraphs with a mid-length declarative sentence that states a claim or sets a scene, then lets the rhythm shorten as the paragraph lands. In the Rolling Stone piece this is almost a poetic pattern: "People in my life have always come and go. Often I am the one who leaves. I ask if its better to be left or to do the leaving." The three sentences step down from ten words to eight to twelve, and the paragraph keeps alternating long and short until it ends on the blunt "Maybe too easily." He likes terminal one-beat sentences. He uses them to land a paragraph the way a drummer uses a rim shot.

In the memos the same pattern survives but gets dressed up. Opening sentences of the Greenland memo's Executive Summary run long (a 32-word sentence beginning "With significant Agents pushing back...") but he still breaks to a short landing sentence: "we should refrain from kinetic action as Danish and more broadly EU resolve to hold Greenland's sovereignty has been clearly signaled." The instinct to end on a clear verdict rather than trail off into hedging is consistent across both registers.

Sentence openings show a strong preference for prepositional and participial scene-setters. "With repeated signals...," "With Multiple veto subgroups...," "By tailoring contract design...," "By stationing ROK officers...," "After the 2022 invasion...," "Throughout my studies...," "For most of my life..." He leads with the condition or circumstance, then delivers the subject and verb. This is a consistent tic across all four samples. He almost never opens with "This" or "There is/are." He does open with "I" freely in personal prose and avoids it almost entirely in memos, substituting "the ROK," "the President," or the nominalized agent.

Fragments appear deliberately in personal prose. "As a foreigner, as an outsider, as an American outside of America." "Back and forth. Couple years here, couple years there." These are not accidents of a rushed first draft (though some clearly are); they are rhythmic choices. Any editor who converts them to complete sentences flattens the voice.

## Diction Range

The register gap between Edgar's memo voice and personal voice is real but narrower than most graduate writers. In memos he reaches for domain nouns (contract design, veto subgroups, delegated authority, adverse selection, monitoring and reporting, fire alarm reporting, burden sharing, kinetic action, ISR, SOP, doctrine, tradecraft) and theory anchors by name (Principal Agent Theory, Kiewet & McCubbins). He capitalizes key theoretical terms mid-sentence as a signal of technical reference: "Principal Agent Lense," "Agents," "Principal," "Monitoring and Reporting." This is idiosyncratic and, read generously, a deliberate flag to the reader that these are terms of art. Any editor should preserve the capitalization pattern rather than silently lowercasing.

In personal prose the diction drops into direct, almost spoken English. "I just cant help it." "I grow smaller and smaller. Until there is little left to kick." He uses academic vocabulary sparingly and only when it earns its keep: "simulacrum of American comforts," "Colonial Episteme, Racism, Capital and Habitus," "Third Culture Kid." When he reaches for a big word in personal writing, he does it once and moves on. He does not stack jargon. He does not decorate.

A notable shared feature across registers: he likes concrete verbs of motion and force. "Kick," "roll," "scrape," "scattered," "percolated," "straddling," "coalesce," "strong armed," "countermand," "bypass." Even in policy writing the verbs are physical. When he writes "Agents will coalesce to move against the wishes of the President," the verbs do the work; the abstract noun ("Agents") sits still while the verbs push.

## Argument Structure Habits

Edgar opens with the claim. In both memos the first real sentence of the Background or Executive Summary is the thesis. The ROK memo's Background opens with the fact that sets up the whole argument: "After the 2022 invasion of Ukraine, DPRK equipment such as missiles and artillery shells began to be found on the front line of the conflict." The Greenland memo's Executive Summary opens with the recommendation in the first clause. He does not throat-clear. He does not write "This memo will argue." He states the situation and names the move.

His transitions are functional rather than literary. He uses "First," "Second," "Lastly," "Moreover," "However," "Additionally," "Simultaneously," and "Therefor" (consistently misspelled without the final "e") to move between points. He rarely uses conjunctive adverbs for rhythm alone; when a transition appears, it is doing structural work. In personal prose the transitions loosen: "I think to...," "I wonder if...," "I guess...," "I recall...," which are closer to interior monologue than exposition.

Landings are his strongest structural habit. Both memos and both personal pieces end on a sentence that returns to the opening frame. The Greenland memo opens with Principal-Agent theory and closes on "set the stage for long term peaceful occupation," recalling the executive summary's "status quo changing authority." The Rolling Stone piece opens with leaving and closes with erosion: "Until there is little left to kick." The GPS statement opens with life abroad and closes with fresh eyes on the country. He writes toward endings. Agents should not cut or rewrite his closing sentences without strong reason.

## Signature Moves

**Triadic lists with escalating weight.** "As a foreigner, as an outsider, as an American outside of America." "Drone Production... Drone Utilization... Drone Electronic Warfare and Counter Drone Measures." "I've seen our poverty, I've seen our abundance, our anger, and our distrust of the government." The triad (sometimes extending to four) is a go-to rhetorical device in both registers.

**Recurring metaphors carried across the piece.** The rolling stone becomes the sphere becomes the dust. The "microcosm of the world" shows up twice in the GPS statement. When he picks up a figure, he works it to the end. Editors should preserve the through-line; if the metaphor appears twice, do not cut one of the two instances.

**Return-to-frame endings.** See above.

**Verb-heavy policy prose.** Where most graduate writers pile up nominalizations, Edgar more often writes "Agents will coalesce to move against the wishes of the President" than "there will be coalescence among the agents." He still nominalizes more than is ideal (the Greenland memo has "renegotiation," "implementation," "dismantlement," "alignment"), but the instinct is to keep verbs working.

**Named theory anchors, cited in-text.** He reaches for (Kiewet & McCubbins, 1991), (NATO, 2014), (Jeon & Bae, 38 North, 2024), (Golove, 2000) inside the paragraph rather than gesturing vaguely at "the literature." This is correct policy practice and should be preserved.

**Direct quotation of lived conversation in personal prose.** Mr Woods's line about passports; the woman at the East Amsterdam housewarming ("Are you planning on going back home?"). He uses quoted speech as a narrative turning point. The quote carries the argument.

## Anti-Patterns (Confirmed)

The samples confirm the known hard rules and surface a few more.

**No emdashes.** Zero emdashes appear across all four samples. He uses periods, commas, and occasional parentheticals where other writers would reach for the em-dash.

**No "it's not X, it's Y" rhetorical framing.** The device does not appear. He states positive claims directly.

**Minimal semicolons and colons.** Colons appear in memos as section headers (Background:, Context:, Policy Recommendations:) but almost never inside sentences. Semicolons are rare. Periods and commas carry the load.

**No throat-clearing openers.** No "It is important to note," no "In this paper I will argue," no "This memo will address." He starts with the substance.

**No "the author" or third-person dodges in personal writing.** He writes "I" freely when the register calls for it.

## Register-Switching Rules

When Edgar moves from personal to memo voice, four things shift and five things hold.

Shifts: (1) "I" disappears and is replaced by the institutional agent ("the ROK," "the President," "the administration"); (2) sentence length increases, especially in opening sentences; (3) capitalized theoretical terms appear as signals of technical vocabulary; (4) citations appear in-text per APA 7.

Holds: (1) the preference for opening with a circumstance or condition ("With...," "After...," "By..."); (2) the triadic list; (3) concrete verbs of motion and force; (4) short terminal sentences to land paragraphs; (5) return-to-frame endings.

An agent drafting in his memo voice should not over-formalize. The ROK memo reads as clearly Edgar because it still uses compact verbs and direct claims even while wearing the institutional costume. An editor who converts "ROK Armed Forces should request" into "It is recommended that the Republic of Korea Armed Forces consider requesting" has destroyed the voice.

## Specific Word Choices and Go-To Phrases

- "Coalesce" and "coalescence" (used in memo prose where most writers would write "unite" or "align")
- "Burden sharing," "contract design," "delegated authority," "veto subgroups" (treated as familiar, not defined every time after first use)
- "Bring to bear" (Greenland memo: "should be brought to bear")
- "Strong armed" (Greenland memo, unhyphenated)
- "Kinetic action" (policy register, used precisely)
- "Touch base" (personal prose, used idiomatically)
- "Roll," "kicked," "scattered," "percolated" (physical verbs in personal prose)
- "Fresh eyes" (repeated in GPS statement)
- "Microcosm of the world" (repeated, doing structural work)
- "Straddling cultures" (GPS statement)
- "Third Culture Kid" (capitalized, identity term)
- "Therefor" (consistent misspelling of "therefore" — this is a typo, not a feature; editors should fix silently)
- "Lense" instead of "lens" (typo, fix silently)
- Possessive apostrophes often dropped in personal prose ("its" for "it's," "cant" for "can't," "dont" for "don't"); fix silently in memo register, preserve or fix-with-comment in personal prose depending on whether the sample is a final or a draft

## What Reads as Distinctly Edgar vs. Generic Graduate-Policy Prose

Generic graduate-policy prose defaults to passive voice, heavy nominalization, abstract subjects, and hedged claims. Edgar's memos actively fight those defaults. The Greenland memo says "President Trump should reapproach the problem" rather than "a reapproach to the problem should be considered." It says "we should refrain from kinetic action" rather than "kinetic action is inadvisable." The agent stays visible. The recommendation is stated as a command, not a suggestion.

What reads as distinctly Edgar is the combination of institutional subject, concrete verb, and short terminal sentence. "With Multiple veto subgroups the President cannot move freely and must first overcome bureaucratic momentum." That sentence could have been "There are multiple institutional obstacles that would need to be addressed," which would be generic. Edgar's version keeps the agent visible, uses "move freely" as a physical verb, and lands on "bureaucratic momentum" as the emphatic end.

What reads as distinctly Edgar in personal prose is the willingness to hold a single figure through a whole piece, the short declarative fragments, and the interior-monologue transitions ("I wonder," "I guess," "I ask"). The Rolling Stone piece is formally unpolished — typos, missing apostrophes, a run-on or two — but the voice is strong because the rhythm and the metaphor hold.

An editor should leave his voice alone when it is working. The mechanical errors (spelling, apostrophes, comma splices) get fixed silently in formal work and fixed-with-comment in drafts. The rhythmic choices, the fragments, the metaphor carried across the piece, the return-to-frame ending, the triadic list, the capitalized theory terms, the physical verbs, the stated recommendation without hedge — these hold. Touch them only with reason, and flag the reason in a margin comment.
