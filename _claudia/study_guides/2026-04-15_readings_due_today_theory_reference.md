## GPCO 403 - International Economics

**Due / relevant readings:** Feenstra & Taylor, Ch. 12.3, 15.5, 19.1-19.4, aligned with the April 15 topic: external wealth I, stock vs. flow, NIIP/NFA concepts. Local textbook text was not available, so this page is calibrated from the syllabus and Week 3 lecture PDFs.

**Situation.** The United States can run persistent current account deficits and still earn surprisingly strong net foreign income because external wealth depends not only on financial flows, but also on valuation effects, exchange-rate movements, and the composition of foreign assets and liabilities.

**Core intuition.** The reading cluster treats international macro as a stock-flow problem. The **current account** records net saving or borrowing in a period, while **external wealth** or the **net international investment position** records the stock of foreign assets owned by residents minus domestic assets owned by foreigners. A country with current account deficits is generally selling claims on itself to the rest of the world, but the change in its external wealth is not mechanically equal to the current account because asset prices and exchange rates can create capital gains or losses. This is why the United States can look deeply indebted on a net position basis while still benefiting from favorable returns on its foreign assets and low-cost liabilities, the basic logic behind "exorbitant privilege."

**Key concepts and terminology.**
- **Current account (CA)**: Income not spent domestically, closely tied to net lending or borrowing from the rest of the world.
- **Financial account (FA)**: Transactions in financial assets; a positive financial account means foreigners are increasing claims on the home country.
- **External wealth / NFA / NIIP**: The stock value of home-owned foreign assets minus foreign-owned home assets.
- **Valuation effects**: Capital gains and losses on external assets and liabilities caused by asset-price changes, exchange-rate changes, or other revaluations.
- **Stock vs. flow**: The current account is a flow over time; external wealth is the accumulated stock after flows and valuation changes.
- **Consumption smoothing**: Borrowing in bad temporary-income states and saving in good states to stabilize consumption over time.
- **International capital mobility**: Financial capital moving across borders, creating intertemporal trade between present and future consumption.
- **Exorbitant privilege**: The U.S. advantage from borrowing cheaply in dollar liabilities while earning higher returns on foreign assets.

**Assumptions.**
- Financial claims are measurable and can be valued consistently across borders.
- Countries can borrow and lend internationally, subject to solvency constraints.
- Temporary income shocks are distinguishable from permanent shocks, since only temporary shocks justify smoothing rather than permanent adjustment.
- Asset returns, exchange rates, and capital gains can materially alter a country's wealth position beyond trade flows.

**Strengths.**
- Separates current account flows from wealth stocks, which prevents the common mistake of reading deficits as one-period losses.
- Explains why valuation effects can change external wealth even when trade balances do not move much.
- Gives a clean framework for interpreting U.S. external balances, safe-asset demand, and financial globalization.
- Connects macro accounting to policy questions about borrowing, crises, and sustainability.

**Weaknesses / watchouts.**
- Accounting identities do not by themselves explain whether deficits are good, bad, sustainable, or crisis-prone.
- Valuation effects can make short-run external wealth changes look benign while underlying borrowing risks accumulate.
- Consumption smoothing only works well for temporary shocks; borrowing after a permanent income decline can worsen solvency problems.
- The framework can understate political and institutional risks, including sudden stops, default risk, and currency mismatch.

## GPCO 410 - International Politics & Security

**Due readings:** Robert Powell, "War as a Commitment Problem"; Kevin Woods, James Lacey, and Williamson Murray, "Saddam's Delusions: The View From the Inside."

**Situation.** The 2003 Iraq War is the hard case for bargaining theory: if war was costly and Saddam Hussein's regime could not survive a U.S. invasion, why could Washington and Baghdad not bargain over inspections, sanctions, disarmament, or exile before the shooting started?

**Core intuition.** Powell argues that many wars are not mainly failures to share information, but failures to make credible promises over time. When today's bargain changes tomorrow's power balance, the side that gains future leverage cannot credibly promise restraint, and the side that fears the shift may prefer war now. The Iraq case adds Woods, Lacey, and Murray's internal evidence: Saddam's regime generated information pathologies that distorted both sides of the bargain. Saddam overestimated Russian and French protection, expected U.S. casualty aversion, preserved WMD ambiguity to deter Iran and domestic rivals, and received poor military reporting from subordinates trained to please him. Together, the readings show how **commitment failure** and **authoritarian information distortion** can reinforce each other.

**Key concepts and terminology.**
- **Commitment problem**: A bargain exists today, but one or both sides cannot credibly promise to honor it tomorrow.
- **Preventive war**: A declining or threatened actor fights now to avoid facing a stronger adversary later.
- **Power-shifting issue**: A disputed object, such as territory, weapons capability, or sanctions relief, that changes future bargaining power.
- **Rapid power shift**: Powell's key condition; gradual shifts can be renegotiated, but sharp shifts can collapse the bargaining range.
- **First-strike / preemptive logic**: If waiting creates vulnerability, actors may attack before the other side can move first.
- **WMD ambiguity**: Saddam's strategy of implying capability to deter Iran and internal enemies while trying to avoid U.S. invasion.
- **Coup-proofing**: Regime-protection behavior that weakens military effectiveness and corrupts internal information flows.
- **Information pathology**: Systematic distortion inside a regime, where subordinates report what leaders reward rather than what is true.

**Assumptions.**
- States are forward-looking and care about future bargaining positions.
- No external enforcer can guarantee interstate compliance under anarchy.
- The disputed policy or capability materially affects future power.
- Authoritarian leaders may prioritize regime survival over national welfare, and internal reporting can be strategically distorted.

**Strengths.**
- Powell explains why complete information may still produce war: the issue is credibility, not ignorance.
- The Iraq reading adds primary-source grounding from captured documents and interviews, making the bargaining failure concrete.
- Together, the readings fit the class memo question on whether a Bush-Saddam settlement was possible before the 2003 war.
- The combined framework is portable to nuclear programs, security guarantees, and rising-power disputes.

**Weaknesses / watchouts.**
- Powell can overpredict preventive war; many large power shifts do not produce attacks.
- Commitment and information problems are hard to separate empirically because most real crises involve both.
- The Saddam article is a rich case study, but the evidence is one regime and partially filtered through postwar interrogation.
- "No possible settlement" should not be asserted too quickly; the exam move is to specify which commitments could not be made credible and why.

## GPPS 444 - History of Warfare

**Due reading:** *The Cambridge History of Warfare*, Part Two, Ch. 5, "New Weapons, New Tactics" by Christopher Allmand. The syllabus also mentions an additional horse/stirrup reading to be placed in Canvas, but no separate local file was found.

**Situation.** Agincourt works because it punctures the romantic image of medieval warfare as mounted knights deciding battles by shock combat; English archers, dismounted men-at-arms, terrain, mud, and tactical integration mattered more than chivalric cavalry mythology.

**Core intuition.** Allmand's chapter argues that late medieval warfare was transformed by infantry, missile weapons, tactical adaptation, and changing military labor systems. The mounted knight remained socially prestigious, but the battlefield was increasingly shaped by cheaper and more disciplined foot forces: longbowmen, crossbowmen, pikemen, halberdiers, and dismounted men-at-arms. Battles such as Courtrai, Bannockburn, Crecy, Poitiers, and Agincourt showed that unsupported cavalry could be destroyed when it attacked prepared infantry and massed missiles. Yet the chapter does not say cavalry disappeared. The key shift is combined arms: cavalry, infantry, archers, fortification, siegecraft, and later artillery had to be integrated under conditions where war was also becoming more expensive, professionalized, and politically tied to state formation.

**Key concepts and terminology.**
- **Myth of the mounted knight**: The literary and romantic overstatement of cavalry's battlefield dominance.
- **Longbow**: A cheap, high-rate missile weapon that could be deployed en masse and made cavalry attacks costly.
- **Crossbow**: Slower than the longbow but powerful and threatening to mounted warriors and horses.
- **Dismounted men-at-arms**: Elite fighters adapting by fighting on foot alongside archers rather than relying only on mounted shock.
- **Pike / spear / halberd infantry**: Relatively inexpensive weapons that let townsmen and common soldiers resist cavalry.
- **Agincourt**: The 1415 battle where Henry V's smaller English army used defensive positioning, mud, archery, and dismounted fighting against a larger French force.
- **Chevauchee**: Destructive mounted raiding used to devastate territory, force battle, or create political pressure.
- **Siege warfare**: Still central; battlefield victories were dramatic, but control of castles and towns remained strategically decisive.

**Assumptions.**
- Tactical effectiveness depends on terrain, cohesion, discipline, and combined arms, not just weapons.
- Social prestige can lag behind military reality; nobles may remain culturally central even when battlefield utility shifts.
- Cheap weapons can be strategically disruptive when paired with training and organization.
- Political and economic change, including paid armies and urban militias, shapes battlefield organization.

**Strengths.**
- Corrects the over-simple "knights dominated medieval warfare" story with evidence of infantry and missile effectiveness.
- Fits Thomas's recurring variables: mass, mobility, adaptability, supply/logistics, and operational leadership.
- Agincourt gives a clean case for terrain plus tactics beating numerical superiority.
- The chapter links technology to social structure, showing why weapons change who can fight and who has political leverage.

**Weaknesses / watchouts.**
- The longbow story can be overlearned; English success depended on terrain, preparation, and French choices, not the weapon alone.
- The chapter's anti-knight corrective can make cavalry seem irrelevant, even though mounted forces remained useful for scouting, raiding, pursuit, and shock under the right conditions.
- Battle narratives can obscure the larger strategic role of sieges, finance, and occupation.
- Agincourt is memorable but not necessarily representative of all late medieval warfare.

## GPPS 463 - Politics of Southeast Asia

**Due reading:** Richard Stubbs, "War and Economic Development: Export-Oriented Industrialization in East and Southeast Asia."

**Situation.** Stubbs asks why Japan, South Korea, Taiwan, Hong Kong, Singapore, Malaysia, and Thailand moved into rapid export-oriented industrialization while many other developing countries did not, and he argues that WWII, Cold War insurgencies, the Korean War, and the Vietnam War shaped the state capacity, capital flows, and markets that made the Asian export miracle possible.

**Core intuition.** Stubbs does not reject market or statist explanations of East and Southeast Asian development. He argues they are incomplete unless placed inside the region's geostrategic history. War had **destructive effects** where fighting occurred, but it also had **formative** and **redistributive** effects for states near, preparing for, or benefiting from the conflicts. The sequence of wars weakened old social orders, empowered central bureaucracies and coercive institutions, injected U.S. aid and procurement spending, opened export markets, and habituated state intervention in economic life. These conditions made export-oriented industrialization more politically feasible and materially supported than it would have been in peacetime.

**Key concepts and terminology.**
- **Export-oriented industrialization (EOI)**: Growth strategy based on manufacturing for foreign markets rather than only replacing imports.
- **Import-substitution industrialization (ISI)**: Earlier strategy focused on producing domestically what had been imported.
- **Destructive effects of war**: Death, displacement, infrastructure damage, shortages, social dislocation, and state breakdown.
- **Formative / developmental effects**: Centralized authority, expanded bureaucracy, broader revenue systems, technology, and mobilization.
- **Redistributive / reformative effects**: Aid, war spending, new trade patterns, skill formation, and reforms made possible by crisis.
- **Strong state / weak society**: The wartime and Cold War condition in which governments could override social opposition to export policies.
- **Cold War capital injection**: U.S. military and economic aid, procurement, and later Japanese investment supporting regional industrialization.
- **Geostrategic context**: The security environment that shaped economic policy opportunities and constraints.

**Assumptions.**
- War and preparation for war can build state capacity, not only destroy it.
- State capacity is necessary for successful EOI because policy shifts require coordination, discipline, and insulation from social opposition.
- U.S. strategic spending and market access were unusually important to East and Southeast Asia's growth path.
- The timing of a state's EOI shift affects how much it benefits from the war sequence.

**Strengths.**
- Adds a missing security variable to standard explanations of the Asian miracle.
- Explains why EOI was politically possible in some East and Southeast Asian states but much harder elsewhere.
- Connects economic development to state formation, war-making, aid, procurement, and market creation.
- Gives a strong seminar link to the Philippines exception: similar war exposure did not automatically produce the same strong-state developmental path.

**Weaknesses / watchouts.**
- The mechanism can blur different countries together even though Japan, South Korea, Taiwan, Singapore, Malaysia, and Thailand followed different paths.
- War may be necessary context but not sufficient explanation; institutions, leadership, class coalitions, and global markets still matter.
- The argument risks survivorship bias by focusing on successful economies that benefited from conflict while underplaying places where war mainly destroyed capacity.
- The Philippines is a useful limit case: if similar geostrategic pressures did not generate the same outcome, inherited institutions and domestic elite power may be doing more causal work.
