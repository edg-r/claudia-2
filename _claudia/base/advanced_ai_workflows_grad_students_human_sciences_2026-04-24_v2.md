# The Terminal Has Moved Into the Seminar Room

*Advanced AI Practice Among Graduate Students in the Human Sciences, April 2026*

## Thesis

Advanced graduate-student practice with AI in the human sciences is consolidating around a single architecture: a terminal-resident orchestrator that drives named subagents against a version-controlled Markdown memory layer, with retrieval grounded in a personal corpus and with the human acting as editor, verifier, and voice. Within eighteen months this architecture will be the default for serious students in law, history, qualitative social science, applied quantitative methods, and policy, and the browser-based single-prompt workflows that dominated 2023 and 2024 will look like the early word-processor era looks to anyone who once ran WordPerfect on DOS. What is emerging is not better chat. It is an apprenticeship in software discipline grafted onto humanistic inquiry, and it is being built by students and a thin layer of sympathetic faculty faster than their institutions can write policy for it.

Three forces are driving the consolidation: model-layer changes (long context, stable agent SDKs, cheap local embedding and generation) that make multi-step orchestration reliable enough to trust with real work; a policy vacuum at professional and graduate schools that has left students free to design their own norms; and a set of qualitative and archival research problems whose scale finally makes the investment in orchestration pay off. The paper that follows traces what is actually happening discipline by discipline, explains why the trajectory is stable rather than hype-driven, identifies the patterns already fading, and closes with concrete additions, subtractions, and changes Edgar should make to his own stack in the next one to three months. The argument throughout is that the center of gravity is no longer the model. It is the pipeline around the model, and the students building those pipelines are operating in a craft that has not been named yet.

## The Landscape, Interpreted

The public narrative about graduate students and AI is still dominated by the hand-wringing genre: students are cheating, faculty are panicking, universities are scrambling for policy. That framing describes the median case and misses the frontier entirely. The frontier is not in the chat window. It is in `~/.claude/` and `~/.codex/` and in the `.cursorrules` files checked into a student's private GitHub. It lives in Markdown files with names like `AGENT_CONTEXT.md` and `TASK_LOG.md`, in SQLite databases that track readings and assignments alongside embeddings, in hook scripts that fire when a file is written, and in the growing habit of students writing SOPs for their own agents the way software teams write runbooks. This is a different activity than chatting with ChatGPT. It is closer to running a small research lab where most of the labor is done by delegated processes the student designed.

The clearest public artifact of this shift is the appearance of academic orchestrator repositories on GitHub over the last twelve months, and the pace has accelerated in the spring of 2026 to the point where one can now count named authors and tracked forks rather than anonymous templates. `letitbk/claude-academic-setup` ships twenty-two skills, thirteen plugins, and a Plan-Mode-first posture aimed explicitly at social-science researchers working across R, Stata, and Python. `Imbad0202/academic-research-skills` decomposes paper writing into a research-draft-review-revise-finalize pipeline with a named agent per stage and is actively versioned, with `academic-paper` at v2.9 and `academic-pipeline` at v3.1 as of spring 2026. `wanshuiyin/ARIS` is framework-agnostic Markdown-only and designed to run overnight, with cross-model review loops that let one agent critique another's work while the author sleeps. `wshobson/agents` is the generic multi-agent template from which many of these setups borrow. More consequential than any of these, for the human-sciences argument, are the named-author academic stacks that have emerged alongside them. Pedro Sant'Anna's `pedrohcgs/claude-code-my-workflow`, extracted from his Econ 730 course at Emory, ships twenty-eight skills, fourteen specialized agents, twenty-four rules, adversarial QA, and replication protocols; the README reports fifteen-plus research groups across economics, energy, political science, and engineering that had forked and adapted it by March 2026, which is the first documented cohort of academic Claude Code adoption outside anecdote. Paul Goldsmith-Pinkham's Markus Academy onboarding guide is the companion text. `Galaxy-Dawn/claude-scholar`, built by Gaorui Zhang, sits at 3.4k GitHub stars and 317 forks and ships a literature-reviewer agent, a paper-miner agent that extracts writing patterns from strong papers, a rebuttal-writer agent, and a review-response skill, all wired into Zotero and Obsidian. `flonat/claude-research`, built by a PhD student, ships forty-six slash-command skills (`/proofread`, `/latex-autofix`, `/literature`, `/bib-validate`), six adversarial review agents (domain-reviewer, paper-critic, referee2-reviewer, peer-reviewer, proposal-reviewer, fixer), and nine safety hooks (block-destructive-git, context-monitor, protect-source-files). These are not vendor products. They are personal systems published for other researchers to fork, they have authors, and the authors are in some cases tracking diffusion themselves. The fact that they converge on a five-element architecture (named subagents rather than generic tool calls, Markdown memory under git, Plan-Mode or review-gate enforcement before expensive operations, adversarial review agents that grade the primary agent's output, and explicit disclosure plus safety hooks) indicates that a shared practice has formed without any vendor or institution shaping it. Claudia sits inside this cohort, not outside it.

In law the pattern is furthest along because the cost of being wrong is legible. The Stanford HAI study by Magesh and colleagues on legal RAG reliability, which documented seventeen to thirty-three percent hallucination rates across Lexis+ Protégé and Westlaw AI-Assisted Research, has become the canonical reference for why JD students no longer trust vendor-wrapped legal AI unsupervised. The response has been a migration away from vendor tools toward Obsidian-plus-RAG pipelines where the student owns the corpus and the query layer. UC Davis's Mabie Law Library maintains one of the more practical student-facing guides to this migration. Clinic work, where client data cannot leave the building, is increasingly routed through local models running on Ollama with Llama 3.2 or Qwen 2.5 as the generator. The mature pattern is consistent across the top JD programs I could find documentation for: outline in Obsidian, query with a local or Claude-Projects-based agent, and always verify primary authority against the cite. The tool layer changes; the verification discipline does not.

History is the most developed humanities discipline for advanced AI work, and its trajectory is the cleanest evidence that the architecture I described above is not a temporary fashion. The Humphries team's benchmark paper on handwritten historical manuscripts, "Unlocking the Archives," reported character-level accuracy of ninety-three to ninety-four percent on handwritten documents after LLM post-correction, a number that was simply not available eighteen months earlier. Boros and colleagues' follow-up on multimodal LLMs for OCR, OCR post-correction, and named-entity recognition describes an end-to-end pipeline that a single PhD student can now run over a forty-five-thousand-page corpus. The Transcription Pearl project is the GUI for students who prefer not to write the pipeline themselves. The bottleneck in historical work has shifted from transcription to validation, which is exactly the shift the broader architecture predicts: when model-layer capability rises, labor concentrates at the boundaries where the human signs their name. The American Historical Association's 2025 *Guiding Principles for Artificial Intelligence in History Education*, the first such statement from a major US humanities learned society, is the disciplinary ratification of this pattern. It endorses AI for identifying key points before reading, for starter bibliographies when each reference is verified, for footnote structure fixes, and for idea generation. It forbids submitting AI-generated essays as one's own, using summaries without reading sources, unchecked references, and unengaged literature reviews. Its governing principle, "expertise must precede AI reliance," is the cleanest single-sentence articulation of the verification discipline the broader architecture encodes. The NLP4DH, DHSI, Digital Orientalist, and MSU Global DH Symposium programs for 2025 and 2026 are where the methodological work is being presented.

Qualitative social science has been slower to consolidate but the protocols are now stable enough to teach, and the empirical evidence shifted decisively in late 2025. Shen, Wu, Patt, and Mendoza-Denton's 2025 GATOS paper in Sage's International Journal of Qualitative Methods is the reference protocol for LLM-assisted thematic coding. It is explicitly a protocol paper, not a hype piece, and it documents failure modes (sarcasm, humor, marginalized-group speech) alongside the cases where LLM coding achieves inter-rater reliability with human coders. Wang and colleagues' LATA pilot at CSCW 2025 is the second entry in what is becoming a canon. The more recent DeTAILS system (Wang et al., arXiv:2510.17575) is the first protocol to ship measured time-to-completion numbers: participants completed thematic coding on test datasets in roughly thirty-three minutes against a seven-to-eight-hour human baseline, with interactive feedback loops designed to preserve analytic agency. Hayes's 2025 piece in Sage, "Conversing With Qualitative Data," provides the cleanest framing of LLMs as conversational partners rather than autonomous coders. The Nature Humanities and Social Sciences Communications 2026 paper on open-source generative-AI-plus-ML inductive codebooks and the Wong et al. SSRN paper on focus-group transcripts round out a genuine methodological canon. Jiang, Ko-Wong, and Valdovinos Gutierrez's 2025 paper in Educational Researcher on AI for qualitative analysis in equity-focused research is the critique the field needed, and it is now cited as required reading before any GATOS or LATA deployment on data from marginalized populations. The significant move in qualitative work over the last year is that LLM-assisted coding has become a method one writes up in the methods section, with an IRB-audited protocol, rather than a shortcut one hides, and the measurement vocabulary now exists to debate what good looks like. This is the shape of a practice that is consolidating rather than fading.

Applied quantitative methods programs, where Edgar's GPEC 446 sits, are consolidating around a Stata-plus-Quarto-plus-Cursor pipeline with Claude Code as the orchestrator, and this is the discipline where the named-author infrastructure argument is most directly visible. Pedro Sant'Anna's Emory Econ 730 Claude Code workflow, already described above, produced six complete PhD lectures with over eight hundred slides, full Quarto companions with interactive Plotly charts, and R replication packages, all built over six Claude Code sessions and released as a forkable template. The `hanlulong/stata-mcp` and `tmonk/stata-workbench` MCP servers are the two active implementations that expose Stata to an agent. Andrew Heiss's stata-quarto gist circulates widely among students, and Bernhard Bieri's literate-programming-in-Stata post is the clearest walkthrough of the Quarto-Stata-AI pipeline end to end. The R equivalent (R plus Claude Code plus Quarto) is mature and slightly older. What is notable about both pipelines is that they are not "use AI to write Stata code." They are environments in which the student, the model, and the statistical engine each do a well-defined job, with the model's output gated by Plan Mode and the human's output gated by Quarto's reproducibility contract. The stargazer-to-OOXML workaround for Word-delivered regression tables, which Edgar documented in his April 20 workflow, is a concrete example of the kind of friction-point fix that only shows up once the rest of the pipeline is working. The Anthropic desktop agent Cowork, which went generally available in January 2026, is beginning to appear alongside Claude Code in these pipelines: Sant'Anna's published convention is to use Claude Code for "anything that compiles, renders, or versions" and Cowork for the surrounding knowledge work (literature organization, data preparation, document conversion, scheduled tasks). This division of labor is new and will spread.

International relations and security studies are the exception that proves the rule. The public documentation is thin. SAIS, Fletcher, GPS, and the UK equivalents are essentially silent about advanced student practice, even as their subject-matter output on AI policy is voluminous. The methods literature that does exist is strong: Le Mens and colleagues' 2024 paper in Political Analysis on positioning political texts with LLMs by asking and averaging is the reference protocol for LLM-based positional coding, and Halterman and Keith's 2025 Codebook LLMs paper evaluates LLMs as measurement tools for political-science concepts at a level of rigor that the qualitative-coding side is still catching up to. The Political-LLM project at political-llm.org aggregates these methods. But the gap between available method and documented student practice is wide, which is itself a finding: in IR and security, students running sophisticated pipelines are doing so in private. The few public artifacts I can identify (theory-reference PDFs built from Fearon, Powell, Walter, and Bueno de Mesquita, embedded reading corpora, multi-agent orchestrators for memo drafting) are idiomatic within the broader pattern but invisible unless the student chooses to publish them. Claudia is one such instance. There are others, but they are not on GitHub.

Policy schools (HKS, Harris, SIPA, LKY, Hertie, SAIS, GPS) are the largest undocumented zone, and the new international evidence does not close the gap so much as sharpen its shape. These programs publish extensively about AI as a subject of policy and almost nothing about AI as a medium of student work. The reasons are structural. Policy schools value writing as the finished artifact of professional identity and are institutionally cautious about anything that touches the prose layer. They also lack the coding culture of CS departments or the bench-method culture of qualitative social science, which means there is no existing practitioner layer for AI workflows to attach to. Sciences Po's School of Public Affairs offers a Digital, New Technology and Public Policy master's stream and hosts AI master classes, but the workflow layer remains undocumented publicly. Hertie School has published explicit AI-use guidelines and runs a Master of Data Science for Public Policy with named AI research strands, but again the student-workflow artefacts are absent; Shruti Kakade's MDS-graduate work at Data for Policy 2025 is the closest named example and it is presentation-level, not workflow-level. HKS's Artificial Intelligence at HKS initiative and HGSE's 2025–2026 AI policy (allow-with-disclosure, forbid-assignment-generation, specific permissible uses itemized) are the more visible US instances. The workflows that do exist (memo pipelines with house-style enforcement, stakeholder-simulation exercises, policy-brief voice profiles) are plausible and I found scattered evidence that students are building them, but the documentation is thinner than in any other discipline reviewed here. The publication gap between "policy schools publish on AI" and "policy schools publish on how their students use AI" is wide and internationally consistent. This is a near-term opportunity for whoever publishes first, and Claudia is one of the stronger candidate artefacts.

Anthropology and ethnography are the disciplines where advanced AI work is most constrained, and legitimately so. The Jiang paper's equity critique cuts hardest in fieldwork that depends on irony, silence, and culturally specific registers that LLMs coarsen or misread. What exists in these disciplines tends to be defensive (Ollama-plus-local-model pipelines for field-note storage, with hard commitments to never send transcripts to cloud APIs) rather than expansive. This is a disciplined response, not a backwardness, and it models one possible future for every discipline that handles data with a high human-meaning component.

The individual-student layer, below the faculty-built infrastructure, is now visible on Medium and personal blogs in a way it was not a year ago. Mart Kempenaar, an MSc student at Groningen working on AI and societal impact, publishes a Claude-Code-plus-Obsidian weekly-reflection system in which a CLAUDE.md configuration file grounds Claude in his vault, custom skills teach it his note structure, and an `inbox-cleanup` skill categorizes and archives based on content importance. Sonny Huynh, Yu-Xi Lim (whose April 2026 Medium post is titled "Making Claude My Second Brain's Second Brain"), Len (`@lennart.dde`), and the author writing as `spektrl` publish structurally similar stacks. Emile van Krieken's PhD Obsidian workflow is another such artifact. These are not toy demos. They are the five-element architecture described earlier scaled down to a single student's research environment, and they indicate that the pattern ported from faculty-built templates into individual practice during the summer of 2025 and has been accumulating primary-voice documentation ever since. Reddit discussion on r/ClaudeCode, r/ClaudeAI, and r/ChatGPTCoding runs alongside this Medium layer and serves as the live evaluative surface where students compare notes on specific skills, hooks, and failure modes. The practitioner ecosystem now has a visible bench.

Two non-disciplinary patterns run across all of the above. The first is the movement of writing from AI-as-drafter to AI-as-analyzer. The mature practice across Kim Klassen's voice-preservation work, the Transmitter's "from bench to bot" column, Thesify's graduate-writing material, and the Indisputably blog converges on the same five-move protocol: draft first in your own voice, use the model as analyzer (reverse outlining, devil's advocacy, citation verification) rather than as writer, feed samples of your own strong prose when rewriting is necessary, document what the model touched, and disclose appropriately. The protocol is now stable enough across sources that it reads like received wisdom, even though it was barely visible two years ago. The second cross-disciplinary pattern is governance. Harvard FAS's 2024 policy treats undisclosed AI-assisted writing as plagiarism while deferring citation form to instructors. Stanford GSB allows AI on take-home work with disclosure and bans it on in-class assessments. LSE's Eden Centre publishes a school position and defers to courses. Oxford's Bodleian guide requires declaration when AI is explicitly permitted. No top program ships a mandatory template. The practitioner response has been to write one's own disclosure block and apply it consistently across submissions, a move that is now visible in a handful of published student workflows and almost certainly operating invisibly in many more.

## Why This Direction

The consolidation is not a fashion cycle. Three distinct forces are pulling advanced student practice toward the architecture I have described, and each is durable enough on its own that removing any one would still leave the pattern intact.

The first force is at the tool layer. Model Context Protocol, which Anthropic released in late 2024 and which has since been adopted by OpenAI and Google, is the reason orchestration is reliable enough to bet a semester on. Before MCP, integrating a Zotero library or a Stata process with an agent required bespoke glue code that broke whenever the vendor changed an API. With MCP, the glue is standardized and the ecosystem has filled in around it. `54yyyu/zotero-mcp` and `cookjohn/zotero-mcp` expose a Zotero library to any MCP-compatible agent. `taylorwilsdon/google_workspace_mcp` and `a-bonus/google-docs-mcp` expose Google Workspace. The Stata MCP pair does Stata. The Notion MCP servers, official and ContexaAI's, cover knowledge bases. This is the kind of plumbing that looks boring but changes what is possible: a student can now build a research environment in which their citation manager, their statistical engine, their note system, and their cloud documents all speak to the same agent without the student writing any integration code. The same period has seen stable agent SDKs appear. Anthropic's Claude Agent SDK, OpenAI's agent framework, and the open-source alternatives (Aider, OpenClaw) have converged on a common mental model: a loop with tool calls, memory, and hooks. Long context has moved from an exotic feature to a standard tier. Claude Opus 4.7 at one million input tokens, Gemini 3.1 Pro at similar scale, and GPT-5.5 Pro at the same window make single-shot synthesis across fifty to one hundred papers possible without RAG at all. The pricing cliff at two hundred thousand input tokens is real and changes how practitioners route calls, but the capability exists. Local embedding and generation via Ollama, with `nomic-embed-text` as the CPU-friendly default and `mxbai-embed-large` as the quality option, have made privacy-respecting pipelines cheap enough that students can run them on a laptop without thinking about infrastructure. Each of these developments is small. Together they are the reason a graduate student in April 2026 can build a system that a CS PhD in April 2023 could not have bought.

The second force is institutional. The policy vacuum at professional and graduate schools is not a bug that universities will fix. It is a stable equilibrium that rewards students who build their own norms. Harvard's FAS policy, Stanford GSB's stance, LSE's Eden Centre framework, and Oxford's Bodleian guide all converge on a thin scaffold that defers to instructors and expects disclosure in a form to be determined. Faculty at the level of assigning coursework are overwhelmingly silent or inconsistent. Some sections of the same course forbid AI use; others allow it with citation; others require it. This produces two effects. First, any student who wants to use AI seriously must design their own disclosure, attribution, and verification workflow because no one will hand them one. Second, the faculty who are most sympathetic to advanced use cases are the ones most willing to let students drive the norm-setting, which means the students with the most developed practice end up operating with more permission than the policy document technically grants. The few programs where faculty are themselves running orchestrators (scattered examples in political methodology, digital humanities, and certain applied-stats courses) produce students who bring that practice with them into the rest of the program. The vacuum is self-perpetuating because filling it would require a level of methodological coordination that graduate faculties are institutionally bad at. Students notice, and they act accordingly.

The third force is method-level. The research problems the human sciences care about are bumping against scale in a way that invites orchestration specifically. A comparative politics dissertation that wants to code ten thousand Southeast Asian newspaper articles across four languages simply cannot be done by a human in a reasonable time, and the GATOS and LATA protocols now give a principled way to distribute that coding between a student and a model with documented reliability. A history dissertation on a twenty-thousand-page archival corpus was a luxury reserved for students at institutions with massive archival support staff, and the OCR-plus-LLM pipeline has collapsed the staff requirement into a laptop. A legal clinic that needs to review a thousand case files for a motion to compel can now do that work with a local model while keeping client data off the cloud. These are not efficiency gains. They are capability gains that change what a single graduate student can reasonably attempt. The architecture I described earlier (orchestrator plus subagents plus Markdown memory plus local embeddings plus careful disclosure) is the minimum viable structure for running these projects without losing the thread. The students who are building these systems are not doing so because they read a paper about agents. They are doing so because their dissertations require it.

The interaction of the three forces is what makes the trajectory stable. Tool-layer changes lower the cost of sophisticated orchestration. Institutional silence raises the cost of not owning your own norms. Method-level pressure makes the investment pay off on real work. None of the three will reverse in the eighteen-month horizon this paper covers.

## What Is Fading

Four patterns that looked like the future two years ago are losing ground, and it is worth naming them specifically because some of them still attract a lot of vendor marketing.

Single-prompt chat is fading as a serious research tool. ChatGPT and Claude.ai in the browser remain useful for Tier 0 and Tier 1 work (quick lookups, ad-hoc summarization, low-stakes drafting) and will not disappear, but serious work has moved to the terminal. The gap is now large enough that recommending browser chat to a graduate student for a dissertation-level task is a marker of being outside the practice rather than inside it. The practical problem with the browser is not the model; it is the lack of memory continuity, the inability to hook into external tools reliably, and the fact that the chat is not under version control. Students who tried to build long-term projects inside Custom GPTs or Claude Projects two years ago have mostly migrated out.

Vendor-wrapped legal research AI is fading for the reasons the Stanford HAI study made concrete. Lexis+ Protégé and Westlaw AI-Assisted Research still have institutional contracts that keep them alive, but the center of gravity for serious legal research has moved to student-owned pipelines. This will continue until the vendors can credibly demonstrate hallucination rates below five percent on primary-authority queries, which is not a near-term event.

Vector databases as the primary memory substrate for orchestration are fading. Qdrant, Chroma, and Weaviate are still widely used inside RAG pipelines, but they have lost the position they briefly held as the default memory layer for agent systems. Markdown under git plus SQLite for structured metadata plus an embedding layer that is read from and regenerated rather than treated as a source of truth is the stable pattern. The reason is that vector databases degrade the human-readability of memory, and graduate-student memory is something students want to read, edit, and carry across sessions. The Claudia-style architecture (Markdown files as memory, SQLite for structured queries, embeddings as a search index rebuilt from the Markdown) is idiomatic within the current practice and is unlikely to be displaced.

Agent-based deep-research vendor tools (Elicit Deep Search, Undermind, Consensus Deep Search, AI2 Paperfinder, SciSpace) are not fading exactly, but their position in the workflow is narrowing. Aaron Tay's "Deep Research, Shallow Agency" post is the sharpest published critique: their agency is bounded by pre-programmed workflows and they fail on off-track queries. The practitioner pattern that has stabilized is to use these tools as first-pass discovery and hand the synthesis off to a general-purpose agent that the student controls. This is not where the vendors were trying to land two years ago, and the positioning gap matters.

## The Invisible Menu Problem

One finding deserves a section of its own because it has implications for every stack built on the architecture I have described. Aaron Tay's more recent Substack post, "From Fixed Search Workflows to Agentic Academic Search: Promise, Progress, and the Invisible Menu Problem," names the central unsolved issue in agentic research. When an orchestrator can theoretically do anything, the user cannot discover what it can actually do without reading the README, and most users will not. Tay documents three concrete instances: Elicit Research Agents, where citations only return if the user explicitly says "search the web"; Undermind Projects, where hidden capabilities like bypassing clarification questions via the Generalist agent only emerge through conversation with the developer; SciSpace Agents, where more than a hundred and fifty apps produce a middle-ground between guided platforms and full DIY setups and leave the user uncertain which tool to invoke when. The failure mode compounds reproducibility concerns, because a query that worked last Tuesday may not be the query the agent runs next Tuesday, and the user does not know which part of the menu was taken.

The practitioner response that is stabilizing, and that is observable across the Section 3 infrastructure cohort, is to treat the menu itself as a first-class artifact. Sant'Anna's `workflow-guide.html` catalogs his twenty-eight skills and fourteen agents by name, purpose, and expected input. `flonat/claude-research` publishes its six-agent taxonomy and nine hooks in the repository README. `Imbad0202/academic-research-skills` versions each skill individually. Claudia's agent roster table in CLAUDE.md, the SOPs in `_claudia/sop/`, and the machine-readable manifest in `_claudia/system/manifest.json` are the same move: write the menu down, keep it under version control, teach the agent where to find it. The stacks that are failing are the ones running unscaffolded Claude Projects or NotebookLM notebooks where the user has no named surface to reason about. This is a more durable architectural principle than any specific tool choice. An orchestrator without a visible menu is an orchestrator that will degrade as it grows.

## Implementation Points for Edgar

Given the trajectory described above and Edgar's current stack (Claude Code with Claudia, named course agents, persistent Markdown memory, `claudia.db` SQLite, Ollama with `nomic-embed-text`, `ocrmypdf` plus Tesseract, skills, hooks, cron dispatches, Gmail and Calendar MCP, voice profiles, AI-disclosure SOP), the following moves are the ones that match where the landscape is going over the next one to three months. They are ordered by expected impact, not by implementation difficulty.

The first move is to add Zotero MCP to the stack. This is the single largest gap between Claudia and the direction the landscape is going. Every advanced human-sciences practitioner I could find has their citation manager wired into their agent, and Edgar does not. Install `54yyyu/zotero-mcp` (external server, more flexible) or `cookjohn/zotero-mcp` (plugin-native, lower friction) and connect Atlas specifically to it. This removes the most common friction point in research work: moving citations between the reading, the note, the draft, and the bibliography without losing fidelity. Expect two to four hours to install and tune, and immediate payoff on any paper or memo that cites more than a handful of sources.

The second move is to decide on a 1M-context synthesis routing rule and make it explicit in Atlas and in Mnemosyne. Edgar has the capability (Opus 4.7 at 1M) and a memory of the pricing cliff at 200k, but there is no current rule that says "for cross-reading synthesis across more than eight readings, route to 1M and pay the cliff; for everything else, stay under 200k." A written rule in `_claudia/sop/` or in the manifest prevents both the failure mode of doing laborious RAG on a task that should have been a single-shot synthesis and the failure mode of accidentally blowing the budget on a task that did not need the large context.

The third move is to build an overnight ARIS-pattern loop for a single, bounded task and see whether it is worth extending. The candidate task is literature-gap scanning against new arXiv and SSRN submissions for Edgar's five courses, run once a week on Sunday night. ARIS is framework-free and Markdown-only, which means it can sit alongside Claudia without refactoring anything. The point is not to automate research. It is to test whether the overnight pattern is useful at the human-sciences graduate scale, because the landscape will pull in this direction over the next twelve months and having a working prototype now is cheaper than building one in response to a deadline.

The fourth move is to publish a single piece of Claudia's practice. Specifically, the voice-pass-on-AI-source rule that inverts the standard voice-preservation protocol when the source prose is already AI-generated is something I could not find a published equivalent of, and it is concrete enough to be useful to other students. A short blog post or a gist on GitHub that describes the rule, shows a before-and-after, and links to the SOP is a two-hour task with an asymmetric upside: the post becomes a calling card the next time Edgar wants to coordinate with another advanced practitioner, and it forces a clean articulation of a rule that currently lives in feedback files.

The fifth move is to retire the skills and patterns that are being displaced by the stack Edgar has already built. The BLUF reading-summary format has already been retired in favor of theory-reference PDFs; the retirement is documented but the skill file is still in `_claudia/skills/`. If it is truly retired, remove it and add a note to the manifest. Any other skills that no longer fit the current workflow should get the same treatment. Skill hygiene is not glamorous but the alternative is skill sprawl, which is the path by which orchestrators become unmanageable.

The sixth move is to add a Canvas integration when one becomes available, and to watch for it actively. No MCP for Canvas exists yet as of April 2026, but it is the obvious next connector, and the student workflow will collapse substantially when it ships. Set a recurring dispatch in Eos to check the MCP directory monthly for a Canvas server. When one appears, integrate immediately.

The seventh move is to add a failure-mode log to Mnemosyne. The documented literature on failure modes in agentic research is thin (Aaron Tay's shallow-agency piece is the closest reference) and Edgar has accumulated a meaningful set of specific failures over the last month (subagent stream timeouts, PDF pipeline breakage, Gmail scope limits, stargazer-OOXML flattening). A structured failure log, with the proximate cause and the fix, is a durable asset and it is the kind of resource the broader practice will need in the next year. It also sets up the next round of skill authoring: failures repeat, and the ones that repeat are the ones that deserve to become a skill or a hook.

Short action list for the next thirty days:

- Install Zotero MCP and wire to Atlas.
- Write the 1M-versus-200k routing rule as an SOP.
- Stand up one ARIS-pattern overnight task (weekly lit-gap scan).
- Publish the voice-pass-on-AI-source rule as a gist.
- Prune retired skills from `_claudia/skills/`.
- Add a Canvas-MCP watch to Eos's monthly dispatch.
- Start `_claudia/failure_log.md` and backfill the last thirty days.
