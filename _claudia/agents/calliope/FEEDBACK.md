### 2026-04-15 — Recurring prose patterns in Edgar's academic drafts (self-observation from Memo 1 pass)
**Type:** observation
**What:** Three patterns surfaced strongly enough in the first full line edit to be worth tracking across future passes. The first is buried BLUF. Edgar's opening paragraphs tend to scaffold the context before stating the claim, so the thesis lands in sentence five or six rather than sentence one. In Memo 1 the commitment-problem thesis was the sixth sentence; moving it to the opener reduced the paragraph from 168 words to roughly 95 and sharpened the rest of the memo. The second is possessive apostrophe omission on proper nouns, specifically "Husseins" for "Hussein's," which appeared three separate times in Memo 1. This is a mechanical tic rather than a style choice, and it is easy to sweep in a final pass. The third is sentence fragments used as rhetorical flourishes at the end of paragraphs, typically in the form of a noun phrase standing alone ("A classic Powell inefficiency condition where..." or "A cost that Hussein was not willing to entertain"). These land as emphatic gestures but read as errors in graduate policy prose. Rolling them back into complete sentences preserves the rhythm without the grammatical cost.
**Why:** These are cheap wins on every pass. If I screen for them first I can resolve a large fraction of the edit surface before doing deeper sentence surgery.
**Rule going forward:** On any Edgar draft, run three quick screens before the main pass. One, locate the thesis sentence and check whether it sits in the first two sentences of the opening paragraph; if not, flag for BLUF repositioning. Two, grep or eyeball-scan for proper-noun possessives missing apostrophes, especially for repeat figures like Hussein, Bush, Putin, Thaksin, Marcos. Three, scan the last sentence of each paragraph for noun-phrase fragments and flag them for promotion to full sentences.

### 2026-04-15 — "Rather than" versus the banned "not X but Y"
**Type:** observation
**What:** In Memo 1 I replaced the banned construction "not because one didn't exist, but because they could not trust the other" with "rather than from a breakdown in bargaining." "Rather than" is a clean substitute that carries the same contrastive logic without setting up the false-binary performance Edgar has banned. Similarly in the revised thesis I used "A commitment problem, not a failure of information, made the Second Gulf War nearly inevitable" — an appositive negation rather than the setup/reveal structure. Both patterns preserve causal contrast without the rhetorical tic.
**Why:** The ban is on a specific rhetorical move (setup/reveal), not on all contrastive negation. Knowing the approved substitutes speeds every edit where the source uses the banned form.
**Rule going forward:** When cutting "not X but Y" or "it's not X, it's Y," prefer either an appositive negation ("A, not B, did the thing") or a "rather than" rephrase ("the result of A, rather than B"). Both are quiet. Neither performs insight.

### 2026-04-15 — Edgar's thesis structure preference is the two-sentence setup, not the single-sentence BLUF
**Type:** correction (partial reversal)
**What:** My 2026-04-15 BLUF entry said to screen for thesis burial and flag for repositioning into the first two sentences. That is right on burial (the original thesis was the sixth sentence in a 168-word paragraph). It is wrong on structure. The shipped thesis keeps the two-sentence "setup plus instead" move Edgar wrote in the original: "War was nearly inevitable, not because of an informational failure or a misread of capabilities. Instead, an insurmountable commitment problem led to war." Athena proposed compressing this to one 16-word sentence ("War in 2003 was nearly inevitable. The cause was not informational failure but an insurmountable commitment problem."). Edgar rejected that compression and said "keep mine for the most part." He replaced "miscalculation in game theory" with "misread of capabilities" — a substance fix Praether would demand — but kept the two-sentence rhythm and the "Instead," pivot. The lesson is that Edgar's BLUF is two sentences, not one: a negation sentence that clears the wrong answer, followed by an "Instead," sentence that lands the claim.
**Why:** Proposing a single-sentence BLUF collapses a structural rhythm Edgar uses deliberately. The two-sentence setup does argumentative work: it preempts the standard information-failure reading before asserting the commitment-problem reading. Compressing it sacrifices the preemption.
**Rule going forward:** Screen for thesis placement (must be in the first two sentences of the opening paragraph) but preserve two-sentence thesis structures by default. If a thesis is one sentence and works, fine. If a thesis is two sentences with a "not X. Instead, Y." rhythm, keep the rhythm. Only collapse to one sentence when the sentences are genuinely redundant.

### 2026-04-15 — Substance corrections can be inserted into Edgar's sentence structure; they are not license to rewrite the sentence
**Type:** observation
**What:** Athena flagged "two-level game" as the wrong term — Hussein's signal was dual-audience (US vs. Iran, both external), not Putnam's domestic-versus-international two-level game. Athena proposed the rewrite "Hussein's dual signal on WMDs operated as a two-level game" (which she knew was still wrong; this was the "put the correction in section 4" hand-off). Same subject, same verb, same sentence architecture, correct terminology. He did the same on the thesis, where "miscalculation in game theory" became "misread of capabilities" — a substance swap inserted into his existing grammar.
**Why:** This reveals the seam between substance and voice. Edgar accepts the correction at the token level (the specific wrong term gets replaced) but not at the sentence level (the surrounding prose stays his). Rewrites that bundle a substance fix with a voice change get rejected; token-level substance fixes that preserve the sentence around them get accepted.
**Rule going forward:** When a course agent or I flag a substance error, propose the minimum-viable token replacement that keeps Edgar's sentence architecture intact. Do not take the substance fix as license to also tighten the sentence, swap the verb, or reorder the clauses. If the surrounding prose also needs work, raise it as a separate margin comment.

### 2026-04-16 — Drafting discipline: prune questions with self-evident answers
**Type:** correction
**What:** The same email draft included three numbered questions. The third asked whether the AI policy differed across assignment types (Concept Checks, Data Briefs, midterm, final). Edgar cut it: "concept checks are clearly meant to test our understanding, and the exams like mid term and final are a clear no." In-class quizzes and exams are obviously closed to outside assistance unless explicitly stated otherwise, so the question signals lazy thinking and wastes the recipient's time. Focus the ask on genuinely ambiguous territory (written take-home assignments, disclosure format, edge cases).
**Why:** A draft with obvious-answer questions reads as underprepared. The recipient either thinks less of the sender or has to write a longer reply covering points the sender should have resolved on their own. In academic correspondence this costs goodwill on every send.
**Rule going forward:** Before finalizing any drafted question list in correspondence (email, memo, clarification request), audit each item: would a reasonable recipient consider the answer self-evident from context, norms, or the nature of the assignment itself? If yes, cut it. Assume exams and in-class quizzes are closed to AI and outside assistance by default; do not ask. Ask only what is genuinely unclear.
