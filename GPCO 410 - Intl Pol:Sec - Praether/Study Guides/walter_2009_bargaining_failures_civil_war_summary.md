# Title: Bargaining Failures and Civil War (2009) — Walter, Barbara F.
Source/Link: *Annual Review of Political Science* 12: 243-261; doi:10.1146/annurev.polisci.10.101405.135301

## BLUF
Walter argues civil wars persist not because grievances or poverty are unique, but because intrastate disputes are especially vulnerable to three bargaining failures — private information, credible commitment, and indivisible stakes — that prevent otherwise rational settlements. The policy takeaway: durable civil-war peace requires third-party enforcement and power-sharing that neutralizes commitment problems, not just addressing root conditions (pp. 244, 257-258).

## Context & Scope
- Review essay covering bargaining-theoretic literature on civil-war onset, duration, and recurrence; applies Fearon (1995) logic to intrastate cases (pp. 243-245).
- Scope: all civil wars, with emphasis on post-1945 cases; compares to interstate-war bargaining (p. 244).

## Key Evidence / Claims
- Three bargaining failures drive civil war: (1) information asymmetries about rebel financing and government resolve, (2) commitment problems (weak institutions, cemented cleavages, expected power shifts), and (3) indivisible stakes (pp. 245-252).
- Information problems are sharper than in interstate wars because rebel capabilities are hidden and rebels must disarm to settle, making them progressively weaker as implementation proceeds (pp. 245-247).
- Commitment problems cluster in three country types: weak political/legal institutions, highly politicized fixed cleavages, and anticipated power shifts (p. 251).
- Duration and recurrence puzzles: guerrilla wars and multi-faction conflicts reveal information slowly; decisive military victories (15% renewed war) produce more durable peace than negotiated settlements (50% broke down, 1945-1993) (pp. 253-257, Licklider data).
- Third-party peacekeepers significantly raise settlement survival; power-sharing clauses also improve durability (pp. 255-256).

## Implications for Policy/Practice
- Peace mediation should prioritize credible third-party enforcement (peacekeepers, sanctions, guarantees), not just ceasefires — especially where government institutions are weak (pp. 255, 258).
- Expect negotiated settlements to fail absent external verification; design power-sharing to lock in post-treaty balance against future defection (pp. 254, 257).

## Limits / Counterpoints
- Walter concedes her framework is a theory-building exercise: hypotheses need disaggregated data that can distinguish information vs. commitment mechanisms from greed/grievance proxies (p. 258).
