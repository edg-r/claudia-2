## SITUATION

Why did the 1991 Cambodia Paris accords implement successfully under UNTAC while the 1994 Arusha accords in Rwanda collapsed into genocide within months of signing? Walter argues the difference is not the "ripeness" of the conflict or the terms of the deal, but whether combatants had a credible third party willing to protect them through the treacherous demobilization window and a guaranteed share of power in the first postwar government.

---

## CORE INTUITION

Civil war negotiations fail at the **implementation phase**, not the bargaining phase. To end a civil war in a negotiated settlement, combatants must demobilize their separate armies and consolidate military and political authority under a single new central government. This demobilization process creates a short but catastrophic **window of vulnerability**: as groups disarm and cede territory, they become progressively unable to defend themselves or retaliate against cheating. A rival who keeps even a sliver of force in reserve can seize the state and permanently exclude the demobilized side from power.

Because this vulnerability is foreseeable, rational combatants **look down the road** via backward induction and refuse to sign or implement agreements — even when both sides prefer peace to continued war — unless two credible commitment devices are in place:

1. A **third-party security guarantee** (verification if the balance is equal; enforcement if the balance is lopsided) to protect demobilization.
2. A **power-sharing pact** that hard-wires political, military, or territorial power for both sides in the first postwar government, rather than leaving distribution to a winner-take-all election.

The core insight, drawn directly from the credible-commitment literature (Fearon 1995; Williamson 1983 on hostages), is that **the problem is not preferences, it is enforcement**. Self-help strategies (tit-for-tat demobilization, own-side monitoring, costly signals) that work in standard Prisoner's Dilemmas all fail here because civil-war demobilization has a **known last move**, because noise swamps violation detection, and because the payoffs from cheating (hegemony) versus being cheated (annihilation) are existentially asymmetric.

## KEY CONCEPTS, KEYWORDS & TERMINOLOGY

- **Credible commitment problem** — Combatants cannot credibly promise to honor a peace deal because once the opponent demobilizes, the incentive to renege and seize full power is overwhelming. This is the core Fearon-lineage mechanism Walter imports into civil-war settings.
- **Implementation phase** — The third and most dangerous phase in Walter's three-phase model (negotiation → bargaining → implementation). Most scholarship ignores it; Walter argues it is where settlements actually break.
- **Window of vulnerability / demobilization hazard** — The period during which combatants are disarming and ceding territory; they are maximally exposed to surprise attack and permanent political exclusion.
- **Third-party security guarantee** — An outside state or IO that either **verifies** compliance (sufficient when the balance of power is roughly equal) or **enforces** it with credible use of force (needed when one side is markedly stronger or must demobilize further). Verification substitutes for combatants' own unreliable monitoring; enforcement deters exploitation of the weaker side.
- **Power-sharing pact** — A hard guarantee of political, military, and/or territorial representation in the first postwar administration. Distinct from simply allowing competitive elections — it removes the winner-take-all payoff that drives combatants back to war.
- **Hegemony (H) / Sucker (S) / War (W) / Democratization (D) / Power-sharing (P)** — Walter's payoff labels. Preference ordering for both players: **H > D (or P) > W > S**. The sucker payoff is so catastrophic that combatants refuse to expose themselves to it absent external guarantees.
- **Backward induction** — The solution concept Walter uses across all three game models; combatants anticipate the last-move temptation to renege and therefore refuse to take step 1.
- **Self-enforcing vs. externally-enforced cooperation** — Axelrod-style self-enforcing cooperation (reciprocal demobilization, monitoring, costly signals) fails under civil-war conditions; externally-enforced cooperation via a third party is the only route.

---

## ASSUMPTIONS

- Each faction has a single coherent set of preferences and leaders represent majority preferences within their group.
- Leaders are rational actors who prefer full control of government to shared control (H > P).
- Both sides know their rival will act opportunistically if given the chance; generosity cannot be assumed.
- Once negotiations are initiated, both sides prefer peace to continued war — this isolates the commitment problem from the "preferences over peace" problem.
- Combatants pay no costs to negotiate (a simplifying assumption).
- Demobilization has a **clear last move** / terminal point, unlike open-ended iterated games.
- In Model 3, combatants can observe signals of a third party's commitment (troop numbers, mandate, deployment posture) but cannot fully verify commitment until tested.

---

## THE THREE GAME MODELS (Walter's formal core)

- **Model 1 — Basic model (elections only, no third party):** Backward induction yields renewed war. Whichever side wins the first election has dominant strategy to consolidate a one-party state (H > D), so no one demobilizes. Shows why **political guarantees matter**.
- **Model 2 — Guaranteed power sharing, no third party:** Even with P locked in as the final payoff, combatants still defect early because demobilization itself is a short-term PD with catastrophic S. Shows that power-sharing alone is insufficient — **short-term safety matters**.
- **Model 3 — Power sharing + third-party guarantee:** If the third party's commitment is credible (p = 1), unique equilibrium is sign + demobilize + power-share. If p = 0, war. Under uncertainty, comparative statics show combatants cooperate more as (a) punishment costs rise, (b) hegemony payoff H shrinks, (c) power-sharing payoff P rises, (d) sucker payoff S becomes less extreme, (e) war costs rise.

**Three derived hypotheses (Walter's H7–H9):**
- H7: Settlements with power-sharing pacts are more likely to be signed and implemented than those without.
- H8: Settlements with credible third-party enforcement/verification are more likely to be signed and implemented.
- H9: The more committed the third party appears, the more likely negotiations succeed.

## STRENGTHS

- **Diagnoses why many well-crafted peace deals fail at implementation** rather than at the bargaining table — a phase most prior scholarship (ripeness theory, hurting stalemate) ignored.
- **Unifies civil-war settlement with the broader credible-commitment research program** (Fearon 1995; Williamson 1983), embedding civil wars in mainstream rationalist IR theory.
- **Policy-operational**: generates concrete, testable levers — troop size, mandate, enforcement posture, power-sharing depth — that outsiders can manipulate.
- **Explains variation in settlement success** where bargaining-range theories cannot: two deals with identical terms can have opposite outcomes depending on third-party presence.
- **Predicts observable comparative statics** (H-S gap, war costs, punishment credibility) that subsequent scholars (Fortna 2008; Hartzell & Hoddie 2007) have largely corroborated.

## WEAKNESSES

- **Treats the third party as non-strategic** — in reality, outsiders face their own commitment problems (US in Somalia, UN in Rwanda), and their intervention decisions are endogenous to expected success.
- **Assumes unitary factions** — ignores spoilers and intra-group heterogeneity (Kydd & Walter 2002 on extremist sabotage partially addresses this, but Ch. 2 abstracts it away).
- **Two-player structure** — multi-party civil wars (Syria, early Angola, DRC) with shifting coalitions strain the framework.
- **Under-specifies identity, ideology, and grievance** — treats combatants as instrumental security-seekers, which fits some wars (El Salvador) less well than others (Rwanda, Bosnia where ethnic extermination may be a terminal goal, not a bargaining move).
- **Silent on partition as an alternative** — Walter notes governments rarely accept it and brackets it, but secession (South Sudan, Eritrea) is a real option the model forecloses.
- **Empirical identification challenges** — third-party guarantees are not randomly assigned; outsiders intervene where they expect success, creating selection bias in the observed correlation between intervention and settlement survival.
- **Does not fully explain partial implementations** — cases like Mindanao or Colombia where deals stall mid-implementation without full reversion to war fit awkwardly.

## CONNECTIONS

- **Fearon (1995) "Rationalist Explanations for War":** Walter ports Fearon's commitment-problem mechanism from interstate to intrastate settings; the "power shift during demobilization" is the civil-war analog of Fearon's preventive-war logic.
- **Powell (2006) "War as a Commitment Problem":** Walter's demobilization window is the clearest empirical case of Powell's large, rapid, exogenous power shifts that defeat bargaining.
- **Orange Memo (Myanmar):** Directly applicable — the Tatmadaw vs. ethnic armed organizations (EAOs) and NUG/PDF face exactly Walter's dual problem: no credible third-party guarantor (ASEAN's Five-Point Consensus has failed), and the junta has no interest in credible power-sharing. Walter's framework predicts continued war, which matches observation.
- **GPPS 444 History of Warfare:** Walter's demobilization hazard echoes post-Thirty-Years-War settlement problems — Westphalia's durability rested on mutual exhaustion plus external guarantors, not on terms alone.
- **W5 Democratization readings (Meng & Little on backsliding):** Walter's Model 1 is essentially a one-shot democratization-under-anarchy failure; Meng & Little extend the logic to repeated backsliding within formally democratic institutions.
