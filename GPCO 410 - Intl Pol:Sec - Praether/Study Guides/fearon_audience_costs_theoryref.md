## Audience Costs and Crisis Signaling  [ANCHOR]

### SITUATION
Kennedy publicly declaring a U.S. quarantine of Cuba in October 1962: by going public, he locked himself into a position that would carry a domestic political cost if he backed down, and Khrushchev — watching the speech — updated that the U.S. commitment was credible precisely because retreat had become expensive at home.

### CORE INTUITION
International crises resemble a **war of attrition** in which each side incrementally escalates and either the other backs down or war occurs. Leaders who escalate publicly incur **audience costs** — domestic political penalties (lost office, reputation, party standing) for backing down after having committed. Because audience costs accumulate with escalation, escalating is a **costly signal** that reveals private information about a state's true resolve; cheap talk cannot do this. States with **larger audience costs** (typically democracies, with organized opposition and free press) can generate credible signals faster and therefore either win concessions early or stop escalating before it is too late. The key implication: democracies are not more pacific overall, but they are **better at signaling resolve**, so crises they enter end quickly — by opponent concessions or by their own early exit.

### KEY CONCEPTS, KEYWORDS & TERMINOLOGY
- **Audience Costs** — Domestic political penalties a leader pays for publicly committing and then backing down; core costly-signaling device.
- **War of Attrition** — Crisis modeled as sequential escalation where each step raises the cost of retreat for both sides.
- **Costly Signal** — An action that reveals private information because bluffers are unwilling to pay its cost; distinct from cheap talk.
- **Private Information / Resolve** — Each state knows its own willingness to fight; the opponent does not, and this asymmetry drives crises.
- **Tying Hands vs. Sinking Costs** — Audience costs are a "tying hands" mechanism (cost paid only if you back down); distinct from upfront costs like mobilization.
- **Regime Type and Signaling** — Democracies generate larger and more transparent audience costs (elections, press, opposition), producing clearer signals.
- **Crisis Duration** — Prediction: high-audience-cost states' crises terminate sooner because informational asymmetry resolves faster.

### ASSUMPTIONS
- Leaders are rational and care about political survival; audience costs map onto a real loss in probability of retaining office.
- Domestic audiences observe the leader's public commitments and punish visible backing down.
- States have private information about their own resolve that cannot be credibly conveyed by cheap talk.
- Escalation steps are publicly observable to both the opponent and the domestic audience.
- The opponent understands the domestic institutional environment well enough to infer audience-cost size.

### STRENGTHS
- Provides a micro-foundation for democratic credibility without invoking norms — complements selectorate/institutional accounts.
- Generates sharp, testable predictions about crisis duration, reciprocity, and early concessions by democracies' opponents.
- Explains why public, high-profile commitments (speeches, red lines) carry signaling weight that private diplomacy lacks.
- Reconciles the dyadic democratic peace with non-monadic behavior: democracies still fight, but they signal resolve earlier.
- Bridges formal bargaining theory (Fearon 1995) and domestic politics, anchoring an entire research program.

### WEAKNESSES
- Empirical identification of audience costs is hard; experimental evidence (Tomz, Trager) is strong but observational tests are mixed.
- Assumes domestic audiences consistently punish backing down; voters may in fact reward prudence or forget quickly.
- Some autocracies (China, Russia) appear to generate real audience costs via nationalism, blurring regime-type predictions.
- Model treats escalation as transparent; many real commitments are ambiguous, deniable, or couched in diplomatic code.
- Under-specifies how leaders choose when to escalate publicly versus privately; selection into public commitments is endogenous.
