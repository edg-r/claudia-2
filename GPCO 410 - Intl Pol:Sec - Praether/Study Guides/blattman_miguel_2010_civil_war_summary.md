# Title: Civil War (2010) — Blattman, Christopher; Miguel, Edward
Source/Link: *Journal of Economic Literature* 48(1): 3-57; doi:10.1257/jel.48.1.3

## BLUF
Blattman and Miguel survey the economics and political-science literature on civil war and find robust cross-country evidence that low per-capita income and slow growth predict conflict, but existing rationalist theories (information, commitment, indivisibility) remain incomplete and weakly tested. Their main call: shift from cross-country regressions to micro-level, subnational, and quasi-experimental research to identify causal mechanisms and guide policy (pp. 3, 8, 41).

## Context & Scope
- Review essay covering roughly 1960-2006 civil-conflict data from UCDP/PRIO (25+ battle deaths = conflict; 1,000+ = civil war); one-third of nations affected since 1960, 20% with 10+ years of war (pp. 3-4).
- Draws on contest models, bargaining theory, behavioral economics, and emerging microeconomic fieldwork (pp. 9-15).

## Key Evidence / Claims
- Low income is the most robust correlate of civil war; non-parametric Fan regressions show strongly declining war incidence as GDP per capita rises (Figure 3, pp. 4-7).
- Rationalist bargaining accounts (Fearon 1995) — asymmetric information, commitment problems, issue indivisibilities — dominate theory but are rarely tested head-to-head; most theory focuses on the "rational war despite known costs" case (pp. 11-13).
- Commitment problems are "the most intriguing" explanatory route: weak state capacity plus anticipated power shifts produce war even under full information; Powell (2006) reduces all three failures to large shifts in future power distribution (pp. 13-14).
- Contest/unitary-actor models fail to capture collective-action foundations of rebellion — group cohesion, selective incentives (material, coercive, ideological), and ethnic mobilization need microfoundations (pp. 14-16).
- Cross-country work faces severe identification problems (reverse causality, measurement error, unit independence); subnational and experimental designs are the productive frontier (pp. 8, 41).

## Implications for Policy/Practice
- Poverty-reduction and growth-stabilization policies likely reduce conflict risk, but causal direction runs both ways — interventions need to be evaluated with credible identification before scaling (pp. 4, 8).
- Third-party enforcement (peacekeepers, sanctions, aid conditionality) can substitute for weak domestic institutions in solving commitment problems, though international indictments (e.g., ICC) may perversely extend fighting when they remove exit options for rebel leaders (p. 14).

## Limits / Counterpoints
- Authors concede behavioral, ideological, and multi-actor mechanisms are underdeveloped in formal theory; neglect of terrorism, coups, and communal violence limits generalizability (pp. 6, 16).
- Cross-country regressions may have been pushed to their empirical limit; strong claims about specific causal channels remain premature (p. 8).
