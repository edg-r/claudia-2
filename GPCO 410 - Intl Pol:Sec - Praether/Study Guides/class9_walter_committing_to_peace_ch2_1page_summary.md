# Class 9 Prep — Why So Few Negotiated Settlements in Civil Wars?

**Date:** Monday, April 27, 2026
**Assigned reading:** Barbara F. Walter, *Committing to Peace*, Chapter 2, pp. 19-43
**Core question:** Why are civil wars so difficult to resolve through negotiated settlements?

## BLUF

Walter's central claim is that civil-war settlements fail because implementation requires armed groups to demobilize before a new state order is secure. That creates a credible commitment problem: once one side disarms, the other can renege, seize power, or eliminate its rival. The practical takeaway is sharp: peace agreements need credible third-party security guarantees and real power sharing, not just mutually acceptable written terms (Walter, 2002, pp. 19-43).

## Argument in One Chain

- **Civil wars are hard to end because the most dangerous moment comes after agreement.** Negotiation and bargaining can produce terms both sides prefer to continued war, but implementation forces combatants to surrender weapons, territory, and autonomous command structures. That creates a "window of vulnerability" during demobilization.

- **Backward induction kills the deal.** If each side anticipates that the final, consolidated government will be tempted to exclude or attack a demobilized rival, rational actors refuse to begin demobilization. The issue is not simply hatred, greed, or bad faith; it is that no side can credibly bind its future self once the balance of power shifts.

- **Power sharing helps but is not enough.** Walter distinguishes agreements that merely promise elections from settlements that guarantee political, military, or territorial shares in the first postwar government. Power sharing reduces the fear of permanent exclusion, but it still does not protect groups while they are physically disarming.

- **Third-party guarantees are the binding mechanism.** A credible outside actor can verify compliance when the sides are relatively balanced, or enforce compliance when one side is especially exposed. The guarantee must be observable and costly enough that combatants believe the third party will act if cheating occurs.

## What to Remember for Class

Walter is extending the course's bargaining-war logic from interstate war into civil war. The basic problem fits the Fearon/Powell lineage: even when a bargaining range exists, the parties cannot commit to respect it after the distribution of power changes. In civil wars, demobilization itself creates that power shift.

The key contrast is between **settlement terms** and **settlement enforcement**. A well-designed agreement can still fail if the implementation environment recreates anarchy. Conversely, a deal with imperfect terms can hold if it includes credible security guarantees and enough power sharing to keep the "sucker" payoff from being catastrophic.

For policy analysis, the question is not "Did the parties sign a peace deal?" but "Who protects each side while its fighters disarm, and what institutional share does each side retain afterward?" That is the standard Walter wants analysts to apply to cases such as Cambodia, Rwanda, Colombia/FARC, and Myanmar.

## Exam / Memo Use

Use Walter when a case involves rebels and governments who might prefer peace but cannot trust the transition from armed competition to unified authority. The clean sentence: **civil-war settlements fail at implementation because demobilization creates a commitment problem that only credible outside guarantees and power sharing can solve.**
