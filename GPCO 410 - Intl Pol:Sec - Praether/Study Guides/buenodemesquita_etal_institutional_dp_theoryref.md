## Selectorate Theory of the Democratic Peace  [ANCHOR]

### SITUATION
Two democracies on the brink of crisis (e.g., the U.S.–U.K. Oregon dispute, Fashoda): each leader survives politically only by delivering public goods to a very large winning coalition, so both prefer to try harder or back down rather than gamble their office on a risky war against another democracy that will also fight hard.

### CORE INTUITION
Democratic peace is not about shared norms or culture but about the **institutional incentives leaders face to stay in power**. Every polity has a **selectorate (S)** — those with a formal say in choosing the leader — and a **winning coalition (W)** — the subset whose support the leader actually needs. When **W is large** (democracy), leaders cannot buy loyalty cheaply with **private goods**; they must provide **public goods**, including policy success. War failure is a catastrophic public bad, so large-W leaders **try harder** in the wars they fight and are highly selective about entering them. When two large-W states meet, each expects the other to mobilize maximum effort, so both prefer negotiation to a coin-flip war. Against a **small-W autocrat**, the democrat expects a weaker effort and is more willing to fight. The dyadic peace between democracies falls out of the model without invoking norms.

### KEY CONCEPTS, KEYWORDS & TERMINOLOGY
- **Selectorate (S)** — The set of people with some formal role in choosing the leader (voters, party members, royal family).
- **Winning Coalition (W)** — The minimum subset of S whose support keeps the leader in office; large in democracies, small in autocracies.
- **W/S ratio (loyalty norm)** — Probability any given selectorate member stays in the coalition; low W/S locks in loyalty cheaply via private goods.
- **Public vs. Private Goods** — Public goods (victory, security, growth) benefit all; private goods (patronage, rents) benefit only coalition members. Large W forces public-goods provision.
- **Trying Harder** — Large-W leaders devote more national resources to wars they enter because losing is politically fatal; a core empirical implication.
- **Political Survival** — The leader's objective function; all foreign-policy choices are instrumental to remaining in office.
- **Dyadic vs. Monadic Peace** — The theory predicts dyadic peace (democracies fight autocracies) rather than monadic pacifism (democracies fight less overall).

### ASSUMPTIONS
- Leaders are rational, office-seeking actors who maximize the probability of political survival.
- Institutions (W and S) are exogenous and observable; leaders cannot costlessly reshape them in a crisis.
- Citizens evaluate leaders on delivered goods (public + private) relative to a challenger's credible offer.
- War outcomes are a function of relative effort, which leaders choose given institutional incentives.
- Information about the opponent's W and expected effort is common knowledge in the crisis bargaining stage.

### STRENGTHS
- Generates the dyadic democratic peace from micro-level survival incentives without invoking norms or culture.
- Explains why democracies win the wars they fight (selection + trying harder) — a robust empirical regularity.
- Unifies domestic politics, war initiation, war effort, and war outcomes in one institutional framework.
- Predicts behaviors beyond DP: autocratic kleptocracy, long autocratic tenures, democratic public-goods bias.
- Offers falsifiable comparative statics (vary W, vary S) that travel outside the democracy/autocracy binary.

### WEAKNESSES
- Treats W and S as sharp categories; measurement in mixed or hybrid regimes is contested.
- Downplays identity, ideology, and normative convergence that Doyle/Russett emphasize.
- Assumes leaders correctly read the opponent's institutions; misperception and mixed signals are under-modeled.
- Dyadic prediction sits awkwardly with cases of democratic covert intervention against elected governments.
- Endogeneity: institutions may themselves be shaped by prior war outcomes and external threat.
