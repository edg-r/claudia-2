## 1. Game Theory
- Week introduced: Week 1
- Status: introduced
- Core intuition: Strategic outcomes depend not only on what one actor wants, but on what all relevant actors choose while anticipating one another.
- Main assumptions:
  - actors are strategic
  - outcomes depend on interdependence
  - incentives and information matter
- Key actors / units of analysis:
  - typically states, leaders, or organized actors
- Strategic logic / mechanism:
  - players choose under conditions shaped by payoffs, information, sequencing, and credibility
- Strengths:
  - sharpens strategic reasoning
  - makes interaction structure explicit
  - useful for deterrence, bargaining, and conflict analysis
- Weaknesses / critiques:
  - may simplify preferences too aggressively
  - can assume cleaner rationality than real political behavior supports
  - outcomes can be highly sensitive to assumptions
- Useful applications:
  - deterrence
  - crisis bargaining
  - alliance behavior
  - war onset / escalation
- Relationship to other theories:
  - likely foundational for later theories involving preferences, bargaining, signaling, and commitment problems

## 2. Preference Theory
- Week introduced: Week 2
- Status: introduced
- Core intuition: Political and strategic outcomes depend heavily on how actors rank options, what they value, and how those preferences are structured.
- Main assumptions:
  - actors have ordered preferences
  - preference structure influences strategy and outcomes
  - conflict/cooperation often cannot be understood without specifying what actors actually want
- Key actors / units of analysis:
  - states, coalitions, leaders, bureaucracies, domestic groups, or other strategic actors
- Strategic logic / mechanism:
  - behavior emerges from the interaction between preferences, constraints, and strategic setting
- Strengths:
  - clarifies what drives actor behavior
  - helps distinguish disagreement over outcomes from disagreement over beliefs or information
- Weaknesses / critiques:
  - preferences may be unstable, endogenous, or hard to observe
  - can become vague if not operationalized carefully
- Useful applications:
  - bargaining analysis
  - coalition behavior
  - foreign policy choice
  - security alignment
- Relationship to other theories:
  - likely complements game theory by specifying what actors are trying to maximize or protect
