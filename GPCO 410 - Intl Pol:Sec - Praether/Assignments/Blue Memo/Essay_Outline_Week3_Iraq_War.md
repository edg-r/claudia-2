# Analytical Essay Outline: The Impossibility of a Negotiated Settlement Before the 2003 Iraq War

**Course:** GPCO 410 – International Politics/Security
**Readings:** Powell, "War as a Commitment Problem" (2006); Woods, Lacey, & Murray, "Saddam's Delusions: The View From the Inside" (2006)
**Target Length:** ~900 words

---

## I. Introduction / Context (~150 words)

**Opening hook:** By March 2003, George W. Bush and Saddam Hussein stood on opposite sides of what appeared to be a negotiable dispute — Iraq's alleged weapons of mass destruction program — yet neither actor could credibly commit to terms that would have averted war.

**Key context to establish:**
- Post-9/11 security environment: Bush's administration reoriented U.S. foreign policy around preempting threats before they materialized, reframing the calculus of tolerable risk.
- Saddam Hussein's strategic position after 1991: over a decade of sanctions, no-fly zones, and UN inspections, yet Saddam maintained a deliberate ambiguity about Iraq's WMD capabilities to serve competing audiences.
- The puzzle: war is costly and destroys value for both sides (the "inefficiency puzzle"). A bargaining range theoretically existed. Why did both actors fail to reach an agreement within it?

**Thesis:** A negotiated settlement between Bush and Saddam Hussein was not possible prior to the 2003 war. Applying Powell's (2006) framework of war as a commitment problem, the structural inability of either actor to credibly commit to a settlement — compounded by severe informational distortions on both sides — made the conflict effectively inevitable.

---

## II. Theoretical Framework: War as a Commitment Problem (~150 words)

**Core concept:** Powell (2006) argues that war should be understood not primarily as a product of informational asymmetry, but as a *commitment problem*. Even when a bargaining range exists (agreements both sides prefer to fighting), actors may be unable to commit to honoring those agreements.

**Three types of commitment problems (briefly note all three, emphasize the first):**
1. **Preventive war from shifting power** — When one actor anticipates that the distribution of power will shift against them in the future, the temporarily strong actor may prefer to fight now rather than make concessions that the rising power will later exploit. *This is the primary mechanism applicable to the Bush-Saddam case.*
2. Preemptive war from first-strike or offensive advantages.
3. Conflicts over issues that affect future bargaining power.

**The inefficiency condition:** Bargaining breaks down when the anticipated shift in power exceeds the bargaining surplus — i.e., when what can be credibly promised is less than what the adversary can lock in through force.

**Transition:** Apply this framework to the specific strategic calculations of Bush and Saddam Hussein to demonstrate why no settlement was reachable.

---

## III. Application of the Framework (~450 words)

### A. Key Actors and What Each Sought to Maximize

**George W. Bush:**
- Sought to maximize U.S. national security by preventing the emergence of a nuclear-armed Iraq, which Bush and his advisors perceived as an unacceptable shift in the future distribution of power.
- Post-9/11, Bush's administration adopted the doctrine of preemption: the cost of being wrong about WMDs (allowing Saddam to develop a nuclear weapon) was judged to far exceed the cost of war.
- Available actions: diplomatic pressure through the UN, continued sanctions and containment, or military invasion. Bush ultimately concluded that containment could not credibly prevent Saddam from reconstituting WMD programs over time — a classic commitment problem.

**Saddam Hussein:**
- Sought to maximize *regime survival and personal control* above all else (Woods et al., 2006). Every strategic decision — military deployments, WMD ambiguity, the appointment of loyalists over competent officers — served this singular objective.
- Available actions: full compliance with UN inspections (disarming transparently), continued obfuscation, or outright defiance. Saddam chose obfuscation — attempting to simultaneously signal to the UN that Iraq had no WMDs while signaling to Arab neighbors and Iran that Iraq retained a deterrent capability.
- This dual-signaling strategy was not irrational from Saddam's perspective: he believed WMD ambiguity deterred Iran and bolstered his regional standing, which he valued for regime security.

### B. Beliefs About Opponent Responses and the Information Problem

**Saddam's beliefs about Bush:**
- Saddam was "very confident" the U.S. would not invade, believing France and Russia would veto any UN authorization and that international pressure would restrain Bush (Woods et al., 2006).
- He believed that even if a ground invasion occurred, Iraqi forces would inflict heavy casualties, causing the U.S. to halt and negotiate — a fundamental miscalculation about American resolve and military capability.
- These beliefs were reinforced by a *domestic information pathology*: Saddam's subordinates, from the Military Industrial Commission to senior Republican Guard commanders, systematically fabricated or exaggerated reports to avoid punishment. This two-level game within the Iraqi regime meant Saddam was operating on distorted intelligence about his own military readiness and the external threat environment.

**Bush's beliefs about Saddam:**
- Bush and his advisors interpreted Saddam's years of obfuscation through the lens of prior deception — a decade of Iraqi noncompliance made any cooperative signals from Saddam appear incredible. Even when Iraq attempted genuine compliance in late 2002, these efforts were read as further concealment (e.g., the "nerve agents" radio intercept).
- Bush perceived an anticipated shift in power: if Saddam were allowed to reconstitute WMD programs, the cost of confrontation would rise dramatically in the future. This maps directly onto Powell's preventive war logic.

### C. Why No Settlement Was Possible

- **Saddam could not credibly commit to disarmament** because his years of deliberate obfuscation had destroyed his credibility. Even genuine compliance efforts were interpreted as deception. Powell's framework explains this: the problem was not the absence of a mutually acceptable deal, but the inability to commit to one.
- **Bush could not credibly commit to restraint** if Saddam disarmed, because the administration's stated goal had expanded beyond WMD disarmament to regime change — meaning even full compliance might not have satisfied Bush's demands.
- **The anticipated power shift drove the timeline:** Bush perceived that delay would allow Saddam to grow stronger (through potential nuclear development), making war more costly in the future. Powell's inefficiency condition was met — the perceived shift in power exceeded the bargaining surplus.

---

## IV. Conclusion (~150 words)

**Restate thesis:** The 2003 Iraq War was not the result of a simple miscommunication or a failure of diplomacy — it was the product of a structural commitment problem that made a negotiated settlement effectively impossible.

**Synthesize the argument:**
- Saddam Hussein's drive to maximize regime control produced the WMD obfuscation strategy, the sycophantic information environment, and the strategic miscalculation about American intentions — all of which eliminated any credible path to a negotiated settlement.
- George W. Bush's perception of a shifting distribution of power (potential Iraqi nuclear capability) created the preventive war logic that Powell's framework formalizes: the cost of tolerating the status quo exceeded the cost of war.
- The informational failures on both sides — Saddam's distorted intelligence from subordinates, Bush's interpretation of Iraqi compliance as deception — were not independent causes of war but rather the *mechanisms through which the underlying commitment problem became irresolvable*.

**Closing:** Frame the case as an illustration of how commitment problems, once embedded in the strategic environment, can make war the rational (if tragic) outcome for actors operating under their respective constraints.
