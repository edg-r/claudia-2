## Thesis (placeholder — sharpen after Edgar reviews)

Myanmar's 2010–2021 arc raised, not lowered, the country's civil war risk — and the 2021 coup is the mechanism predicted by the theoretical literature rather than a departure from it. The 2010 opening created a Cederman-style incomplete democratization with elite mobilization ahead of institutional depth; the 2008 constitution's reserved military powers built an unresolved commitment problem in Walter's sense; the 2020 NLD supermajority raised the future cost of that reservation beyond what the Tatmadaw was willing to bear. The post-coup civil war is the realization, not the refutation, of that risk.

**Alternative one-sentence framing if the 972-word ceiling bites:** Both the 2010 democratization and the 2021 coup raised Myanmar's civil war risk for reasons Cederman et al. and Walter predict — the coup did not depart from the democratization risk, it completed it.

## Structure — 5 body beats + counterargument + policy implication

### Beat 1 — Theoretical frame (~200 words)

Lead with Cederman, Hug & Krebs 2010 finding: periods of democratization raise civil war onset probability, controlling for Polity level and institutional quality. Pair with Walter 2009: commitment problems are especially severe in regime transitions because no third-party enforcer exists to bind future behavior. Powell 2006 (one footnote) provides the underlying commitment-problem vocabulary — present as a *unifying mechanism*, not a three-part taxonomy (per Athena FEEDBACK 2026-04-14).

Key terminology discipline: avoid "miscalculation in game theory." Use "information failure" or "commitment problem" directly (Athena FEEDBACK).

### Beat 2 — The 2010–2015 opening as an incomplete democratization (~200 words)

- USDP election 2010 was Tatmadaw-managed, NLD boycott.
- 2008 constitution as commitment device: 25% reserved seats (Art. 109, 141), military ministries (Home/Defense/Border), constitutional veto (Art. 436 requires >75%).
- NLD landslide 2015 created a dual-center regime: elected civilian government without civilian control over coercion.
- Under Cederman, this is governmental-conflict risk territory. Under Walter, this is an unresolved commitment problem parked in the constitution.

### Beat 3 — The Rohingya crisis as evidence the reforms did not bind the Tatmadaw (~150 words)

- 2016–2017 Rakhine operations: 740,000 displaced. Tatmadaw conducted mass violence during the reform era. ASSK's ICJ defense in 2019 demonstrated the NLD's dependence on the Tatmadaw for ethnic-security management.
- Reframe: the pre-coup period already carried significant intrastate violence. Baseline civil-war risk was not low even before 2021.
- Useful pivot to Beat 4: the commitment problem was visible before it detonated.

### Beat 4 — The 2020 landslide and the power-shift trigger (~200 words)

- NLD 82% of contested seats in 2020, up from 77% in 2015.
- From the Tatmadaw's view, the future cost of honoring the 2008 settlement was rising: NLD could plausibly build a coalition for constitutional amendment over 2–3 electoral cycles.
- Powell's power-shift logic: the incumbent (Tatmadaw) cannot credibly bind itself to wait for the shift to complete. Coup is the rational action when the bargaining range narrows below the war cost.
- The USDP/Tatmadaw fraud allegations after the 2020 vote served as the pretext, not the cause. The cause is the structural commitment problem.

### Beat 5 — Post-coup civil war as the realization (~150 words)

- NUG formation, PDF mobilization, Three Brotherhood Alliance Operation 1027 (2023).
- Critical: pre-2021 Myanmar's civil wars were territorial (EAOs on periphery). Post-2021 fighting is *governmental* — PDFs aim to restore civilian rule at the center. This matches Cederman et al.'s finding that democratization risk is concentrated in governmental rather than territorial civil conflict.
- Evidence pegs: ACLED fatalities 50,000+, UNOCHA 3M+ IDPs, SAC-M territorial-control analysis showing junta <50% of townships.

### Beat 6 — Counterargument (~150 words)

The strongest counterargument: a "stability narrative" that sees the 2010–2020 period as delivering reduced internal conflict relative to the pre-2010 baseline. Economic opening, EAO ceasefires (Nationwide Ceasefire Agreement 2015), reduced direct military governance.

Rebuttal: the stability was contingent on the Tatmadaw's continued veto. It was not a stable equilibrium — it was an unresolved bargain. The 2021 coup shows the bargain's terms could not survive a shift in the underlying distribution of power. Cederman et al.'s finding that *both* democratization and autocratization raise risk fits this pattern: the 2010–2020 democratization period raised risk slowly; the 2021 autocratization realized it suddenly.

Alternative counterargument worth one sentence of acknowledgment: the structural determinants of civil war (poverty, ethnic diversity, rough terrain — Fearon & Laitin 2003) predict high baseline risk regardless of regime type. True, but these don't explain the *timing* of the coup-to-war transition. The theoretical frame (Cederman + Walter) does.

### Beat 7 — Policy implication (~100 words)

- Democracy-promotion programs that stop at elections without building civilian control over coercion are building commitment problems, not democracies.
- For Myanmar specifically: a viable settlement requires a credible third-party enforcer (Walter). ASEAN's Five-Point Consensus is not one. China's interest is stability on its border, not regime transition.
- The 2010–2020 "reform" narrative was wrong in theory (it ignored the commitment problem) and wrong in practice (the reforms did not bind the Tatmadaw during the Rohingya crisis).
