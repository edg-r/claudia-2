## Reading 1 — Cederman, Hug & Krebs (2010) — PRIMARY ANCHOR

**Title:** "Democratization and Civil War: Empirical Evidence" (2010) — Lars-Erik Cederman, Simon Hug, Lutz F. Krebs
**Source/Link:** *Journal of Peace Research* 47(4): 377–394; DOI 10.1177/0022343310368336
**Local file:** `W6 - Seeking the Bomb/Week 6-Democratization and Civil War.pdf` (OCR-clean, 18pp, 66,002 chars)

### BLUF
Democratization — even partial and especially when it falters — robustly raises the probability of civil war onset; autocratization does too, but its effect hits sooner. Using a new period-finding algorithm on Polity IV data, the authors confirm a strong effect that persists controlling for regime incoherence and instability, but the effect is confined to **governmental** (center-seeking) conflicts, not territorial ones (p. 377 abstract; Table III results).

### Context & Scope
- Quantitative cross-national study of all states since 1946, testing whether regime-change episodes (periods of democratization or autocratization in Polity IV) predict civil war onset (pp. 377–378).
- Core theoretical bet (Mansfield & Snyder 1995, 2005; Snyder 2000; Mann 2005): elites mobilizing newly enfranchised citizens during transitions build in-group / out-group images that can tip into violence when weak institutions cannot absorb the mobilization (pp. 378–380).

### Key Evidence / Claims
- **Democratization coefficient is positive and robust across all specifications** (Table I, p. 383): "dem" variable statistically significant at p<0.01 across models 1.1–1.4, controlling for Polity level, anocracy, population, GDP, peace years.
- **Autocratization also predicts onset** but its time profile is shorter (p. 378, p. 383 Table I). With three years of lag (Table II, p. 384), democratization retains a significant effect (dem03 p<0.001 across specifications) while autocratization's lagged effect is weaker. Interpretation: democratization's destabilization plays out over several years as elite competition unspools; autocratization violence erupts in year one.
- **Governmental vs territorial conflicts** (Table III): the democratization effect is restricted to center-seeking civil wars, not separatist/territorial wars. Theoretical implication: the violence mechanism is elite competition over state capture, not ethnic-territorial grievance (p. 387 discussion).
- **Case illustrations** (Figure 4, pp. 383–384): Congo-Brazzaville (1990s), Pakistan (1970–71 Bangladesh war), Russia (1993 constitutional crisis), Federal Republic of Yugoslavia — supportive cases where democratization periods directly preceded internal war onset; Yugoslavia partially coded due to Polity's failure to register the 1990 multiparty opening at the federal level.
- **Method** (pp. 380–382): new "period-finding algorithm" that identifies regime-change periods flexibly rather than imposing rigid lag windows. Addresses Gleditsch, Hegre & Strand (2009); Treier & Jackman (2008); Vreeland (2008) critiques of Polity IV measurement.

### Implications for Policy/Practice
- Democracy promotion programs that produce partial transitions without institution-building raise civil war risk, particularly for center-seeking insurgencies. Sequencing matters: institutions first, elections later, rather than the reverse.
- Analysts measuring transition-era risk should track *periods of change* rather than Polity level alone — a state scoring +4 on the way up and a state scoring +4 on the way down carry different risk profiles (Figure 4 visual logic).

### Limits / Counterpoints
- The authors cannot identify causal mechanisms directly — they infer from distributional patterns. Cases like Russia post-Chechnya and Yugoslavia pre-Balkan wars show the Polity coding itself fails to register the relevant institutional change (p. 384).
- The autocratization finding complicates the "democratization is dangerous" headline. Both directional changes elevate risk, which challenges any Cold War framing that sees authoritarian stability as a safe baseline.

### Application to Myanmar
- Myanmar 2010–2015 coded as a democratization period (Polity score moves from −6 toward positive; transfer of civilian governance under a NLD-led government in 2015). Under Cederman et al.'s logic, this period itself elevates civil war risk even before the 2021 coup.
- 2021 coup codes as an autocratization — under Cederman et al., autocratization hits sooner and harder. Post-coup conflict explosion (NUG/PDFs vs Tatmadaw, expanded EAO activity) matches that prediction.
- Governmental vs territorial distinction matters: the post-coup civil war is a hybrid. PDF fighting is largely center-seeking (restore NUG as legitimate government). EAO fighting is longstanding territorial (Kachin, Karen, Shan, Rakhine). Cederman et al.'s mechanism explains the first better than the second.

## Reading 2 — Walter (2009) — COMPLEMENTARY MECHANISM

**Title:** "Bargaining Failures and Civil War" (2009) — Barbara F. Walter
**Source/Link:** *Annual Review of Political Science* 12: 243–261; DOI 10.1146/annurev.polisci.10.101405.135301
**Local file:** `W5 - Negotiated Settlements & Democratization/Week 5-Walter.pdf` (misfiled; actually Week 4 Class 8 reading — OCR-clean, 21pp, 85,756 chars)

### BLUF
Civil wars are bargaining failures driven by information and commitment problems that are severe in intrastate disputes because governments and rebels lack credible third-party enforcers. Walter argues commitment problems are especially acute across regime transitions, where a newly strengthened adversary cannot credibly bind its future self (p. 243 abstract, pp. 248–252).

### Context & Scope
- Review essay synthesizing two decades of rationalist civil war scholarship; applies Fearon's (1995) bargaining framework and Powell's (2006) commitment-problem logic to intrastate wars (pp. 244–245).
- Canvasses onset, duration, and resolution — but the Myanmar-relevant portion is onset and especially commitment-problem dynamics during regime change (pp. 248–253).

### Key Evidence / Claims
- **Bargains are harder to reach in civil wars than in interstate wars** — fewer negotiated settlements signed; when signed, less likely to be implemented; when implemented, more likely to break down (p. 243). Structural reason: no third-party enforcer comparable to the international security environment between states.
- **Commitment problems are central to civil war onset in regime transitions** (pp. 248–252). A regime opening toward democracy creates an expected shift in the distribution of power. The incumbent cannot credibly promise not to exploit its current advantage; the opposition cannot credibly promise not to punish former regime elites once in power. This mirrors Powell's commitment-problem logic but applies it to intrastate actors (cites Powell 2006).
- **Information problems matter but are less central than commitment problems** for intrastate cases (pp. 246–248). Private information about resolve and capability can be bluffed in civil war contexts, but the harder problem is enforcement of any deal.
- **Anocracies disproportionately affected by civil war** — reference to Hegre et al. (2001) curvilinear finding (p. 244 fn 3). Middle-range Polity scores are a risk zone. This is directly what Cederman et al. dispute and reframe as a period-of-change effect.

### Implications for Policy/Practice
- External enforcers (peacekeepers, binding treaties, institutional lock-ins) are not optional extras in civil war settlements — they are the mechanism by which the commitment problem gets resolved. Absent third-party guarantees, deals won't hold.
- For states in transition: building institutions that credibly bind the future strongest actor (independent judiciary, military-civilian firewall, constitutional protections for minorities) is the substantive content of a viable peace, not democracy-promotion window-dressing.

### Limits / Counterpoints
- Walter's framework is diagnostic, not predictive. It tells you why wars happen but not which specific wars will happen in which years — the Cederman et al. empirical test is the complement that the Walter review explicitly calls for.
- Commitment problems could, in principle, stabilize authoritarian rule just as readily as they fuel civil war — the framework alone doesn't predict direction without case-level inputs.

### Application to Myanmar
- **2010–2015 opening:** Tatmadaw (the military) never fully relinquished power. The 2008 constitution reserved 25% of parliamentary seats for the military, control over three ministries (Home, Defense, Border), and a de facto veto on constitutional amendment. This is a textbook unresolved commitment problem: civilian governance without a credible mechanism binding military non-interference.
- **Post-2015 NLD government:** Aung San Suu Kyi's government bargained within the constraints the Tatmadaw set. The bargain held as long as the expected-power shift stayed gradual. The 2020 NLD landslide accelerated that shift, raising the military's expected future loss.
- **2021 coup:** Walter's framework predicts exactly this sequence. When the incumbent (Tatmadaw) faces a credibly irreversible shift in the distribution of power (NLD supermajority plus ongoing civilian legitimacy), and cannot credibly be bound by civilian promises of non-prosecution, it strikes. The Feb 2021 coup is the commitment problem cashing in.
- **Post-coup war:** The NUG/PDF resistance and the EAO alliances reflect a bargaining range that has collapsed. No credible enforcement mechanism exists for a return to civilian rule. Walter's framework predicts prolonged conflict unless an external enforcer (ASEAN, UN, China) imposes one — which none currently has incentive or capability to do.
