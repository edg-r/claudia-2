## Actors

- **Tatmadaw** — Myanmar armed forces, the institutional military. Held direct power 1962–2011; held reserved constitutional power 2011–2021; resumed direct rule Feb 2021 under the State Administration Council (SAC) led by Sen-Gen Min Aung Hlaing.
- **NLD (National League for Democracy)** — civilian party led by Aung San Suu Kyi (ASSK). Won landslide elections 2015 and 2020. Dissolved in 2023 under SAC-imposed electoral law. ASSK held multiple concurrent prison sentences totaling 27+ years as of 2023.
- **USDP (Union Solidarity and Development Party)** — military-proxy civilian party. Won the 2010 election (boycotted by NLD). Lost decisively in 2015 and 2020.
- **NUG (National Unity Government)** — parallel government formed Apr 2021 by ousted NLD parliamentarians and ethnic minority representatives. Operates in exile and underground. Recognized by the European Parliament (resolution) but no state has extended full diplomatic recognition as of 2025. Self-identifies as the legitimate government of Myanmar.
- **PDF (People's Defence Force)** — NUG-aligned armed resistance, formed May 2021. Decentralized township-level militias. Numbers estimated 60,000–100,000 fighters in 2023–2024 ACLED/ISP reporting; not a unified command.
- **EAOs (Ethnic Armed Organizations)** — longstanding ethnic insurgencies predating the 2021 coup. Major groups: KIA (Kachin), KNU (Karen), SSPP and RCSS (Shan), AA (Arakan/Rakhine), MNDAA (Kokang), TNLA (Ta'ang). Some fight Tatmadaw; some maintain ceasefires; alliances shift. The Three Brotherhood Alliance (AA, MNDAA, TNLA) launched Operation 1027 in Oct 2023, the largest coordinated anti-SAC offensive since the coup.
- **China** — principal external influence. Hosts Tatmadaw investment, weapons supply, and border-stability deals; also maintains working relationships with select EAOs (MNDAA, UWSA) on the Yunnan border. Brokered 2024 ceasefires with Three Brotherhood factions.
- **ASEAN** — diplomatic lead via the April 2021 "Five-Point Consensus." Widely judged a failure. Myanmar's junta representatives have been excluded from ASEAN summits since late 2021.

## Polity IV coding history (relevant for data memo cross-reference)

Myanmar's Polity2 score is a paradigmatic example of the measurement problem the data memo will interrogate. Key values from Polity V/PolityProject:

- **1988–2010:** Polity2 = −8 (full autocracy / military junta).
- **2010:** transition year. Polity codes include −66 ("foreign interruption") or transition-period codes during coups and collapses. Myanmar 2010 is coded as a regime transition.
- **2011–2020:** Polity2 rises to +8 after the 2015 election (partial electoral democracy with reserved military powers).
- **2021:** coup year. Polity V coding marks the 2021 coup with a transition / interruption code depending on edition. This is the live coding puzzle — see `Data Memo - Regime Type/data_plan.md`.

The +8 score for post-2015 Myanmar is itself debatable: a constitutional system reserving 25% of seats and all coercive ministries for the military is not a standard democracy by most substantive definitions. Polity's weighting of the electoral-competition dimension above executive-constraints pushed Myanmar up the scale faster than a checks-and-balances reading would.

## Mechanisms the ORANGE memo must address

1. **Cederman-style democratization risk:** 2010–2020 was an incomplete democratization with weak institutional ballast. Elite mobilization around electoral competition but no civilian control of coercion. Matches Cederman et al.'s governmental-conflict risk profile.
2. **Walter-style commitment problem:** Tatmadaw's 2008 constitution was a commitment device to preserve the military's veto. When NLD's 2020 supermajority threatened that veto's future sustainability (possible constitutional amendment with sufficient coalition building), the commitment failed. Tatmadaw struck preemptively.
3. **Powell-style power-shift mechanism:** the 2020 NLD landslide signaled an accelerating shift in the distribution of political power away from the Tatmadaw. The military's present advantage in coercive capability could not be credibly reserved for future use. Coup is the rational action under Powell's commitment-problem logic.
4. **Post-coup explosion — the risk was realized:** pre-2021 Myanmar's EAO wars were geographically peripheral. Post-coup, PDF fighting brought the war into Bamar-majority heartland regions. This is the direct civil-war realization of the risk the framework predicted.

## Evidence pegs (for ready use in memo)

- "25% reserved seats" — 2008 constitution, Article 109(b) and 141(b).
- "Tatmadaw veto over constitutional amendment" — 2008 constitution, Article 436 (amendments require >75% of parliament).
- "NLD 2015 vote share" — NLD won 255 of 330 contested lower-house seats (77%); USDP 30.
- "NLD 2020 vote share" — NLD won 258 of 315 contested lower-house seats (82%). Expanded mandate.
- "Feb 1 2021 coup" — emergency declared under Article 417 of 2008 constitution. Vice President Myint Swe (Tatmadaw-aligned) transferred power to Min Aung Hlaing.
- "Post-coup war intensity" — ACLED recorded 50,000+ reported fatalities 2021–2024; >3 million IDPs by late 2024 per UNOCHA.
- "Rohingya precedent" — 2016–2017 Tatmadaw conduct in Rakhine demonstrated willingness to use mass violence during the "reform" period. Shows reforms did not bind Tatmadaw behavior on ethnic-security matters even pre-coup.
