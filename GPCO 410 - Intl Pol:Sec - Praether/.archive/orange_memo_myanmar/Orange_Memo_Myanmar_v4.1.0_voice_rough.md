After the 2010 opening, Myanmar carried a higher risk of political violence and civil war because democratization widened the fight over governmental power while leaving coercion in military hands. Cederman, Hug, and Krebs predict this danger. Regime-change periods raise civil war risk when political mobilization outruns the institutions meant to absorb the struggle over government.[^1] In Myanmar, Aung San Suu Kyi tried to turn National League for Democracy victories into civilian authority. Senior General Min Aung Hlaing guarded the Tatmadaw's autonomy, reserved political role, and control over force. The 2021 coup was the incomplete transition hitting the wall.

Under the 2010 reforms, the military opened the regime while keeping its hand on the state. President Thein Sein's government released Aung San Suu Kyi, loosened repression, and let electoral politics breathe, but the 2008 constitutional order preserved the Tatmadaw's veto. The military kept reserved parliamentary seats, security ministries, and enough constitutional leverage to block reforms that threatened its institutional position.[^2] Aung San Suu Kyi's 2015 victory changed who could claim democratic legitimacy. It did not change who controlled the guns. The Rohingya crisis exposed the same hierarchy. During the reform period, the Tatmadaw could still carry out mass violence in Rakhine while the civilian government lacked the power, and sometimes the willingness, to restrain it.[^3]

Cederman, Hug, and Krebs help explain why this arrangement was unstable. They separate ordinary regime type from regime change. Democratization raises civil war risk because it opens political participation, creates new winners and losers, and pushes elites to mobilize before state institutions can settle the contest. Their governmental-conflict finding matters most for Myanmar. Territorial conflict is fought over regional autonomy or secession. Governmental conflict is fought over the national state. Myanmar already had longstanding territorial conflicts involving ethnic armed organizations. The 2010 opening added a new fight over whether elected civilians or the military would control the national government. Cederman, Hug, and Krebs find that democratization hits hardest in these governmental conflicts.[^4]

The actor preferences failed to line up into a reform path. They crashed into each other. Aung San Suu Kyi wanted electoral legitimacy to become real civilian authority. If that failed, gradual reform under military constraint was still survivable. The dead end was a hollow democracy where elections existed but coercive power sat outside civilian control. Min Aung Hlaing faced the same transition from the opposite side. He wanted to preserve the Tatmadaw's autonomy, security ministries, parliamentary bloc, and final veto over the state. Civilian politics was tolerable only while it left military supremacy alone. Repeated NLD victories made that bargain look temporary. The 2015 election gave Aung San Suu Kyi a governing mandate. The 2020 landslide told Min Aung Hlaing that elections had begun stripping the Tatmadaw's legitimacy in public view. Fraud claims supplied the pretext for the coup. Cederman's mechanism supplied the pressure. Democratization intensified a governmental conflict faster than Myanmar's institutions could contain it.[^5]

After February 2021, the war confirmed the theory. Resistance did not stay a street protest movement or simply fold into older ethnic wars on the periphery. The National Unity Government and People's Defence Forces claimed legitimate national authority against the junta, while ethnic armed organizations widened military pressure in multiple regions.[^6] The pattern maps onto Cederman, Hug, and Krebs's distinction. The EAOs still often fight territorial conflicts over autonomy and local control. The NUG and PDFs fight a governmental conflict over who has the right to rule Myanmar. Democratization had already mobilized citizens around elections, legitimacy, and civilian rule. When the military reversed the outcome, the opposition did not demobilize. It escalated.

The main counterargument is that Myanmar looked calmer from 2010 to 2020, so democratization must have lowered risk. That reading mistakes a frozen conflict for a resolved one. The reform decade stayed relatively stable because the Tatmadaw's veto held the struggle over government in place. The transition never made the state safe. Once NLD victories made civilian supremacy politically plausible, the military chose autocratic reversal. Once the coup erased the electoral channel, the opposition chose resistance. Myanmar became violent because democratization raised the stakes of governmental power while leaving the strongest armed actor outside democratic control.
