## Memo

Myanmar was at greater risk of political violence and civil war after the 2010 opening because democratization expanded competition over governmental power without producing civilian control over coercion. Cederman, Hug, and Krebs predict this danger. Their claim is not that stable democracies cause civil war, but that regime-change periods raise civil war risk when political mobilization grows faster than institutions can absorb the struggle over government.<sup>1</sup> In Myanmar, Aung San Suu Kyi tried to convert National League for Democracy electoral victories into civilian authority. Senior General Min Aung Hlaing protected the Tatmadaw's autonomy, reserved political role, and control over force. The 2021 coup realized the risk created by that incomplete transition.

The 2010 reforms were a military-managed opening, not a transfer to civilian supremacy. President Thein Sein's government released Aung San Suu Kyi, loosened repression, and created space for electoral politics, but the 2008 constitutional order preserved the Tatmadaw's veto. The military retained reserved parliamentary seats, control over security ministries, and enough constitutional leverage to block reforms that threatened its institutional position.<sup>2</sup> Aung San Suu Kyi's 2015 victory therefore changed who could claim democratic legitimacy, but not who controlled the state apparatus of coercion. The Rohingya crisis exposed the same hierarchy. During the reform period, the Tatmadaw could still conduct mass violence in Rakhine while the civilian government lacked the power, and sometimes the willingness, to restrain it.<sup>3</sup>

Cederman, Hug, and Krebs help explain why that arrangement was dangerous. They distinguish ordinary regime type from regime change. Democratization raises civil war risk because it opens political participation, creates new winners and losers, and encourages elites to mobilize supporters before state institutions can settle the contest. Their governmental-conflict finding is especially important for Myanmar. A territorial conflict is fought over regional autonomy or secession. A governmental conflict, or conflict over government, is fought over who controls the national state. Myanmar already had longstanding territorial conflicts involving ethnic armed organizations. The 2010 opening added a different risk: a fight over whether elected civilians or the military would control the national government. Cederman, Hug, and Krebs find that the democratization effect is strongest for these governmental conflicts, not for territorial conflicts.<sup>4</sup>

The actor preferences show why democratization increased risk instead of simply liberalizing the regime. Aung San Suu Kyi's first preference was to turn electoral legitimacy into real civilian authority. Her fallback was gradual reform under military constraints. Her worst outcome was a hollow democracy in which elections existed but coercive power remained outside civilian control. Min Aung Hlaing's preferences ran in the opposite direction. His first preference was to preserve the Tatmadaw's autonomy, security ministries, parliamentary bloc, and final veto over the state. His fallback was civilian politics as long as it did not threaten military supremacy. His worst outcome was repeated NLD victories making military dominance look temporary. The 2015 election gave Aung San Suu Kyi a governing mandate. The 2020 landslide told Min Aung Hlaing that elections were not stabilizing the military's role. They were eroding its legitimacy. Fraud claims supplied a pretext for the coup, but the deeper mechanism was Cederman's: democratization intensified a governmental conflict faster than Myanmar's institutions could contain it.<sup>5</sup>

The post-coup war confirms the theory rather than contradicting it. After February 2021, resistance did not remain only a street protest movement or a set of older ethnic wars on the periphery. The National Unity Government and People's Defence Forces claimed legitimate national authority against the junta, while ethnic armed organizations widened military pressure in multiple regions.<sup>6</sup> That pattern maps onto Cederman, Hug, and Krebs's distinction. The EAOs still often fight territorial conflicts over autonomy and control of local areas. The NUG and PDFs fight a governmental conflict over who has the right to rule Myanmar. Democratization had already mobilized citizens around elections, legitimacy, and civilian rule. When the military reversed the outcome, the mobilized opposition did not demobilize. It escalated.

The main counterargument is that Myanmar looked calmer from 2010 to 2020, so democratization must have lowered risk. That view mistakes a frozen conflict for a resolved one. The reform decade stayed relatively stable because the Tatmadaw's veto contained the struggle over government, not because the transition had made the state safe. Once NLD victories made civilian supremacy politically plausible, the military chose autocratic reversal. Once the coup erased the electoral channel, the opposition chose resistance. Myanmar became violent because democratization raised the stakes of governmental power while leaving the strongest armed actor outside democratic control.
