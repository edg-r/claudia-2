# Votes and Vetoes: Myanmar's Failed Democratic Opening

Manual-writing outline for a 750-800 word ORANGE memo.

## Core Thesis

Myanmar was at greater risk of civil war after the 2010 opening because democratization intensified competition over who controlled the state while leaving coercive power and constitutional veto authority with the military. Cederman, Hug, and Krebs predict that regime-change periods are dangerous not because democracy itself causes war, but because political mobilization expands before institutions can contain struggles over governmental power.

### 3. Theory: Cederman, Hug, and Krebs Only: 150-175 words

What to prove: The key theory is about regime change and governmental conflict, not a generic claim that democracy causes violence.

Use: Explain three moves:

1. Cederman et al. distinguish regime type from regime-change periods.
2. Democratization raises risk when political mobilization outpaces institutional capacity.
3. The democratization effect is strongest for governmental conflicts, defined in the reading as conflicts over government, rather than territorial conflicts.

Apply immediately: Myanmar matters because the post-coup conflict added a governmental-conflict layer to older territorial conflicts. The NUG/PDF struggle is about who governs the national state; many EAO struggles remain about territorial autonomy, so Cederman explains the former more cleanly than the latter.

Where this comes from in the reading: Cederman, Hug, and Krebs state in the abstract that the democratization effect is limited to governmental rather than territorial conflicts. Their Table III then replicates the model using separate dependent variables for conflicts over government and conflicts over territory, finding the democratization effect remains significant for conflicts over government but not for territorial conflicts. Use Cederman's terms: "governmental conflict," "conflict over government," and "territorial conflict." Do not substitute shorthand like "capturing the center" as the main theoretical language.

Evidence/source peg: Cederman, Hug, and Krebs, especially the abstract and Table III / p. 387 discussion on governmental-versus-territorial conflict.

### 4. Actor Preferences and Strategic Choice: 220-250 words

What to prove: Democratization increased risk because the actors' preferred outcomes became increasingly incompatible.

Use: This should be the memo's center of gravity.

Aung San Suu Kyi / NLD-civilian government: preferred elections to become civilian authority; fallback was gradual reform; worst outcome was a hollow democracy where the military retained coercive veto power.

Min Aung Hlaing / Tatmadaw-military: preferred military supremacy; fallback was civilian politics inside military-set limits; worst outcome was repeated NLD victories making Tatmadaw dominance look temporary.

Strategic-choice clarity: The 2015 election gave Suu Kyi a governing mandate. The 2020 landslide told Min Aung Hlaing that electoral politics was not stabilizing the military's role; it was eroding the political legitimacy of that role. Fraud claims were the pretext. The deeper mechanism was Cederman's: democratization intensified a governmental conflict over national authority faster than institutions could absorb it.

Evidence/source peg: NLD 2015 and 2020 victories; 2008 constitutional veto structure; Cederman et al. on mobilization and governmental conflict.
