## 2026-04-13 — Correction: France/Russia veto belief assigned to wrong war

Error: In a prior analysis, attributed Saddam's belief that France and Russia would veto UN authorization to his 1990-91 calculus before the First Gulf War.

Correction confirmed by source material (Woods/Lacey/Murray, "Saddam's Delusions," Foreign Affairs 2006): The France/Russia P5 veto shield belief is explicitly about 2002-2003 and the Second Gulf War (Operation Iraqi Freedom). Saddam thought their economic stakes in Iraq (trade and service contracts worth billions) would lead them to block US action at the Security Council.

For 1990-91 (Kuwait invasion): Saddam's reasons for discounting US intervention were (1) the Vietnam syndrome — he believed American society could not accept heavy casualties; (2) the Glaspie signal — her "no opinion on Arab-Arab conflicts" statement interpreted as US indifference; (3) a general belief that US resolve was weak. Source: Yetiv, "Persian Gulf Crisis."

Rule going forward: Always separate the two Gulf Wars when discussing Saddam's strategic miscalculations. The information problems are distinct and must not be conflated.

## 2026-04-14 — Praether-specific writing traps (Memo 1 proofread)

Three terminology errors Praether will flag, surfaced while reviewing Edgar's Memo 1 Iraq War draft. All three are mistakes Edgar was making and should be screened out of any future GPCO 410 drafts before returning them to him.

**"Miscalculation in game theory" is not a valid phrase.** Game theory is the analytical lens used to model strategic interaction, not a process one can "miscalculate in." The acceptable moves are "miscalculation within the bargaining model," "rational miscalculation about the adversary's type," or simply "information failure." Praether teaches the distinction and will mark papers that use game theory as if it were a failure mode.

**"Two-level game" (Putnam) means domestic-vs-international audience, not any dual signaling.** In the Memo 1 draft Edgar applied it to Hussein's US-vs-Iran dual signal, but both of those are external audiences. The correct term for that case is "dual-audience signaling" or "multi-audience signaling." Reserve "two-level game" for cases where an actor is simultaneously negotiating with another state and managing domestic ratification constraints. Praether teaches Putnam explicitly and will catch misuse.

**Gulf War conflation remains the single most common error in this course.** See 2026-04-13 entry above. Keep 1990-91 and 2003 strictly separated; 1990-91 runs on Fearon-style information failure logic, 2003 runs on Powell-style commitment problem logic. When writing about either war, name the year explicitly in the opening sentences and avoid carrying rhetoric from one into the other.

## 2026-04-14 — Powell 2006 terminology precision

Powell does NOT name "three types of commitment problems." His argument is explicitly unifying: one mechanism (inability to commit across a shifting power distribution) generates multiple observable phenomena. The three-part framing (preventive war / bargaining over power / first-strike advantage) that appeared in the theory-reference PDF is editorial synthesis, not Powell's own taxonomy.

How to apply: On an exam essay, say "Powell argues commitment problems are the master mechanism behind rationalist war" and then discuss preventive war, power-shifting bargains, and first-strike dynamics as illustrations of that single mechanism. Do not present them as three named categories. Presenting them as a taxonomy misrepresents Powell's unifying claim and gives up theoretical command.

Exam-winning move: Powell's payoff is showing that the mechanism holds under complete information, with perfectly divisible stakes, between fully rational actors. Stating that in an intro paragraph demonstrates theoretical command before any case application.

## 2026-04-23 — Ali feedback on Memo 2, 90/100 (Second Gulf War)

TA Abdirisak Ali graded Memo 2 90/100. Argument and theory deployment were strong; the lost points were all about under-applying the strategic choice framework. In this course the framework is not optional scaffolding — it is the analytical grammar, and Praether/Ali expect each of its five elements to be named and worked explicitly rather than implied inside a narrative.

Durable rule for every GPCO 410 memo going forward: structure the analysis around the full strategic choice framework — **actors, preferences, strategies, beliefs, payoffs** — and make each element visible on the page. Do not invoke the framework rhetorically ("applying strategic choice...") and then collapse it into a preference story. Walk the reader through all five slots for each primary actor.

Three specific underweight points Ali flagged. Screen for all three in every future memo draft:

1. **Full strategy set, including alternatives not chosen.** For each actor, lay out the plausible strategies they could have pursued, not only the one they ultimately chose. Then explain why the unchosen alternatives were rejected (dominated by payoff, ruled out by beliefs, domestically infeasible, etc.). Memo 2 only narrated the chosen path; it did not construct the choice set.

2. **Beliefs and expected payoffs for each actor's actions.** State what each actor believed about the other's likely moves, and attach an expected payoff to each strategy in the choice set given those beliefs. Beliefs are where information problems and miscalculation actually enter the model — skipping them collapses the analysis into post-hoc description.

3. **Why no settlement was reached — dedicated deep analysis.** The "was a negotiated settlement possible, what would the terms have been, why wasn't one signed" question is where the strategic choice framework pays off. Give it its own paragraph (or two). Tie the no-deal outcome back to specific framework elements: whose preferences ruled out which bargaining range, whose beliefs made which offers non-credible, which payoff structure made war preferable to the best available settlement.

Operational screen before returning any Athena memo draft: can the reader point to paragraphs (or clearly marked passages) that do each of — name actors, state preferences, enumerate strategies including rejected ones with reasons, specify beliefs, compute/compare expected payoffs, explain the settlement failure? If any of the six is implicit-only, mark it for a rewrite pass before the memo goes to Edgar.
