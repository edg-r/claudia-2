| Saxon (John George I) | ~15,000 (Britannica) | 17,350 (Cristini 2017) | ~18,000–19,000 (Wilson 2009) | 10–12 (some 10–20) |

**Where sources diverge:** Cristini (2017, p. 100) — used by the current Wikipedia ORBAT — gives the most granular figures and runs lower on the Imperial side (31,400). Guthrie (*Battles of the Thirty Years War*, 2002) and older narrative histories (Wedgwood 1938; Roberts 1958) tend to cite ~35,000 for Tilly. Wilson (*Europe's Tragedy*, 2009) is closer to Cristini's ~32,000. The ~3,000-man spread largely reflects whether one counts Pappenheim's detached cavalry regiments that arrived late in the campaign and the Catholic League camp followers armed as auxiliaries. Peter Wilson cautions that all Thirty Years' War muster figures are paper strengths — actual bayonets on the day were typically 10–15% below roll.

The artillery disparity is the one number that almost every source agrees on in the 2:1 band: the Allies fielded roughly twice Tilly's tubes. Historynet / *Strategy & Tactics* 235 gives the well-known line that during the opening cannonade Swedish guns outnumbered Catholic guns "fifty-one to twenty-seven." Cristini raises the Allied total to 66 by counting the Saxon siege pieces.

## 2. Swedish Core (Gustavus Adolphus) — ~22,800

**Administrative frame (Wikipedia ORBAT after Cristini / Mankell 1854):**
- 192 infantry companies
- 131 cavalry companies
- 54 guns

### Infantry — roughly 13,000–14,000 effectives
- Organized into the new **Swedish brigade** system (typically 3–4 squadrons per brigade, 6 ranks deep), not tercios.
- **Pike-to-musket ratio: 8:13 (about 38% pike, 62% shot).** Source: Wikipedia article analysis section, consistent with Brzezinski, *The Army of Gustavus Adolphus (1): Infantry* (Osprey MAA 235, 1991). Brzezinski notes Swedish regulations called for 72 pikes to 96 muskets per 216-man squadron, roughly the same proportions.
- Regiments at Breitenfeld included the four "colored" brigades of the center — **Yellow (Lifeguard), Blue, Green, and Red** — plus the Scots (Hepburn), Swedish native regiments (Teuffel, Axel Lillie), and German mercenary foot.

### Cavalry — roughly 7,000–8,000 sabers
- Types present:
  - **Native Swedish and Finnish horse** ("Hakkapeliitta" under Stålhandske) — light-to-medium cavalry trained to charge at a trot with pistol-then-sword, *not* the caracole. These were the shock arm on the Swedish wings.
  - **German mercenary cuirassiers** (Baudissin, Courville, others) — three-quarter armor, pistol and sword.
  - **Commanded musketeers** — small platoons of musketeers (usually ~200 men per wing) interleaved *between* cavalry squadrons. This is the Breitenfeld innovation that crushed Pappenheim's caracole.
- Right wing (Baner); left wing (Horn).

### Artillery — 54 guns (Cristini; Wikipedia ORBAT lists 12 quarter-cannon siege + 42 regimental pieces)
- **Heavy/field pieces:** 12 quarter-cannon (roughly 12-pounder equivalent) in a grand battery.
- **Regimental guns: ~42 light pieces**, predominantly the famous **3-pounder "regementsstycke"**, assigned 2 (sometimes 4) per infantry regiment. Brzezinski (Osprey *Lützen 1632*) documents the distribution scheme; Torstensson operationalized it. This is the "revolutionary" ingredient Roberts (1956, 1958) foregrounds.

## 3. Saxon Contingent (John George I) — ~17,350 (the left wing that collapsed)

**Administrative frame:** 56 infantry companies; 39 cavalry companies; 10–20 guns (most sources settle on 12 quarter-cannon siege pieces).

### Infantry — roughly 11,000–12,000
- Organized in deeper, more traditional German formations (effectively proto-tercios ~10 ranks deep), **not** the Swedish brigade system.
- Almost all the infantry regiments carried a **"Raw" / militia designation** in the Wikipedia ORBAT after Cristini — freshly raised during summer 1631 in response to Magdeburg. Guthrie emphasizes this: the Saxon foot had never stood in line against veteran tercios before 17 September.
- Pike-to-shot ratio closer to **1:1**, following older German practice, with poorer musket quality and lower rate of fire.

### Cavalry — roughly 4,500–5,500
- ~24 companies, overwhelmingly German "Reiter" in the harquebusier mold — light-armor, pistol-armed — plus a smaller cuirassier element.
- Wilson (*Europe's Tragedy*, 2009) characterizes them as undertrained; several officers had been Saxon household cavalry with no recent combat.

### Artillery — 10–12 quarter-cannon
- Lost to the Imperial right wing during Fürstenberg's exploitation of the Saxon rout. (The "7 captured Saxon quarter cannon" Wikipedia lists in the Imperial artillery park is captured Saxon tubes turned around.)

### Why the Saxon rout matters compositionally
Tilly's right — 5 cuirassier regiments under Fürstenberg + Isolani's Croats + a dragoon regiment — caught raw Saxon militia and predictably shattered them. The collapse happened in under an hour. Horn's subsequent 90-degree pivot with the Swedish left onto the now-exposed flank is the tactical move that decided the battle, and it only matters because the Saxon segment of the Allied line had completely vanished. Presentation takeaway: **the Saxon collapse was not a Swedish failure, it was the problem the Swedish brigade system was designed to solve.**

## 4. Imperial–Catholic League Army (Tilly) — 31,400 (Cristini) / ~35,000 (Guthrie)

**Breakdown after Britannica / Cristini:**
- Imperial contingent: 14,700
- Catholic League contingent: 15,700
- Irregulars: ~1,000
- Guns: 27–28

### Infantry — ~21,000 (Britannica gives the cleanest split: "21,000 infantry and 11,000 cavalry, as well as 28 heavy field guns")
- Organized in **tercios**. Most sources cite **14 large tercios**; Historynet and *Deadliest Blogger* cite **17 smaller squares** arranged in three blocks, with the center block slightly forward. The discrepancy is about whether one counts full-strength (2,000+) veteran tercios or the reduced squares Tilly actually fielded on the day. Guthrie favors 17.
- Typical tercio at Breitenfeld: ~50-man frontage × 30-man depth. Pike-to-shot ratio about **1:1 to 3:2 (more pike than shot)**, following Spanish / Walloon practice.
- Core units: Alt-Tilly, Blankart, Chiesa, Comargo, Erwitte, Gallas, Geleen, Goess, Hoch-und-Deutschmeister, Holstein, Pappenheim (foot component), Reinach, Savelli, Wangler. Mostly German Catholic and Walloon veterans, many with 5–10 years of continuous service.

### Cavalry — ~11,000
- **Left wing (Pappenheim): 7 full cuirassier regiments** — Bernstein, Merode-Waroux, Neusächsisch ("New Saxon"), Piccolomini, Rangoni, Strozzi, Trčka. Three-quarter armor, wheel-lock pistol, trained in the *caracole* (the technique Gustavus's combined musketeer-cavalry wings defeated).
- **Right wing (Fürstenberg): 5 cuirassier regiments** + **1 dragoon regiment** + **1 Croat light-horse regiment** (Isolani/Isolano). The Croats were mounted skirmishers — short carbines, sabers, irregular tactics — used against the Saxon wing.
- **Central reserve: cavalry only** behind the tercio line.

### Artillery — 27–28 guns
- Wikipedia ORBAT (after Cristini): 10 demi-cannon (siege-grade, ~24-pounder equivalent), 16 quarter-cannon (12-pounder equivalent), plus 7 captured Saxon quarter-cannon acquired the previous week at Leipzig.
- Clustered on the **Galgenberg** ridge. Immobile after emplacement — a critical vulnerability once Gustavus overran the position.
- **No regimental guns.** This is the compositional asymmetry Roberts and Parker both emphasize: Tilly's artillery was a grand battery in the Italian mode, Gustavus's artillery was a distributed fire-support system embedded in the infantry brigades.

## 5. Scholarly Disagreements to Flag in Presentation

1. **Imperial total strength: 31,400 (Cristini/Wilson) vs. ~35,000 (Guthrie/Wedgwood).** Pick one, acknowledge the other. Wilson 2009 is the safer current citation for a policy-school audience.
2. **Number of tercios: 14 (Britannica / older narrative) vs. 17 (Guthrie; *S&T* 235).** Guthrie's regimental breakdown is more granular and probably more accurate, but 14 is the number Thomas is likeliest to quote from Keegan.
3. **Swedish pike:shot ratio: 8:13 (Wikipedia/Brzezinski regulation) vs. ~1:1 in practice** (some units, per Brzezinski's own caveats). Reality was regiment-by-regiment variable.
4. **Regimental gun count: 42 (ORBAT) vs. "2 per regiment" (Roberts).** These aren't incompatible — 21 infantry units × 2 = 42 — but several regiments actually had 4 pieces.
5. **Saxon size: 15,000 (Britannica) vs. 17,350 (Cristini) vs. ~18,000–19,000 (Wilson).** The upward revision reflects recent work on Saxon muster rolls; the older ~15,000 number came from Imperial intelligence estimates.

### 8.5 Caveats

1. Breakdowns are **derived**, not directly enumerated in any single source. Cristini 2017 gives the most granular company-level ORBAT but does not publish per-arm headcounts in the form above.
2. The Swedish 38%/62% pike:shot split is the 1620 regulation; actual regiments at Breitenfeld drifted toward 1:1 as combat attrition favored musketeers. If asked in Q&A, acknowledge ~±500 drift on each infantry number.
3. Imperial cavalry regimental strengths of "~500" are a rounded convention. Guthrie's narrower ~450 is probably closer to the day's effectives.
4. "Commanded musketeers" are counted inside the Swedish infantry total, not added on top. Detaching them does not change the 22,800 side total.
