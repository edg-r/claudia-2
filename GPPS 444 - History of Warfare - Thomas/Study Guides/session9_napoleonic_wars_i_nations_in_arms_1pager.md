## Bottom Line

Lynn's chapter argues that the American and French Revolutions transformed warfare from a dynastic contest among rulers into a national struggle among mobilized peoples. Napoleon inherited that revolutionary military system and refined it through conscription, meritocratic command, corps organization, flexible logistics, artillery, and operational maneuver. Session 9's two 1805 battles show the two halves of the Napoleonic problem: Napoleon could destroy continental armies at Austerlitz, but Britain could deny him command of the sea at Trafalgar and then fund coalitions until France was exhausted.

## What Changed by 1805

The core shift is political before it is tactical. Revolutionary France created "nations in arms": citizens who saw state wars as their own wars, not merely a king's quarrel (Lynn, 2005). That gave France a scale of manpower and motivation unavailable to most old-regime armies. The Jourdan Law of 1798 regularized conscription and supplied more than two million soldiers to Napoleon's armies by 1815. This mass mattered, but it only became decisive when paired with a system that could move, command, feed, and concentrate it.

Napoleon's battlefield method combined several tools. He used flexible mixed-order tactics, expanded artillery, revived French cavalry, and built the corps system: semi-independent formations of infantry, cavalry, and artillery that could march separately, forage across wider zones, and concentrate for battle. This solved a practical command-and-control problem created by army size. His preferred operational move was the *manoeuvre sur les derrieres*: fix the enemy with part of the army, send another force against the flank or rear, cut the line of retreat, then turn defeat into annihilation.

Logistics remained both enabler and trap. French armies could move rapidly because they were willing to live off the country, but foraging worked best in rich terrain and during movement. It failed when armies paused too long or moved through poor, exhausted, or hostile regions. That limitation would later help destroy Napoleon in Russia, but in 1805 it gave the Grande Armee tempo.

## Austerlitz: Continental Masterpiece

In 1805, Napoleon shifted about 210,000 troops from the Channel coast toward the Rhine after Austria and Russia joined Britain in the Third Coalition. He neutralized the Austrian position at Ulm, occupied Vienna, and then engineered the conditions for battle at Austerlitz. Lynn presents Austerlitz as Napoleon's masterpiece because he manipulated enemy perception: he made his right flank look weak, induced the Austro-Russian army under Kutuzov to attack it, then struck the exposed allied center on the Pratzen Heights. The result was not merely a French battlefield victory. It broke the Third Coalition and helped end the Holy Roman Empire in 1806.

For Thomas's framework, Austerlitz is the cleanest Session 9 case for operational leadership, mobility, adaptability, and mass. Napoleon did not win by brute numbers alone. He won by using organization and tempo to create local superiority at the decisive point.

## Trafalgar: Why Napoleon Could Not Finish Britain

The chapter's naval section, "The Nelson Touch," explains why Napoleon's continental dominance had a strategic ceiling. Britain had maintained pressure at sea since the French Revolutionary Wars, and Nelson's victories at the Nile and Trafalgar made French blue-water challenge impossible. At Trafalgar on October 21, 1805, Nelson rejected cautious line-ahead orthodoxy and drove columns into the Franco-Spanish line. The point was to create a melee where British seamanship, gunnery, and captain-level initiative could isolate enemy ships. Britain captured seventeen enemy ships and sank one, though Nelson was mortally wounded.

Trafalgar mattered because it preserved British command of the sea. That meant Napoleon could not invade Britain, while Britain could protect trade, seize colonies, conduct amphibious operations, and subsidize continental allies. Lynn's strategic punchline is that British finance and naval supremacy let London keep reconstituting coalitions, while Napoleon's empire relied on coercion, plunder, and continental compliance.
