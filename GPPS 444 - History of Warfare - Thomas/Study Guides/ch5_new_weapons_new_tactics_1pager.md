# TCHW Part Two, Ch. 5 — "New Weapons, New Tactics, 1300-1500"

**Book:** Geoffrey Parker (ed.), *The Cambridge History of Warfare*, 2nd ed. (Cambridge UP, 2005/2020). Chapter by **Christopher Allmand**.
**Session:** GPPS 444, Session 6 — Medieval Warfare II (Wed Apr 15). Pairs with the Agincourt case.

## BLUF
Between 1300 and 1500, disciplined infantry armed with cheap, mass-produced missile and pole weapons (longbow, pike, halberd, early gunpowder) displaced the armored knight as the decisive arm of Western war, pulling command, recruiting, and finance toward paid, contractual, and eventually standing armies.

## Period and Course Arc
Chapter 5 covers the late medieval transition from the world Bachrach described in Ch. 4 (Roman ramparts, castles, heavy cavalry) to the world Parker opens in Ch. 6 (the Gunpowder Revolution). It is the hinge between the Age of Stone Fortifications and the Age of Guns and Sails, and the intellectual setup for Thomas's Session 6 on Agincourt and the "stirrup thesis" in decline.

## Key Weapons Introduced
- **Longbow** — roughly ten arrows a minute, penetrates mail; cheap, "democratic," devastating when massed (Crécy 1346, Poitiers 1356, Agincourt 1415).
- **Crossbow** — slower (about two bolts a minute) but accessible to untrained townsmen and condemned by the Church for precisely that reason.
- **Pike, halberd, spear, club, axe** — cheap pole arms that let Scottish, Flemish, and Swiss commoners break armored cavalry (Bannockburn 1314, Morgarten 1315, Dithmarschen 1319).
- **Plate armor** — reinforced mail from c. 1250 as a response to the bow; raised the cost of cavalry and made it less flexible.
- **Early gunpowder artillery** — mortars and bombards for siege; wagon-mounted field guns with the Hussites under Zizka (Kutna Hora 1421); two-wheeled trunnion carriages only in the mid-fifteenth century.
- **Handguns** — integrated into Swiss and Burgundian formations by the 1470s alongside pikemen.

## Tactical and Operational Implications
- **Offense-defense flip on the battlefield.** Pike squares and massed longbow fire made the mounted charge a losing proposition against prepared infantry on chosen ground. The defense, if it had the right weapons and discipline, beat the offense.
- **Siege regained primacy off the battlefield.** Because raids alone (the chevauchée) could not force surrender, Henry V returned to formal sieges from 1415 onward (Harfleur, Falaise, Cherbourg, Rouen). Gunpowder began eroding the stone fortress by 1450 but had not yet won.
- **Mass.** Infantry-to-cavalry ratios moved from 2:1 under Edward I to 10:1 under English armies in the 1440s. The Hussite army ran 19:1. Armies got bigger and cheaper per fighter.
- **Adaptability and operational leadership.** Edward III combined dismounted men-at-arms with longbowmen; Zizka invented the wagenburg and field artillery; Charles the Bold built combined-arms formations (15 percent heavy horse, 50 percent mounted archers, 15 percent pike, 10 percent handgun, 10 percent foot archers).
- **Logistics and finance.** The indenture contract (England), muster-and-review discipline, and eventually Charles VII's **Compagnies d'Ordonnance** (1445) created paid, inspectable, semi-permanent forces. Spain mobilized up to 80,000 men for ten campaigns to take Granada (1492). Warfare became a fiscal problem, not a feudal obligation.
- **Discipline.** Allmand stresses that the new tactics only worked because training, drill, and contractual accountability replaced aristocratic honor as the binding force on the line.

## What to Watch for in Class and on the Quiz
- **"New tactics" precedes "new weapons."** The Scots at Bannockburn and the Swiss at Morgarten beat knights with pikes and terrain before the longbow or gunpowder decided anything. Thomas will likely press that the tactical idea (disciplined massed infantry) is the driver; the technology is the accelerant.
- **Agincourt (1415) as exemplar.** Expect a question that ties Allmand's argument to the battle: mud, stakes, dismounted men-at-arms fighting alongside longbowmen, French cavalry wrecked on prepared ground.
- **The stirrup thesis in reverse.** If the stirrup gave the knight his age (Lynn White), the pike and the bow took it away. Be ready to defend or qualify that symmetry.
