# The Chevauchée: A Field Guide

## 1. Definition and Etymology

*Chevauchée* is Old French for "a ride" or "a horse charge," from *chevaucher*, to ride. In the military vocabulary of the late Middle Ages, the word hardened into a term of art for a specific operational form: a fast-moving mounted raid driven deep into hostile territory for the purpose of burning, looting, and ruining the enemy's economic and political base. The term begins appearing in French and Anglo-Norman chronicles during the thirteenth century to describe routine border raiding, but it acquires its full strategic weight in the 1340s, when Edward III of England industrialized the practice into a theater-level operational doctrine against the Valois crown. By the fifteenth century any contemporary chronicler using the word would have understood it to mean a deliberate strategy of devastation, not merely a raid.

## 2. Historical Context

The chevauchée is inseparable from the Hundred Years' War (1337-1453), though it drew on older practice. Cross-border devastation raiding had been routine in Anglo-Scottish warfare, in the Iberian *Reconquista*, and in the endemic feuding of Capetian France. What changed under Edward III was scale, coordination, and strategic intent.

Three campaigns anchor the canon:

- **Edward III, 1346 (the Crécy campaign).** Edward landed at Saint-Vaast-la-Hougue in Normandy in July with roughly 14,000 men, burned Caen, swept east across the Seine, and laid waste to the countryside north of Paris. Philip VI pursued, caught Edward at Crécy on 26 August, and was destroyed by English longbowmen in a set-piece battle the French had been provoked into. The chevauchée set the trap; the longbow sprung it.
- **The Black Prince, 1355 (the Grand Chevauchée through Languedoc).** Edward of Woodstock, Prince of Wales, launched from Bordeaux in October with roughly 6,000 men and rode 675 miles in eight weeks, sacking Carcassonne and Narbonne and burning more than 500 settlements. He fought no major battle. The operation was pure economic and political warfare, designed to humiliate Jean II and shatter the tax base of southern France.
- **The Black Prince, 1356 (the Poitiers campaign).** A second chevauchée north from Aquitaine provoked Jean II into pursuit and an unwise battle at Poitiers on 19 September, where the French king was captured. Henry V's 1415 Agincourt campaign followed the same template: land, ravage, draw the French army onto ground of English choosing.

## 3. Mechanics

A mature chevauchée was a disciplined operational instrument, not a rabble. Typical force composition ran 4,000 to 10,000 men, overwhelmingly mounted for mobility even when the men-at-arms intended to dismount for battle. The core elements were mounted men-at-arms (the heavy shock component and command spine), mounted archers (English longbowmen on ponies, the force multiplier), light horse and hobilars for screening and scouting, and a small logistics tail of wagons, farriers, and camp servants kept deliberately thin.

Daily tempo ran 15 to 25 miles on flat ground, with the column dispersed across a frontage of 15 to 20 miles via three parallel battles (van, main, rear) to maximize the area being burned and foraged. The force destroyed crops in the field, granaries, mills, wine presses, orchards, villages, and undefended market towns. It killed livestock it could not drive off, seized coin and portable wealth, and took high-value prisoners for ransom. It deliberately avoided what it could not quickly reduce: stone castles, fortified cities, and any serious standing army. Sieges were selective and short. When a position would not fall in days, the column moved on. The point was never to hold ground.

## 4. Strategic Logic

The question that matters for a history-of-warfare course is why. Why ride 700 miles burning barns instead of besieging Paris or seeking a single decisive battle? Four interlocking reasons.

**(a) Economic warfare.** The Valois monarchy funded itself through extraordinary taxation (the *taille*, the *gabelle*, hearth taxes) levied on the productive peasantry and the towns. Burn the harvest, destroy the mills, depopulate the villages, and the tax base evaporates. A chevauchée through Languedoc or the Île-de-France could cost Paris a year's revenue from the affected region. Medieval states had shallow fiscal reserves; the damage compounded.

**(b) Political warfare.** The feudal contract obligated a lord to protect his vassals. A king who could not prevent English columns from burning his peasants' fields in broad daylight was visibly failing the basic bargain of medieval kingship. The chevauchée was designed to delegitimize the Valois crown in the eyes of its own nobility and towns, and to pressure regional lords toward separate peace, neutrality, or defection to the English party. Gascony, Brittany, and Normandy all saw this dynamic.

**(c) Self-sustaining logistics.** Medieval armies were logistics-constrained. A chevauchée solved its own supply problem by eating the countryside it was destroying. The force carried weeks of fodder and food on the hoof, then replenished from seized stores. This kept the operational tether short and the force fast.

**(d) Provocation.** A king who allowed systematic devastation without response lost legitimacy faster than one who lost a battle. The chevauchée was a standing dare. Philip VI took it at Crécy. Jean II took it at Poitiers. In both cases the French king pursued a smaller, rested, defensively arrayed English force onto ground the English had chosen. Battle was the secondary effect. The raid was the primary instrument.

## 5. Effectiveness and Limits

At peak efficiency (roughly 1346-1360), the chevauchée produced the most favorable strategic results England would achieve in the entire war: the capture of a French king, the Treaty of Brétigny (1360), and temporary English sovereignty over a third of France. It broke the Valois fiscal system and the military reputation of the French nobility.

It could not, however, take or hold terrain. It could not reduce major fortifications. It could not convert battlefield victories into lasting political settlement, because the devastation that produced the victory also destroyed the very tax base and local cooperation any English administration would have needed to govern. And it generated its own antibodies. Under Charles V and his constable Bertrand du Guesclin (1370s), France adopted what the course text will probably call a Fabian response: refuse battle, shadow the English columns, shelter the population in walled towns, retake castles patiently, and let the chevauchée exhaust itself against empty countryside. Combined with the gradual professionalization of the French royal army (the *compagnies d'ordonnance* of 1445, the first standing force in Western Europe since Rome), hardened urban fortification, and improved field artillery, the chevauchée lost its edge. By the closing phase of the war it was no longer decisive.

## 6. Theoretical Significance in a History-of-Warfare Course

Given Thomas's five themes (mass, mobility, adaptability, logistics, operational leadership) and the offense-defense tension he flags throughout, three framings are most likely to appear on a quiz or essay.

**First, the chevauchée as an early case of attritional and economic warfare against a political center of gravity.** It is Clausewitz before Clausewitz, targeting not the enemy army but the enemy state's capacity to field one. This is the frame that connects it forward to Sherman, strategic bombing, and modern sanctions.

**Second, the siege-versus-raid debate, a proxy for the offense-defense balance.** Medieval warfare is often taught as the age of the castle and the defensive dominant. The chevauchée is the mounted offensive answer to stone fortification: accept that you cannot reduce the castles, and ruin everything around them until the castles become irrelevant. Expect Thomas to frame it as a mobility-led workaround to a defense-dominant technological environment.

**Third, the chevauchée as the clearest medieval case of the civilian-combatant tension.** Chivalric norms notionally protected noncombatants. The chevauchée deliberately and systematically attacked them, because the peasantry was the tax base and the tax base was the target. This is useful for the course's later arc on the demodernization thesis and the ethics of coercion.

A fourth, lighter framing worth keeping in your pocket: operational leadership. Edward III and the Black Prince ran these campaigns with a coordination and discipline their French counterparts could not match, which is a clean example of Thomas's leadership variable driving outcomes independent of mass.

## 7. Modern Analogues and Doctrinal Echoes

The operational logic reappears wherever a belligerent concludes that the enemy's will or economic base is a softer target than the enemy's army. Sherman's 1864 March to the Sea was a chevauchée in all but name: a mobile column cutting loose from its supply line, living off the country, and destroying the economic substrate of Confederate resistance (Sherman had read his medieval history). The Allied strategic bombing campaigns of 1942-1945 extended the logic to industrial economies via air power, with the same ambiguous results on enemy morale. Thomas Schelling's *Arms and Influence* formalized the underlying theory as coercion by punishment, distinct from denial. Contemporary economic sanctions regimes (Iran, Russia) are the diplomatic descendant: impose economic pain on a population to pressure a regime, accepting that the target state can absorb the pain longer than the sanctioning coalition often expects. The chevauchée's historical record, strong short-term effect, limited long-term political conversion, is a useful cautionary datum for all of its descendants.
