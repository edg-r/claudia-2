## BLUF
Between 1494 and 1660 European warfare shifted from pike-square shock battles to linear musket-and-artillery firepower, forced by the "military revolution" of fortresses, firearms, and larger armies. Gustavus Adolphus's Swedish system (volley fire in six ranks, standardized field guns, disciplined cavalry charges) demonstrated the new model at Breitenfeld 1631 and became the template every western army copied.

## Context & Scope
- European dynastic conflicts, c. 1494 Italian Wars to 1660; focus on Spain, France, Dutch Republic, Sweden, Holy Roman Empire; Habsburg–Ottoman border referenced but not the chapter's main lens (p. 148, 164–65).
- Five interlocking drivers: army growth, fortress-centric strategy, firearms, religious polarization, fiscal strain.

## Key Evidence / Claims
- Armies exploded in size: Charles the Bold's Netherlands force ~15,000 (1470s) → Philip II's 86,000 (1570s) → French wartime 125,000 (1639); 10–12 million Europeans became soldiers in the 17th c. (pp. 148–50).
- Sieges dominated over battles; mercenaries (Swiss, German entrepreneurs like Wallenstein, 25,000 men) bridged the gap before permanent regiments (Spanish *tercios*, 1534) professionalized forces (pp. 150–51).
- Musket share rose from 2:5 shot-to-pike (1571) to 3:1 (1601); Maurice of Nassau's countermarch (1594, from Aelian's *Tactics*) enabled continuous volley fire — the tactical unlock (pp. 156–58).
- Breitenfeld (17 Sep 1631): Sweden's 28,000 foot / 13,000 horse / 51 heavy guns + regimental 3-pounders defeated Tilly's 31,400 in deep squares with 27 guns; ~8,000 Imperial dead in the second hour, two-thirds of the Imperial army lost. Decided by firepower + discipline, not mass (p. 159).
- Logistics as the real constraint: a 30,000-man army needed 20 tons of bread + 30,000 lbs of meat daily; moving ten siege guns required 1,849 pairs of oxen. Garrison drag kept field armies small even when paper strength hit 183,000 (pp. 160–61).
- Ottoman coverage is thin in this chapter — Habsburgs and Ottomans "at war two years out of every three" in the 17th c. (p. 148); 1683 Vienna and 1687 Mohacs referenced as endpoints of Ottoman decline (pp. 164–65). For Janissaries + 1526 Mohacs + 1529 Vienna, supplement with lecture.

## Implications for Thomas's Framework
- **Mass + firepower + drill** is the period's synthesis: the western way of war consolidates around paid, drilled, standardized infantry — a direct answer to Thomas's five themes (mass, mobility, adaptability, logistics, leadership).
- **Offense vs. defense tension**: trace italienne fortresses made defense dominant; volley fire and field artillery at Breitenfeld temporarily restored offensive decisiveness — but Lützen (1632) and Nördlingen (1634) show the advantage was copyable and short-lived.

## Limits / Counterpoints
- Chapter is Eurocentric and under-treats Ottoman military institutions (Janissaries, devshirme, siege artillery at Constantinople 1453); the syllabus session title "Ottoman Expansion" is only partially served by this reading — Mohacs 1526 and Vienna 1529 are not covered in depth here.
