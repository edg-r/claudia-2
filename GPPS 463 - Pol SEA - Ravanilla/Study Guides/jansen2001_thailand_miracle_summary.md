# Thailand: The Making of a Miracle? (2001) — Karel Jansen

**Source/Link:** *Development and Change* 32(3): 343–370. Institute of Social Studies / Blackwell; N/A
**Context for use:** GPPS 463, W4 — Asian Miracle (Wed Apr 22 lecture, post-Midterm 1)

## BLUF
Thailand's 6.6% average annual GDP growth from 1950–2000 was driven primarily by rapid factor accumulation (capital, labor, land) rather than productivity gains, coordinated through a pragmatic pro-business accommodation between a dynamic Chinese-origin entrepreneurial class and a small, non-interventionist state (pp. 343, 366). The policy takeaway: growth rested on conditions (frontier land, demographic transition, low-wage Chinese commercial networks, business-friendly macroeconomic stability) that are now largely exhausted, so future Thai growth will be slower and more contested.

## Context & Scope
- Fifty-year review (1950–2000) of Thai economic growth, placed in comparative East Asian perspective; written in the aftermath of the 1997 Asian Financial Crisis to ask whether the "miracle" was structural or episodic (pp. 343–345).
- Thailand is unique in the group as the only non-colonized SEA economy, yet constrained by nineteenth-century unequal treaties that locked in low tariffs and a small state (p. 354).

## Key Evidence / Claims
- Growth accounting: capital accumulation is the largest contributor; TFP growth contributes ~1.2–2.0 pp/yr across five studies (Table 2, p. 348), higher than Latin America (0.2), South Asia (0.8), and Africa (−0.6) per Collins and Bosworth (p. 347).
- Structural shifts: agriculture fell from ~50% to ~10% of GDP (1950s–1990s) but still ~60% of employment; female labor force participation >70%, among Asia's highest (pp. 349–350). Cultivated area rose from 800,000 ha (1850) to ~20 million ha (1990s) — extensive land-based growth (pp. 351).
- State–business coalition: post-1958 military rule gave Chinese business a "free hand" in exchange for macro stability, cheap labor, and low food prices; political shift after 1988 moved business interests directly into parliamentary politics ("authoritarian institutionalization," Pei 1998) (pp. 354–355).
- Distinctive features vs. other East Asian "miracles": Thai state was among the *least* interventionist (gov't spending only 16.4% of GDP, 1970–97), with no industrial policy comparable to Korea/Taiwan; growth relied on openness, FDI, and export orientation rather than state-directed credit (p. 356).

## Implications for Policy/Practice
- For development policy: Thailand shows that a weak, non-interventionist state can still produce rapid growth *if* a commercially networked entrepreneurial class, macroeconomic stability, and openness are present — complicating the developmental-state (Korea/Taiwan/Singapore) template.
- For Thailand forward: land frontier closed, demographic dividend ending, secondary/tertiary schooling still lags — without R&D capacity and human capital upgrading, Thailand is poorly positioned for the IT-driven growth wave (p. 367).

## Limits / Counterpoints
- Growth was built on "submission and repression" of farmers and workers (weak unions, poorly enforced minimum wage, 1976 bloodbath); distributional costs are under-weighted in the "miracle" framing (pp. 355–356, 367). Krugman (1994) counter-view — that factor-accumulation-driven growth is unsustainable — is acknowledged but Jansen partially rebuts via Thailand's above-average TFP.
