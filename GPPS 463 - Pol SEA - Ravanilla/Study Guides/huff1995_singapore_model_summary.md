# Huff (1995), "What Is the Singapore Model of Economic Development?"

**Class:** GPPS 463, Lecture Day 9, Monday, April 27, 2026
**Topic:** How did Singapore do it?
**Required reading:** W. G. Huff (1995)

## Core Argument

Huff argues that Singapore's "model" was neither laissez-faire nor centrally planned socialism. It was a highly interventionist, market-facing system in which the PAP state replaced the price mechanism in selected areas, especially wages, savings, infrastructure, and strategic services, so that export capitalism could work on Singapore's terms (Huff, 1995, pp. 735-736, 753-755).

## Mechanism

- **Wage and labor control made export manufacturing viable.** Singapore entered the 1960s with wages too high for world markets, then used labor laws and the National Wages Council to weaken union bargaining, stabilize wages, and keep MNE production profitable. Unemployment fell from 8.9% in 1966 to 4.5% in 1973 (Huff, 1995, pp. 740-741).
- **Foreign firms substituted for missing domestic entrepreneurs.** By 1992, wholly or majority foreign-owned firms produced about three-quarters of manufacturing output and value added, and 84.5% of direct manufactured exports. Singapore's industrial success was therefore deeply dependent on MNEs (Huff, 1995, pp. 741-742).
- **Forced saving financed public capacity.** The Central Provident Fund and public-sector surpluses pushed the savings ratio from under 10% for much of the 1960s to more than 40% after 1980. The state then used these resources for infrastructure, housing, and reserves rather than mass consumption (Huff, 1995, pp. 738, 744-746).
- **Infrastructure crowded in private investment.** Ports, airports, telecoms, roads, industrial estates, English-language education, and training reduced firms' operating and "distance" costs. Huff treats this as public investment that complemented, rather than displaced, private capital (Huff, 1995, pp. 746-747, 754).
- **Services mattered as much as manufacturing.** Singapore's location and activist policy made it a major financial center. By 1992, financial and business services provided 26.1% of GDP, nearly as much as manufacturing (Huff, 1995, pp. 739, 750-752).

## How to Use This in Class

For Ravanilla's course arc, Singapore is the clearest case against a simple "free markets caused the Asian miracle" story. The state was strong, but not in the same way as Korea or Taiwan: it did not mainly protect national firms and pick domestic champions. It made Singapore attractive to global capital by disciplining labor, forcing savings, building world-class infrastructure, and managing a small open economy around international prices.

The limit case is also important. Huff says Singapore is hard to copy because it is a city-state with unusual location advantages, a high foreign economic presence, and unusually deep PAP control over society (Huff, 1995, p. 753). The exam-ready answer is therefore: Singapore "did it" through disciplined state capitalism, but its success was built on conditions that are not easily portable.
