## Card 1 — Hayton (2014), Ch. 1: Wrecks and Wrongs

### Front
- Hayton, B. (2014). "Wrecks and Wrongs: Prehistory to 1500." *South China Sea*, Ch. 1.
- Thesis: SEA produced an indigenous maritime civilisation; Indic forms were *adopted*, not imposed.
- Key terms: Nusantao, mandala, cakravartin, entrepôt, monsoon (mawsim), tributary relation.
- Core argument:
  - Reject diffusionist myth (civ from China/India) and Chinese ownership myth of S. China Sea.
  - Nusantao = multi-ethnic seagoing trading network pre-dating Indic/Sinic political forms.
  - SEA rulers selectively borrowed Hindu-Buddhist kingship for state-building.
  - Mandala = borderless ritual-centric polity; flexible but doesn't consolidate.
  - Tribute = trading protocol, not vassalage.

### Back
- Evidence/cases:
  - Funan (1st–6th c.): Isthmus of Kra portage, Indianised, sent embassies to China.
  - Srivijaya (7th–12th c., Palembang): 20,000 armed men by 683 CE; gatekeeper to Nanyang; Chola raid 1025.
  - Champa: Hindu brick towers; rotating central ruler across river valleys.
  - Belitung shipwreck 826 CE: 55,000 Chinese ceramics on Arab dhow.
  - Zheng He (1405–1433): coercive gunboat diplomacy per Wade; Ming then burned maps.
- Limits: Popular-academic, not peer-reviewed; archaeology synthesised from others.
- Ravanilla hook: Anchors the Sinic vs. Indic divide that Dell-Lane-Querubin operationalises; supplies mandala vocabulary required for "why Europe won" SAQs.

## Card 2 — Dell, Lane, Querubin (2018): Historical State, Vietnam

### Front
- Dell, M., Lane, N., Querubin, P. (2018). "The Historical State, Local Collective Action, and Economic Development in Vietnam." *Econometrica* 86(6).
- Thesis: Sinic-style village governance built civic-capital norms that persist 300+ years and crowd *in* civil society.
- Key terms: Sinic vs. Indic states, institutionalised village governance, RD design, crowd-in vs. crowd-out, civic capital.
- Core argument:
  - Dai Viet ran Chinese-style centralised bureaucracy w/ village as administrative unit.
  - Khmer periphery ran patron-client mandala; no village layer.
  - Quotas forced villages to elect councils, register pop., redistribute land.
  - Norms persisted after formal institutions razed (French, RVN, SRV).
  - Flips Putnam/Fukuyama: right kind of strong state crowds in.

### Back
- Evidence/cases:
  - 1698 Gia Dinh boundary RD: Dai Viet side ~33% higher household consumption today.
  - 25 km bandwidth; balance on elevation, soil, rainfall.
  - Dai Viet villagers ~2x more in civic orgs; +1 yr schooling; higher local tax collection.
  - 1461 village elections by male suffrage (centuries before EU equivalents).
  - Hamlet Evaluation System (1969–73) confirms norm channel.
  - Provincial-level effects = 0; rules out higher-tier govt.
- Limits: One-country RD; mechanism inferred from proxies; doesn't prove norms vs. unobserved variables.
- Ravanilla hook: Empirical backbone for Sinic-Indic divergence story; sets up Stubbs strong-state contrast.

## Card 3 — Diamond (1997), Prologue: Yali's Question

### Front
- Diamond, J. (1997). "Prologue: Yali's Question." *Guns, Germs, and Steel*, pp. 13–32.
- Thesis: Intercontinental wealth gap is ultimately biogeographic, not racial or cultural.
- Key terms: proximate vs. ultimate causes, Yali's question, east-west vs. north-south axes, domesticable species, guns/germs/steel.
- Core argument:
  - Eurasia had rich starter kit: domesticable grasses + 13 of 14 large mammals.
  - East-west axis allowed crops/tech to diffuse across same climate band.
  - Surplus → density → specialists → writing → states → crowd diseases.
  - Rules out racial-cognitive explanations (false + noxious).
  - Rules out culture as ultimate (culture itself needs explaining).

### Back
- Evidence/cases:
  - Wheat spread Fertile Crescent → EU/China fast; maize Mexico → US slow.
  - 200,000 wild plants, only ~hundreds domesticated; 148 large mammals, only 14.
  - Pizarro's steel + horses + smallpox at Cajamarca = proximate.
  - Apply to SEA: rice domesticated Yangtze ~8500 ya, "Out of Taiwan" radiation; Portuguese 1511 Melaka, VOC 1600 Moluccas bring Eurasian guns/steel package.
- Limits/counters:
  - Geographic determinism too coarse below continental-millennial scale.
  - Can't explain within-Eurasia variation (England vs. China) or within-SEA (Dai Viet vs. Khmer).
  - Sample SAQ commentary: Diamond standalone = B/B+; must place inside institutional story.
- Ravanilla hook: Use as *amplifier* of European institutional capacity, not substitute. The "why Europe won" SAQ.

## Card 4 — Acemoglu & Robinson (2012), Ch. 9: Reversing Development

### Front
- Acemoglu, D., & Robinson, J. (2012). "Reversing Development." *Why Nations Fail*, Ch. 9.
- Thesis: VOC didn't slow SEA development; it actively *reversed* an ongoing commercialisation trajectory.
- Key terms: extractive institutions, inclusive institutions, reversal of development, monopoly rent-seeking, autarky response.
- Core argument:
  - Pre-1600 SEA city-states (Aceh, Banten, Melaka, Makassar) commercialising fast.
  - VOC seized Moluccan spice trade via coercion + monopoly treaties.
  - Cut world spice supply ~60% to extract rents.
  - Neighboring states retreated from trade defensively → de-urbanisation.
  - Counterfactual: SEA might have followed EU path to inclusive institutions absent Dutch aggression.

### Back
- Evidence/cases:
  - 1600 Ambon clove monopoly treaty.
  - 1621 Banda genocide: Coen massacred ~15,000; islands divided into 68 Dutch-owned parcels worked by slaves.
  - Banten cut down pepper trees 1620 (autarky).
  - Burma moved capital Pegu → inland Ava 1635.
  - Maguindanao raja destroyed spice trees (1686, 1699).
  - Dutch ~doubled nutmeg price.
- Limits: Counterfactual unprovable; absolutist pre-VOC states weren't truly "inclusive"; book-length argument compressed.
- Ravanilla hook: Pair with Dell-Olken for the "two Dutch colonialisms" comparison — single most likely cross-reading SAQ. Use "reversal of development" by name.

## Card 5 — Dell & Olken (2020): Dutch Cultivation System in Java

### Front
- Dell, M., & Olken, B. (2020). "The Development Effects of the Extractive Colonial Economy: The Dutch Cultivation System in Java." *RES* 87(1).
- Thesis: Extractive colonialism can produce *positive* long-run effects when extraction requires on-site manufacturing and infrastructure.
- Key terms: Cultuurstelsel, catchment area, input-output linkages, counterfactual factory analysis, spatial RD.
- Core argument:
  - 1830s–1870s forced sugarcane cultivation in Java; 94 Dutch-owned factories; ~1/3 of Dutch govt revenue.
  - Sugar cane spoils → processing must be ≤4–7 km from field → forced local industry.
  - Areas near factories today: more industrial, more educated, ~10% richer per capita.
  - Channels: agglomeration, infrastructure (roads/rail), human capital via village schools.
  - "Extractive" insufficient descriptor; *form* of extraction matters.

### Back
- Evidence/cases:
  - Counterfactual: 1,000 random feasible factory configs; p = 0.001.
  - Spatial RD on catchment-area boundaries.
  - Pasuruan/Kediri/Surabaya sugar belt → Indonesia's densest food processing today.
  - Java had Asia's densest road/rail network early 20th c.
  - 10–15% more communal land (1980, 2003); +education from 1920s cohort.
  - Contrast: Peruvian *mita*, Congolese rubber = raw extraction → negative persistence.
- Limits: One-region; effect could reflect Dutch site-selection on unobservables; doesn't justify extraction normatively.
- Ravanilla hook: Other half of "two Dutch colonialisms" SAQ. Rent vs. output extraction is the key distinction.

## Card 6 — Stubbs (1999): War and Economic Development

### Front
- Stubbs, R. (1999). "War and Economic Development: Export-Oriented Industrialization in East and Southeast Asia." *Comparative Politics* 31(3).
- Thesis: The 1941–1989 war sequence built the strong states, capital flows, and export markets that made the East/SEA miracle possible.
- Key terms: EOI, ISI, destructive/formative/redistributive effects of war, strong state/weak society, Cold War capital injection, war-induced mercantilism.
- Core argument:
  - WWII + Japanese occupation destroyed old landed elites/patronage.
  - Cold War threat justified labour suppression, central state-building.
  - U.S. procurement + aid injected capital at scale.
  - Wars (esp. Vietnam) opened U.S. consumer market for allied exports.
  - Inserts war into the neoclassical-vs-statist miracle debate.

### Back
- Evidence/cases:
  - Japan: Korean War procurement $600M (1951), $850M (1952) = 63% of exports; "gift of the Gods."
  - South Korea: $4.2B U.S. aid 1953–69; ~80% of fixed capital formation; Park's 1961 coup → autonomous state.
  - Taiwan: $1.7B aid 1949–mid-60s; KMT detached from Taiwanese society.
  - Malaya: Korean War commodity boom funded Emergency counterinsurgency.
  - Singapore 1965: PAP used dual communist threat for control.
  - Negative: Philippines — landed elite never dislodged; weak predatory state; stuck in ISI.
- Limits: Hard to isolate war from other variables; doesn't fully explain post-1990 outcomes.
- Ravanilla hook: W3 close. Pair with colonial-legacy readings to argue war reshaped the inheritance Acemoglu/Dell-Olken describe. Philippine negative case is gold.
