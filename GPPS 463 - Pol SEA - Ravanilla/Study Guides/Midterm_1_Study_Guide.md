## How the Midterm Asks Questions: The SAQ Pattern

The only sample assessment material in the course folder is `Midterm 1/Sample Short Essay Responses.pdf`. That document contains two model answers to the prompt "Why Did Europe Win the Game of Colonization?" along with a graded commentary explaining why the A/A+ answer beats the B/B+ one. Reading it carefully tells you more about what Ravanilla wants than any abstract rubric could.

The pattern that emerges is this. The question is thematic and ties to one of the week's central lecture questions. The expected answer is not a chronology and not a reading recap. It is a causal political-economy story with four moving parts stacked in sequence. First, name the outcome to be explained. Second, identify a causal mechanism in institutional or political-economy terms, not just factual detail. Third, use a comparison against at least one Southeast Asian case to do analytical work, not as background color. Fourth, deploy named course concepts (mandala, patron-client, extractive institutions, path dependence, crowd-in, strong state, catchment area) as the vocabulary that holds the argument together. Technology, geography, and biogeography should be placed inside the institutional story as amplifiers, not used as standalone explanations.

The graded commentary on the sample answer is explicit about what loses points. Chronological narrative without mechanism language loses. Europe-only or Southeast-Asia-only answers lose; comparison is required even when the prompt names only one region. Diamond-style geography on its own loses because it is treated as proximate rather than ultimate, and because it fails to engage with institutional variables the course emphasises. Restating the readings without "doing analytical work" loses. The A/A+ answer does not just mention mandala and feudalism; it specifies the mechanism by which each political form shaped subsequent state capacity and commercial outcomes.

Practical working template for a midterm short essay:
- Open with a one-sentence causal claim that answers the prompt directly. No windup.
- Unpack the mechanism in two or three steps: condition X produced institution Y, which produced outcome Z.
- Bring in a named Southeast Asian case with a date and a concrete institutional detail (Dutch Cultivation System 1830–1870, VOC treaty with Ambon 1600, 1698 Dai Viet boundary, Korean War special procurement 1950–1953).
- Name the course concept explicitly as you use it.
- Close with the comparison: how does the contrast case (Europe, Khmer, Africa, Philippines, Moluccas) sharpen the claim?

For multiple choice, the likely question stems are definitions of named concepts (mandala, Cultivation System, EOI, reversal of development, extractive institutions), author-to-claim pairings (who argued what), and chronology anchors (which kingdom came before which, which colonial treaty preceded which).

## Part I. Per-Reading Entries

### Reading 1. Hayton (2014), Chapter 1: Wrecks and Wrongs, Prehistory to 1500

**Full citation.** Hayton, B. (2014). Wrecks and wrongs: Prehistory to 1500. In *The South China Sea: The struggle for power in Asia* (Ch. 1, pp. 1–28). Yale University Press.

**Core argument and contribution.** Hayton is a journalist writing for a popular-academic audience, but the Week 1 use of his chapter is specifically archaeological and historiographic. His contribution to the course is to dismantle two origin myths about Southeast Asia and replace them with a maritime-network model. The first myth is that Southeast Asian civilisation was imported wholesale from China or India, with the region serving as a stage on which outsiders played imperial games. The second myth is that the Chinese "discovered" and have always owned the South China Sea. Against both, Hayton synthesises recent archaeology (Paz, Bellwood, Solheim), linguistics (Dempwolff, Blust on Austronesian), and oceanic navigation history to argue that Southeast Asia produced a genuinely indigenous maritime civilisation. The key conceptual move is the Nusantao Maritime Trading and Communication Network: a loose, polyglot, seagoing population of traders, fishers, and raiders who tied the coastlines of what is now southern China, Vietnam, the Philippines, Indonesia, and Malaysia into a single commercial zone centuries before Indic or Sinic political forms arrived. Against Coedès's old claim that Southeast Asia was "stuck in the Stone Age" until Hindus and Buddhists brought civilisation in the fourth century CE, Hayton argues Southeast Asians were trading with India long before "Indian culture took root," and that Southeast Asian rulers *chose* to adopt Indic political vocabulary and ritual for their own state-building purposes. Colonisation was not imposed; elite adoption was selective.

**Key concepts and definitions.**

*Nusantao.* A term Solheim coined from Austronesian roots for "south island people." Refers to the seagoing, multi-ethnic, weakly-settled population that ran maritime trade across the South China Sea from roughly the first millennium BCE onward. The Nusantao are not an ethnic group. They are the *agents* of a trading network. Modern descendants include the Badjao, Bajau, Orang Laut, Tanka, and Dan sea peoples. The concept matters because it relocates the origin of Southeast Asian civilisation from land-based imperial capitals to the maritime commons.

*Mandala.* The Sanskrit-derived political form adopted by the great Indianised kingdoms of Southeast Asia (Funan, Champa, Srivijaya, Angkor, Majapahit, Malacca). A mandala is a centre of ritual and commercial gravity whose ruler, the *cakravartin* or "wheel-turner," commands loyalty through religious legitimacy, patron-client ties, and the ability to extract tribute from smaller polities. It has no fixed borders. Its reach expands and contracts as peripheral rulers shift allegiance. Peripheral elites can owe tribute to more than one mandala at once. This flexibility is its short-run strength and its long-run weakness: mandalas rarely consolidated into hard territorial states with impersonal bureaucracies.

*Cakravartin.* The "wheel-turner," the idealised universal sovereign at the centre of a mandala. Legitimacy is ritual and cosmological, not bureaucratic.

*Indianisation (adoption, not colonisation).* The process by which Southeast Asian rulers selectively imported Indian political, religious, and administrative concepts (Hinduism, later Buddhism, Sanskrit royal titles, Indian-inspired town planning) to reinforce their authority at home. Hayton insists this was active borrowing by Southeast Asian rulers, not a one-way diffusion from Indian colonisers. This matters for any question about whether Southeast Asia's "Indianness" means it lacks agency.

*Tributary relation.* An entry ticket to the Chinese port system, not a sign of vassalage. Chinese emperors interpreted foreign "tribute" as recognition of their heavenly mandate. Southeast Asian rulers interpreted the same transactions as a trading partnership. Both sides performed the ritual for different reasons, and modern nationalist Chinese historiography has retroactively read feudal subordination into what was a commercial protocol.

*Entrepôt.* A trading port that serves as a relay point in a longer commercial chain. Funan, Srivijaya, Malacca, and modern Singapore are all entrepôts. The form depends on controlling a chokepoint (Isthmus of Kra, Strait of Malacca) and maintaining good relations with the rulers of distant markets (India, southern China, later Europe).

*Monsoon cycle (mawsim).* The annual southwest-northeast wind reversal that governs sailing in the South China Sea. Ships from China had to wait in Funan or Champa from February to June before safe passage west. This enforced dwell time is what made intermediate entrepôts rich. The monsoon is not incidental geography; it is the infrastructure of premodern maritime commerce.

**Real-world SEA examples.**

*Funan (first to sixth century CE, Mekong Delta, southern Vietnam and Cambodia).* The earliest major South China Sea trading power. Controlled the Isthmus of Kra portage, the 40-kilometre land crossing that let traders skip the 1,600-kilometre sail around the Malay Peninsula. Adopted Hinduism, Sanskrit names, Indian political culture. Sent embassies to China. Fell to competitors in the fourth century when traders learned to bypass the Isthmus.

*Srivijaya (seventh to twelfth century, based at Palembang on the Musi River, Sumatra).* A "classic mandala" per Hayton. By 683 CE it commanded roughly 20,000 armed men, many of them Nusantao hired hands. It controlled both the Strait of Malacca and the Sunda Strait, making east-west trade "practically impossible without Srivijaya's consent." It was the Tang Dynasty's gatekeeper to the Nanyang (the maritime region of Southeast Asia). Declined after a raid by the Chola kingdom of southern India in 1025.

*Champa (roughly fourth to fifteenth century, central Vietnam coast).* Indianised, named after an Indian kingdom, built massive red-brick Hindu towers still standing today. Ran on a mix of legitimate trade and piracy. Not a centralised state but a coalition of river-valley settlements recognising a rotating central ruler. Illustrates the mandala form very cleanly: power moves between valleys, but the ritual centre persists.

*The Tang-Abbasid trade (618–907 CE).* Integrated the South China Sea into a Baghdad-to-Guangzhou commercial "superhighway" running through Srivijaya and Sri Lanka. The Belitung shipwreck of 826 CE, carrying 55,000 pieces of Chinese pottery on an Arab dhow, is the material evidence: by the ninth century, the region was producing and mass-exporting specifically for Middle Eastern and South Asian markets.

*The Zheng He voyages (1405–1433).* Hayton uses Geoff Wade's reading, not the modern Chinese state's. Zheng's voyages were "clear shows of force," carrying 20,000 armed troops, used to coerce tributary relations, install puppet rulers (Sri Lanka 1411), and garrison Malacca (1405). They were gunboat diplomacy, not peace missions. The voyages also illustrate how thin and contingent Chinese naval engagement with the South China Sea actually was: the Ming burned the maps and let the ships rot after 30 years and did not return to blue-water operations for 500 years. Any modern claim that the South China Sea "has belonged to China since ancient times" runs into the historical record.

**Anticipated SAQ prompts this reading answers.**

- "How does the Nusantao framework change our understanding of the origins of Southeast Asian civilisation?" Good answer: reject the diffusionist model (civilisation arrives from India or China), reframe Southeast Asia as a maritime network that pre-existed and then selectively incorporated Indic political forms, use Funan and Srivijaya to show indigenous agency in adopting Hindu-Buddhist kingship.
- "What is a mandala, and how did it shape the trajectory of Southeast Asian states?" Good answer: define mandala as ritual-centric, borderless, patron-client state form (Srivijaya, Angkor, Majapahit as exemplars); explain that its flexibility enabled commercial success but worked against consolidation into tax-raising centralised states; set up the comparison with European feudal-to-nation-state trajectory that Diamond and Acemoglu pick up.

### Reading 2. Dell, Lane, and Querubin (2018): The Historical State and Economic Development in Vietnam

**Full citation.** Dell, M., Lane, N., and Querubin, P. (2018). The historical state, local collective action, and economic development in Vietnam. *Econometrica*, 86(6), 2083–2121. https://doi.org/10.3982/ECTA15122

**Core argument and contribution.** The paper delivers one of the cleanest empirical demonstrations in the political-economy literature that precolonial state structure still conditions local development today. The authors exploit the fact that Vietnam sits on the seam between the Sinic (Northeast Asian) and Indic (Southeast Asian) civilisational zones. Northern Vietnam, Dai Viet, inherited a Chinese-style centralised bureaucratic state in which the village was the fundamental administrative unit. The Khmer periphery to the south ran on a decentralised patron-client model in which villages were not administrative units. Using a regression discontinuity design along a 1698 administrative boundary inside southern Vietnam, they show that villages converted to Dai Viet administration in 1698 have household consumption roughly one-third higher today than neighbouring villages that were not formally incorporated until about 150 years later. The mechanism is not formal institutions, which were razed by successive states (French, South Vietnam, Socialist Republic of Vietnam). The mechanism is informal norms of local collective action, which Dai Viet villages accumulated over centuries of having to elect councils, maintain land and population registers, fill tax and conscription quotas, and run local public works, and which persisted long after the institutions that produced them were destroyed. The contribution is threefold: it pushes back against Putnam's and Fukuyama's claim that strong bureaucratic states crowd out civil society, showing that a particular *kind* of strong state (one that delegates implementation to villages) crowds it *in*; it supplies a credible identification for the Sinic-Indic divergence story that has otherwise lived in qualitative history; and it foregrounds local cooperation as a third channel of development, alongside markets and developmental national governments.

**Key concepts and definitions.**

*Sinic versus Indic states.* The Sinic model, exemplified by imperial China and adopted by Dai Viet, Japan, and Korea, features a centralised impersonal bureaucracy with uniform territorial administration, village-level administrative responsibility, meritocratic recruitment via examination, and written legal codes. The Indic model, exemplified by Khmer, Srivijaya, Majapahit, and other mandalas, features personalised patron-client rule, land-based landowning elites rather than bureaucrats, temple-based public finance, and weak central control over the periphery. The village is not an administrative unit under the Indic model.

*Institutionalised village governance.* The specific Dai Viet institutional arrangement in which the central state set tax, conscription, and public-goods quotas at the village level but lacked the capacity to micro-manage implementation. Villages therefore had to elect councils (by popular male suffrage from 1461), maintain population and property registers, organise land redistribution (from the 1690s), discipline free riders, and fund local public works. Repeated cooperation under a credible external mandate built what social-capital theorists would call civic capital.

*Regression discontinuity (RD).* A causal-inference design that compares units just on either side of an arbitrary treatment boundary. The identifying assumption is that all other factors vary smoothly across the boundary, so any jump in outcomes at the threshold can be attributed to the treatment. Dell, Lane, and Querubin use a 25-kilometre bandwidth on each side of the 1698 boundary, with a local linear RD polynomial, and show balance on elevation, slope, temperature, precipitation, rice suitability, and other geographic variables.

*Crowding in versus crowding out.* Putnam and Fukuyama argued that strong bureaucratic states crowd out civil society by absorbing functions that civic organisations would otherwise perform. Dell, Lane, and Querubin flip this: by delegating implementation rather than performing it centrally, Dai Viet's state crowded *in* sustained local cooperation. The type of state matters, not just its strength.

*Civic capital / social capital.* Informal norms and networks that enable cooperation for collective purposes. The concept draws on Putnam, Leonardi, and Nanetti (1994) on Italy and Guiso, Sapienza, and Zingales on civic behaviour. Dell, Lane, and Querubin operationalise it through participation in local civic organisations, local self-defense and self-development projects, filled village council seats, local tax collection rates, and redistribution to needy households.

*Hamlet Evaluation System.* A U.S. military programme from 1969–1973 that collected monthly data on roughly 18,000 South Vietnamese hamlets. The authors use it as a mechanism-evidence dataset, since formal state institutions were uniform across the RD boundary by that date but informal norms were not.

**Real-world SEA examples and the empirical core.**

*The 1698 boundary, Gia Dinh region, southern Vietnam.* In 1698, Nguyen Phuc Chu formally organised Dai Viet administrative villages in the Dong Nai and Sai Gon river basins (today's Ho Chi Minh City area). Villages to the east of the boundary got the full Dai Viet institutional package in 1698. Villages immediately to the west did not become Vietnamese administrative units until about 150 years later, just before French colonisation. The delay was driven by "idiosyncratic political circumstances" (internal Vietnamese strife between Trinh and Nguyen, Cambodian civil war, the arrival of Ming refugees), not by differences in economic geography. That is what makes the RD credible.

*Main finding.* Household consumption today is roughly 33 percent higher on the Dai Viet side of the boundary. The effect is robust across bandwidths, holds when Ho Chi Minh City is dropped, and replicates in colonial-era and 1970s income data. This is not a short-run effect of communist collectivisation or French colonial policy; it tracks back through multiple successive regimes.

*Mechanism evidence.* Dai Viet villagers were nearly twice as likely to participate in civic organisations, more likely to run self-defense and self-development projects, more likely to fill village council seats, more likely to collect taxes locally, more likely to redistribute to needy households, and more educated (with roughly one additional year of schooling). Crucially, effects at the *provincial* level are zero, ruling out higher-tier government as the channel, and Dai Viet residents had *more negative* views of the national government, ruling out generic pro-state sentiment.

*1461 village elections.* Dai Viet villages elected councils by popular male suffrage from 1461 onward, centuries before similar institutions in most of Europe and well before anything comparable in the Khmer zone. This is a concrete institutional detail worth knowing for a short essay that wants to contrast Sinic and Indic governance.

**Anticipated SAQ prompts this reading answers.**

- "Why have Northeast Asian economies outperformed Southeast Asian ones over the long run?" Good answer: locate the divergence in the administrative technology of the precolonial state (institutionalised village governance versus patron-client rule), use the Dai Viet-Khmer 1698 boundary as the identification strategy, and argue that local collective-action norms are the mechanism of persistence.
- "Do strong states crowd out or crowd in civil society?" Good answer: reject the Putnam-Fukuyama generalisation, distinguish between states that centralise implementation (crowd out) and states that delegate implementation under credible quotas (crowd in), use Dai Viet village councils as the concrete case.

### Reading 3. Diamond (1997), Prologue: Yali's Question

**Full citation.** Diamond, J. (1997). Prologue: Yali's question. In *Guns, germs, and steel: The fates of human societies* (pp. 13–32). W. W. Norton.

**Core argument and contribution.** Diamond asks the biggest possible question in comparative development: why did wealth, technology, and political power become so unequally distributed across continents over the last 13,000 years? His answer is that the proximate causes of European global expansion after 1500 (steel weapons, oceangoing ships, centralised states, writing, epidemic diseases) are themselves consequences of a deeper ultimate cause: the unequal distribution across continents of wild plants and animals suitable for domestication, combined with the orientation (east-west or north-south) of continental landmasses. Eurasia had a rich starter kit of domesticable grasses and large mammals and an east-west axis that let innovations diffuse across similar climate bands. The Americas and Africa had fewer domesticable species and north-south axes that forced innovations to cross sharp ecological barriers. The causal chain runs: geography produced biogeography produced agriculture produced food surpluses produced dense sedentary populations with specialists, writing, hierarchical states, and (through sustained proximity to livestock) the crowd diseases that would later devastate populations on contact. The contribution is twofold. Substantively, Diamond reframes the long-run development question as a biogeographic one rather than a cultural or racial one. Methodologically, the Prologue is as important for what it *refuses* as for what it asserts: it rules out racial-cognitive explanations as empirically false and morally noxious, and it rules out "culture" explanations (Protestant ethic, Confucian values) as proximate rather than ultimate, on the grounds that culture itself requires explanation.

**Key concepts and definitions.**

*Proximate versus ultimate causes.* A proximate cause is the immediate mechanism (Pizarro had steel swords and horses). An ultimate cause is the deep condition that made the proximate cause available (Eurasia had the biogeographic starter kit that eventually produced steel metallurgy and domesticated horses). Short essays that answer Ravanilla's "why did Europe win" type prompts using proximate causes alone will be treated as incomplete.

*Yali's question.* "Why is it that you white people developed so much cargo and brought it to New Guinea, but we black people had little cargo of our own?" The question Yali posed to Diamond in Papua New Guinea in 1972. In Diamond's hands it becomes the framing question for the whole book: why the intercontinental wealth gap?

*East-west versus north-south axes.* Eurasia stretches longitudinally, so crops, livestock, and technologies could diffuse across thousands of miles without crossing major climate zones. The Americas and Africa stretch latitudinally; the same diffusion would require a crop to survive from temperate to tropical to temperate climates. This is why maize took thousands of years to spread from Mexico to the eastern United States and to the Andes, while wheat spread from the Fertile Crescent to Europe and China comparatively quickly.

*Domesticable species.* Of the world's roughly 200,000 species of wild plants, only a few thousand are edible and only a few hundred have been domesticated. Of roughly 148 large mammal species, only 14 were ever domesticated, and 13 of those were native to Eurasia. Diamond argues the distribution of these species is not a function of human ingenuity; it is a function of which continent you happen to live on.

*Guns, germs, and steel.* Diamond's shorthand for the proximate European advantages at the moment of contact with non-Eurasian populations after 1500. Guns (military technology), germs (crowd diseases to which Eurasians had immunity and others did not), and steel (metallurgy and the material basis of ocean-going ships). Diamond's point is that all three are downstream of the ultimate biogeographic condition.

**Real-world examples relevant to Southeast Asia.**

Diamond's Prologue is continental and does not dwell on Southeast Asia, so the right move on a midterm is to *apply* the framework to the region rather than quote it. The useful applications are:

*Rice domestication in the Yangtze basin circa 8500 years ago.* Proto-Austronesian speakers in the Yangtze region domesticated rice and then radiated outward through Taiwan and into the Philippines and Indonesia (the "Out of Taiwan" model Hayton describes). This is a Diamond-style biogeographic advantage: Southeast Asia was downstream of a Eurasian agricultural origin, not an independent inventor of the staple.

*European technological edge at contact.* When the Portuguese arrived in Melaka in 1511 and the Dutch East India Company arrived in the Moluccas in 1600, they brought oceangoing ships, cannon, gunpowder, and joint-stock financing. All of these are Diamond's "guns and steel" descended from the Eurasian biogeographic pathway. Acemoglu and Robinson will then take this observation and argue that what *mattered* was not the technology itself but the extractive institutions the technology enabled.

*Epidemic disease.* Less central to Southeast Asia than to the Americas, because Eurasian disease pools were shared across Asia, but still visible in the depopulation of some island archipelagos after contact.

**Assumptions and scope limits.** Diamond's framework works at the continental-millennial scale. At finer resolutions (why England rather than China industrialised first, why Dai Viet villages outperform Khmer villages inside a single province), geographic determinism is too coarse. Institutional, political, and cultural variables do the work. This is the productive tension the course wants you to hold: Diamond at the top scale, institutional variables (Dell-Lane-Querubin, Acemoglu-Robinson, Dell-Olken, Stubbs) at the finer scales.

**Anticipated SAQ prompts this reading answers.**

- "Why did Europe win the game of colonisation in Southeast Asia?" The sample A/A+ answer explicitly places Diamond's thesis as an *amplifier* of institutional capacity rather than a standalone explanation. Do the same: lead with European state formation (feudalism to nation-state), then invoke Diamond's guns-germs-steel as the technological payoff of biogeographic inheritance, then contrast with mandala political forms that could not consolidate.
- "What does Diamond's framework add to explanations of Southeast Asian underdevelopment, and what does it miss?" Good answer: it supplies the ultimate cause of the intercontinental gap (biogeography, continental axes) and rules out racial and culturalist explanations; it misses the fine-grained institutional variation within Eurasia and within Southeast Asia that drives most of the variation the course actually studies.

### Reading 4. Acemoglu and Robinson (2012), Chapter 9: Reversing Development

**Full citation.** Acemoglu, D., and Robinson, J. A. (2012). Reversing development. In *Why nations fail: The origins of power, prosperity, and poverty* (Ch. 9, pp. 244–271). Crown Publishing.

**Core argument and contribution.** Chapter 9 is the Southeast Asian chapter of *Why Nations Fail*. Its contribution is to extend the book's extractive-versus-inclusive institutions framework to a region where the story runs against the grain of the authors' usual narrative. Instead of the standard claim that colonialism extracted more or less intensively depending on settler mortality or factor endowments, Acemoglu and Robinson argue that in Southeast Asia, European colonialism actively *reversed* an ongoing process of commercialisation and institutional development. The Moluccan Archipelago in modern Indonesia (Tidore, Ternate, Bacan, Ambon, and the Banda Islands) was the fourteenth-through-sixteenth-century engine of the global spice trade. City-states like Aceh, Banten, Melaka, Makassar, Pegu, and Brunei were expanding rapidly. These were absolutist states, far from ideal on their own terms, but they were undergoing the same commercialisation-driven institutional ferment as early modern Europe. Then the Dutch East India Company (VOC) arrived. It forced the ruler of Ambon into a monopoly treaty on cloves in 1600. It massacred roughly 15,000 Bandanese in 1621 and replaced them with a Dutch-owned plantation system worked by slaves. It cut world spice supply by roughly 60 percent, doubling prices and extracting monopoly rents. Other Southeast Asian polities, watching this, voluntarily retreated from export agriculture to avoid attracting Dutch attention: Banten cut down its pepper trees in 1620, Maguindanao's raja destroyed its spice trees before his death, Burma moved its capital inland from Pegu to Ava in 1635. The result was de-urbanisation, a continent-wide retreat into absolutism and autarky, and the inability of Southeast Asia to participate in the industrialisation that arrived in Europe two centuries later. The chapter's power lies in its willingness to imagine a counterfactual: without Dutch aggression, Southeast Asian states "may have continued their commercialization by gradually adopting more and more inclusive institutions." Colonialism foreclosed that path.

**Key concepts and definitions.**

*Extractive institutions.* Political and economic arrangements designed to transfer wealth from the many to a narrow elite. Key features: insecure property rights for most of the population, forced labour or tribute, monopoly trade rights for colonial or domestic elites, political systems that exclude the majority. The VOC plantation system in Ambon and Banda is the paradigm case.

*Inclusive institutions.* The opposite: pluralistic political systems with secure property rights, open markets, and broad opportunity. Acemoglu and Robinson's counterfactual claim is that pre-VOC Southeast Asia was on a trajectory toward something like this; Dutch intervention foreclosed it.

*Reversal of development.* The process by which colonial intervention did not merely slow growth but actively undid prior commercialisation and institutional change. The term is distinctively Acemoglu-Robinson and worth deploying explicitly on the midterm.

*Monopoly rent-seeking.* The VOC strategy of reducing global spice supply to raise prices and extract rents, rather than increasing output. This is the opposite of the Dell-Olken reading's Cultivation System, where the Dutch *expanded* output by coerced means. The two Dutch colonial projects represent very different political-economy logics: rent extraction in the Moluccas, production expansion in Java.

*Forced labour / tribute intensification.* The Dutch did not always build new extractive institutions from scratch. In Ambon, they took over and intensified pre-existing tribute systems: extended families had owed cloves to the Ambonese elite, and the Dutch now stipulated that each household was tied to the soil and obligated to cultivate a specified number of clove trees. Colonial extraction often worked through, rather than around, indigenous hierarchy.

*Autarky response.* Neighbouring states retreated from trade to avoid Dutch aggression, deepening regional underdevelopment even in areas the VOC did not directly colonise. This is one of the chapter's most important arguments for a Southeast Asia course: colonialism's damage extended beyond the colonies.

**Real-world SEA examples.**

*The 1600 Ambon treaty and the clove monopoly.* In 1600, the Dutch persuaded the ruler of Ambon to sign an exclusive monopoly on clove trade. This was the template: a single treaty with a coercible central ruler, backed by VOC military power (from 1602 onward with its own army and chartered war-making authority), which then enabled the elimination of all competing traders.

*The Banda genocide, 1621.* Jan Pieterszoon Coen, Dutch governor of Batavia, sailed to Banda and massacred roughly 15,000 Bandanese. The Banda Islands had no central ruler Coen could coerce into a monopoly treaty, so he eliminated the population instead. The surviving fragment was kept alive to teach the Dutch how to grow mace and nutmeg. The islands were then divided into 68 parcels distributed to Dutch colonists, who bought slaves from the VOC to work them at fixed prices. The Banda case is the sharpest illustration of what "reversal of development" means in human terms.

*Banten's voluntary destruction of pepper trees, 1620.* Banten cut down its own pepper production to avoid attracting the VOC. This is the autarky response made concrete: a Southeast Asian state destroying its own productive capacity as defensive policy.

*Maguindanao, southern Philippines, 1686 and 1699.* Travelers reported that the raja had ordered all spice trees destroyed so "the Dutch Company would not come to fight with them." Second-order effects of Dutch aggression propagating across non-Dutch territories.

*The Burmese capital move, 1635.* Burma moved its capital from coastal Pegu to inland Ava on the Irrawaddy, a deliberate retreat from the maritime trading economy. This is de-urbanisation as state strategy.

*Dutch supply reduction of roughly 60 percent.* By the end of the seventeenth century, the Dutch had reduced world spice supply by about 60 percent and roughly doubled the price of nutmeg. Quantifiable evidence that monopoly rent extraction, not productive expansion, was the VOC's strategy.

**Anticipated SAQ prompts this reading answers.**

- "How did European colonialism reverse, rather than merely slow, Southeast Asian development?" Good answer: define reversal of development explicitly, use the VOC Moluccas case (1600 Ambon treaty, 1621 Banda genocide, 60 percent supply cut), then show the spillover (Banten 1620, Burma 1635, Maguindanao) to make the argument regional rather than local.
- "Contrast the Dutch colonial project in the Moluccas with the Dutch Cultivation System in Java." Good answer: Moluccas was rent extraction via monopoly restriction of output and forced-labour intensification (Acemoglu-Robinson); Java was production expansion via coerced reorganisation of village economies around sugar factories (Dell-Olken). Different political-economy logics, different long-run effects.

### Reading 5. Dell and Olken (2020): The Development Effects of the Extractive Colonial Economy

**Full citation.** Dell, M., and Olken, B. A. (2020). The development effects of the extractive colonial economy: The Dutch Cultivation System in Java. *Review of Economic Studies*, 87(1), 164–203. https://doi.org/10.1093/restud/rdz017

**Core argument and contribution.** Dell and Olken deliver the sharpest published counter-example to the Acemoglu-Robinson "extractive institutions cause underdevelopment" thesis. They study the Dutch Cultivation System (*Cultuurstelsel*), the 1830s–1870s colonial programme that forced villages along Java's northern coast to grow sugarcane for processing in 94 water-powered Dutch factories. At its peak the system accounted for over one-third of Dutch government revenue. The conventional extractive-institutions story predicts that the areas most intensively subjected to this system should be poorer today. Dell and Olken find the opposite: areas within a few kilometres of historical sugar factories are today more industrialised, better connected by roads and rail, more educated, and roughly 10 percent richer per capita than nearby locations that were equally suitable for sugar but were not selected. They show this using two independent identification strategies: a randomisation-inference approach that compares actual factory locations to 1,000 randomly generated feasible counterfactual configurations (p-value 0.001), and a spatial regression discontinuity at the boundaries of each factory's coerced-cultivation catchment area. The reason for positive persistence, they argue, is that *this particular kind* of extractive system required substantial local manufacturing (sugar processing had to happen within 4–7 kilometres of the cane field because raw cane spoils quickly) and substantial transport infrastructure (roads and rail to move heavy processed sugar to ports). Three channels of persistence drive the result: input-output linkages to downstream food-processing industries that agglomerated around former factory sites even after sugar collapsed; infrastructure (Java's early-twentieth-century road and rail network was the densest in Asia, built for the Cultivation System); and human capital, since the Dutch empowered village heads to redistribute land and fund schools from communal revenues, producing 10–15 percent more communal land and higher education levels that persist to the 2000s. The contribution is to show that "extractive" is not a sufficient description. The specific form of extraction matters. Peruvian silver mining (*mita*) and Congolese rubber had negative persistence because they involved raw-material extraction with no local manufacturing. The Dutch Cultivation System had positive persistence because the physics of sugar processing forced the Dutch to build lasting industrial structures in Java.

**Key concepts and definitions.**

*Dutch Cultivation System (Cultuurstelsel).* The 1830s–1870s Dutch colonial programme that forced Javanese villages along the northern coast to cultivate sugarcane on portions of their land and deliver it to Dutch sugar factories. Revenues funded roughly one-third of the Dutch home budget. Forced labour, village-head compensation tied to cane output, and Dutch-owned processing were the core features.

*Catchment area.* The 4–7 kilometre radius around each sugar factory within which all villages were forced to grow cane and supply labour. The radius was set by the transport-spoilage constraint on raw cane. Catchment areas are the spatial unit of coerced reorganisation, and their boundaries are what the spatial regression discontinuity exploits.

*Input-output linkages / agglomeration.* Downstream food-processing industries clustered near former sugar factory sites because of supplier networks and consumer concentrations. Even after sugar production collapsed in the Great Depression, the industrial base persisted and attracted new downstream activity. This is the key mechanism distinguishing the Cultivation System from mineral-extraction colonies.

*Counterfactual factory analysis.* The authors' main identification strategy. They construct 1,000 randomly generated alternative configurations of feasible factory sites (constrained by sugar-suitability, river access, and spacing requirements) and compare actual-factory effects to the distribution of counterfactual effects. The p-value of 0.001 means the observed effect would occur in fewer than 1 in 1,000 random configurations.

*Spatial regression discontinuity.* The authors' second identification strategy, exploiting the sharp boundaries of each factory's catchment area. Villages just inside the catchment were forced to grow cane; villages just outside were not. This isolates the effect of forced cultivation itself, separate from proximity to the factory.

*Village communal land.* Land set aside for collective village use. Dell and Olken show 10–15 percent more communal land in Cultivation System villages in both 1980 and 2003. Village heads, empowered by the Dutch to deliver cane quotas, redistributed land and used the communal portion to fund schools and public goods. The long-run effect is higher education levels from the 1920s cohort through 2011.

*Infrastructure persistence.* Roads and rail built to transport sugar persist as the backbone of Java's modern transport network. Villages near historical factories had higher road and rail density in 1900 and still do today. Infrastructure, once laid, compounds.

**Real-world SEA examples.**

*Pasuruan, Kediri, and Surabaya sugar belt, East Java, 1830s–1930s.* The core Cultivation System districts. The 94 factories were built along Java's northern coast and in its interior river valleys. By the early twentieth century the Pasuruan-Surabaya corridor was one of the most industrialised zones in colonial Asia.

*Collapse of sugar production, Great Depression and after.* The System's formal end came in the 1870s, and sugar production continued until the Great Depression, when Indonesia (lacking the protected European markets that had sustained the industry) became one of the world's largest sugar *importers*. Yet the industrial base survived: today the same districts host Indonesia's densest food-processing industries.

*The 1920s schooling cohort.* Dell and Olken trace the human-capital effect back to the 1920s birth cohort, meaning the educational premium predates modern Indonesian state investment. This is the smoking gun that the effect is driven by historical village-level spending on schools, not by contemporary government targeting of already-developed areas.

*The 10 percent per-capita consumption premium.* Today, households within a few kilometres of historical sugar factory sites have per-capita consumption roughly 10 percent higher than those more than 10 kilometres away, controlling for counterfactual factory effects.

**Anticipated SAQ prompts this reading answers.**

- "Are colonial institutions bad for long-run development?" Good answer: the conventional Acemoglu story says yes (Moluccas as exhibit A), but Dell and Olken show the answer depends on the *form* of extraction. When extraction requires local manufacturing and infrastructure, the physical and human-capital structures colonisers built can outlast the colonial system itself and generate positive persistence. Contrast Moluccas (rent extraction, output restriction, negative legacy) with Java (output expansion via coerced manufacturing, positive legacy).
- "What does the Dutch Cultivation System share with, and differ from, the Peruvian *mita* or Congolese rubber extraction?" Good answer: all three are extractive in the sense that they coerced labour for colonial profit; the Cultivation System differs in requiring on-site processing and lasting infrastructure, which Peru and Congo did not, which is why only Java exhibits positive long-run persistence.

### Reading 6. Stubbs (1999): War and Economic Development

**Full citation.** Stubbs, R. (1999). War and economic development: Export-oriented industrialization in East and Southeast Asia. *Comparative Politics*, 31(3), 337–355. https://www.jstor.org/stable/422343

**Core argument and contribution.** Stubbs argues that the East and Southeast Asian "miracle" economies (Japan, South Korea, Taiwan, Hong Kong, Singapore, Malaysia, Thailand) industrialised rapidly in the second half of the twentieth century because the sequence of wars from 1941 to 1989 (World War II, the Chinese Civil War, the Korean War, the Malayan Emergency, the Vietnam War, the Indonesian Confrontation, the Vietnamese occupation of Cambodia, and the overarching Cold War) produced geostrategic conditions that no other region of the developing world enjoyed. His contribution is to insert war into the neoclassical-versus-statist debate on the Asian miracle. Neoclassical economists had credited free markets; statists had credited strategic state intervention. Stubbs argues both accounts are incomplete without the war story. Wars did three things. They built strong states: World War II and the Japanese occupation destroyed the old social order (landowning elites, entrenched patronage networks) across most of the region, and the Cold War threat of Communist insurgency justified the centralisation of power, suppression of labour, and construction of capable bureaucracies free from ordinary societal opposition. They injected capital: U.S. military procurement, economic aid, and strategic spending flooded the region (roughly $600 million per year to Japan during the Korean War, $4.2 billion in aid to South Korea 1953–1969, $1.7 billion to Taiwan 1949–mid-1960s, later Japanese foreign direct investment). And they opened export markets: wars, especially Vietnam, drove U.S. consumer demand and created permissive trade policies for allied economies, giving East and Southeast Asian manufacturers the demand pull they needed for the import-substitution-to-export-orientation transition. The seven miracle economies all made this transition; the timing of each country's shift within the war sequence determined how much it benefited. The negative case is the Philippines: wars did not dismantle Filipino landed elites and did not build a strong central state, so the country remained stuck in import-substitution mode under what Gary Hawes called a "weak predatory" state. Stubbs's contribution for a course on Southeast Asia specifically is that war is also the mechanism by which the W3 chapter closes: the colonial institutional legacies of Acemoglu-Robinson and Dell-Olken get reshaped (or not) by the war sequence Stubbs describes.

**Key concepts and definitions.**

*Export-oriented industrialisation (EOI).* A development strategy built around manufacturing competitively priced goods for overseas markets, especially the U.S. consumer market. The hallmark of East Asian rapid growth from roughly the 1960s onward.

*Import-substitution industrialisation (ISI).* The predecessor strategy of replacing imports with domestically produced goods, usually behind protective tariffs. ISI built the industrial capacity that EOI then leveraged; the transition from ISI to EOI is the policy event Stubbs is explaining.

*Destructive effects of war.* Loss of life, forced migration, social and political dislocation, destruction of infrastructure, collapse of the wartime state. Experienced on the war's own territory. Indochina (Vietnam, Cambodia, Laos) suffered these effects and did not industrialise.

*Formative or developmental effects of war.* Centralisation of government, expansion of bureaucracy, broadening of the revenue base, technological development, mobilisation of underemployed resources. Experienced by states preparing for or peripheral to war. Japan, Korea, Taiwan, Malaya, Singapore, and Thailand all experienced these effects.

*Redistributive or reformative effects of war.* Redistribution of wealth through war spending, changes in trade patterns, new markets, socialisation and integration of society, expansion of education and skills, habituation to state intervention, opportunities for reform that peacetime politics would block. All seven miracle economies experienced these effects.

*Strong state / weak society.* The configuration Stubbs says is necessary (though not sufficient) for the EOI transition. Wars weakened traditional social elites (landowners, commodity-export oligarchs) and empowered centralised institutional states that could override opposition to EOI policies. The Philippine counterexample shows what happens when war does not produce this reconfiguration: Spanish and American colonial rule left a strong landed elite, the second world war and the Huk rebellion did not unseat it, and post-war Philippine politics reverted to regional autocrats and pork-barrel patronage.

*Cold War capital injection.* Specific measurable flows: Korean War U.S. special procurement in Japan rose from zero in 1949 to $600 million in 1951 and $850 million in 1952, accounting for 63 percent of all Japanese exports in 1952; $4.2 billion in U.S. economic aid to South Korea 1953–1969, covering roughly 70 percent of South Korean imports 1953–1962 and nearly 80 percent of total fixed capital formation; $1.7 billion to Taiwan 1949–mid-1960s, roughly 27 percent going to mixed public-private enterprises; Vietnam War procurement generating "at least an extra $1 billion per year" for Japan on average 1966–1971.

*War-induced mercantilism.* States at war or threatened by war prioritise self-sufficiency and state-business cooperation, producing the neomercantilist variant of EOI that ties Stubbs to the statist literature (Amsden, Wade, Johnson).

**Real-world SEA examples.**

*Japan in the Korean War, 1950–1953.* Yoshida called the Korean War boom "the gift of the Gods." U.S. special procurement for the war effort ran $600 million annually in 1951, $850 million in 1952, and saved many of Japan's major industrial companies. Japanese industrial modernisation advanced on this base. The Vietnam War then "pulled Japan out of the 1965 slump" with an additional $1 billion per year on average through 1971.

*South Korea's EOI pivot, early 1960s.* Park Chung Hee's 1961 military coup produced a "relatively autonomous state" largely independent of social pressures. U.S. aid had already covered 80 percent of fixed capital formation in the 1950s. The shift to EOI in the early-to-mid 1960s was driven by balance-of-payments crises and direct American pressure. The outcome was the South Korean developmental state that dominates later weeks of the course.

*Taiwan, Kuomintang period.* $1.7 billion in U.S. aid, 1949 through the mid-1960s. A Kuomintang government with few links to Taiwanese society was relatively independent of local social pressures. The U.S. Agency for International Development heavily promoted Taiwan as a site for U.S. investment. Import-substitution capacity was built first (about 27 percent of aid went to mixed enterprises), then deployed as the platform for EOI.

*Malaysia and the Malayan Emergency, 1948–1960.* Commodity-price booms during the Korean War allowed Britain and the Malayan government to enlarge the military, police, and intelligence services and conduct a successful counterinsurgency against the Malayan Communist Party. The Emergency built the civil-military apparatus Malaysia would use to manage its slower and later EOI transition, which shifted into high gear only in the early 1980s.

*Thailand, 1970s–1980s.* The long-dominant position of the military and bureaucracy was reinforced by the internal Communist guerrilla threat and the external threat from Communist Vietnam after the 1978 invasion of Cambodia. U.S. Vietnam-era aid flowed through. Thailand made its main EOI shift in 1984–1985 with a sharp baht devaluation and tariff reform.

*Singapore after separation from Malaysia, 1965.* The People's Action Party used the dual threat of Communism (regional and internal) and of Singapore's own structural vulnerability to maintain tight control. Vietnam-era U.S. spending helped capitalise the state's intervention in industry. Lee Kuan Yew's authoritarian-developmental model is unintelligible without the security story Stubbs tells.

*The Philippine negative case.* Spanish and American colonial rule produced a weak central government and relatively powerful regional strongmen. Neither the second world war nor the Huk rebellion undermined the traditional landed order. Martial law under Marcos briefly centralised authority but then reverted to regional autocrats and pork-barrel politics. There was no Cold War justification for suppressing domestic opposition in the same way there was in Malaysia or South Korea, because the Huk had been defeated by the early 1950s and no equivalent external communist threat arose. The Philippines remained stuck in import-substitution mode with pervasive patronage politics, an "obvious counterexample" to the Stubbs thesis, and a useful negative case for midterm short essays.

**Anticipated SAQ prompts this reading answers.**

- "How did World War II and the Cold War impact Southeast Asia's economic development?" Good answer: describe the three effects of war (destructive, formative, redistributive); identify the specific mechanism by which wars built strong states, injected capital, and opened export markets; use Japan, South Korea, and Taiwan as the core cases and Malaysia-Singapore-Thailand as the near-industrialised cases; close with the Philippine negative case to show the mechanism is necessary but not sufficient.
- "Why did some Southeast Asian countries industrialise rapidly after 1945 and others not?" Good answer: strong state produced by war versus weak state not produced by war, with Cold War capital and market access as the necessary enablers.

## Part II. Cross-Reading Synthesis

Now the real analytical work. The midterm will almost certainly reward answers that hold two or three readings together and articulate where they agree, where they contradict, and where they build on each other. Below are the genuine cross-reading tensions and linkages that matter. I am not manufacturing contradictions where none exist; each of these is visible in the actual texts.

### 1. Geography versus institutions: Diamond against everyone else

The cleanest methodological tension on the syllabus runs between Diamond and the four institutional readings (Dell-Lane-Querubin, Acemoglu-Robinson, Dell-Olken, Stubbs). Diamond argues that long-run outcomes are dominated by slow-moving biogeographic endowments: which plants and animals were available for domestication, whether your continent stretched east-west or north-south, how close your ancestors lived to livestock. At continental-millennial scale, he is probably right that these variables do most of the work. But the moment you zoom in on Southeast Asia specifically, institutional variation takes over. Dell, Lane, and Querubin show a 33 percent consumption gap across a single historical boundary inside one Vietnamese province with effectively identical soil, climate, and rainfall. No Diamond-style variable can explain that. Acemoglu and Robinson show spice islands that were biogeographically advantaged collapsing under Dutch extractive institutions while less advantaged areas that escaped VOC reach persisted better. Dell and Olken show two Javanese villages separated by a catchment-area boundary having different industrial and educational trajectories 200 years later. The right move on the midterm is to treat Diamond as the ultimate cause of the Eurasia-versus-elsewhere gap and institutional readings as the proximate cause of within-Eurasia and within-Southeast-Asia variation. The sample A/A+ answer models this exactly: it uses Diamond's "guns, germs, and steel" as an *amplifier* of the institutional capacity that European nation-states had already built, not as a substitute for the institutional story.

### 2. The two Dutch colonialisms: Acemoglu-Robinson versus Dell-Olken

This is the sharpest genuine contradiction in the reading set, and it is the single most likely cross-reading prompt on the midterm. Both readings are about the same colonial power in the same archipelago. Acemoglu and Robinson argue that the VOC in the Moluccas *reversed development*: it massacred the Bandanese in 1621, cut world spice supply by 60 percent to extract monopoly rents, triggered de-urbanisation and autarky responses across the region, and foreclosed a commercialisation path that would have continued without it. Dell and Olken, studying the same Dutch colonial state two centuries later on the same island of Java, find *positive* long-run development effects: areas forced into the Cultivation System are today more industrialised, more educated, and roughly 10 percent richer. How can both be right? They are, and the reconciliation is the key analytical move. The two Dutch colonial projects had fundamentally different political-economy logics. The VOC in the Moluccas was about extracting *rent* by restricting output. The Cultivation System in Java was about extracting *output* by forcing its expansion. Rent extraction required destroying competing producers and suppressing supply; output extraction required building factories, roads, rail, and a village-level administrative apparatus capable of delivering cane. The physical infrastructure of the second project persisted and compounded; the institutional destruction of the first project persisted and compounded negatively. The right short essay does not try to adjudicate between Acemoglu and Dell-Olken as if one is wrong. It names the mechanism that distinguishes the two cases (rent versus output, raw-material versus on-site-processing) and then uses both to argue that "extractive" is not a sufficient descriptor. The *form* of extraction matters for long-run effects.

### 3. The strong state paradox: Dell-Lane-Querubin versus Stubbs on when central authority helps

Both papers treat strong states as central to development, but they identify very different *kinds* of strength. Dell, Lane, and Querubin argue that the Dai Viet state helped long-run development precisely because it was strong *enough* to impose credible tax and conscription quotas on villages, but not strong enough to implement them centrally. It had to delegate implementation, which built local collective-action norms that persisted for centuries. Stubbs argues that the Cold War East Asian state helped development precisely because it was strong *enough* to suppress labour, override landed-elite opposition, and implement EOI policies centrally, with heavy American financial backing. One paper's hero state delegates. The other paper's hero state centralises. Are these incompatible? Not quite. The reconciliation runs through timescale and function. Dell-Lane-Querubin's mechanism operates across centuries through accumulated norms that substitute for missing markets and missing state capacity. Stubbs's mechanism operates across decades through credible central coordination of industrial policy and capital flows. A good short essay holds both: local collective-action capacity (Dell-Lane-Querubin's Dai Viet legacy) is a slow-building civic-capital endowment; central state autonomy (Stubbs's war-forged Park Chung Hee or Lee Kuan Yew state) is a rapidly-deployable policy-implementation capacity. The two are complementary. Taiwan and South Korea had both. The Philippines had neither. Thailand had more of the first than the second.

### 4. Colonial institutions versus Cold War security pressures as explanations of post-1945 variation

This was the W3 discussion question the agent context flags as approved, and it almost certainly reappears on the midterm in some form. Stubbs argues that the variation in post-1945 industrialisation across Southeast Asia is explained primarily by the sequence of wars and their effect on state-building, capital supply, and export markets. Dell-Olken and Acemoglu-Robinson argue that colonial institutional legacies do substantial work: Java's Cultivation System districts are still richer today; the Moluccas are still underdeveloped. How much of the postcolonial variation is colonial legacy and how much is Cold War sequencing? The honest answer is that the two channels interact. Colonial institutions set the initial distribution of state capacity, landed elites, and commercial infrastructure that post-war states inherited. War then reshaped that inheritance, empowering some states (Malaysia with commodity-boom revenues to fight the Emergency, Singapore thrown out of Malaysia in 1965) and failing to reshape others (the Philippines). The midterm-worthy move is to note that Stubbs's mechanism depends on having a society *to* reshape, and the nature of that society is precisely what Dell-Olken and Acemoglu-Robinson argue was set in the colonial period. The Philippines is the useful limiting case: Spanish and American colonial rule produced a strong landed elite, weak central state, and strong regional autocrats; neither World War II nor the Huk rebellion shifted that configuration; post-war Philippine political economy reverted to what it had been, and EOI never took off.

### 5. The Hayton-Dell-Lane-Querubin connection: the Sinic-Indic divide as the deep structure

Hayton's chapter introduces the mandala form as the characteristic Southeast Asian political organisation: centred on a *cakravartin* and ritual legitimacy, with patron-client ties, no fixed borders, and no institutionalised village layer. Dell-Lane-Querubin take that framing and put a number on it. The mandala model is not just aesthetic description; it is an administrative technology whose economic effects can be measured two centuries later across a historical boundary. Hayton is doing the archaeology and ethnography. Dell-Lane-Querubin are doing the econometrics. Both are pointing at the same thing. The Sinic-Indic distinction the authors make is not cultural essentialism. It is a claim about administrative technology: Sinic states ran through institutionalised villages, Indic states ran through personalised patron networks, and the difference in accumulated local collective-action capacity is why the former became Japan, Korea, and Taiwan, and the latter became Cambodia, Laos, and much of the Philippines.

### 6. Where the readings agree: path dependence

Every reading on this midterm is, at some level, a path-dependence argument. Hayton: the Nusantao maritime network shaped what Southeast Asian political forms could emerge centuries before Islam or Europeans arrived. Dell-Lane-Querubin: informal norms of village cooperation produced by a 17th-century administrative choice still shape household consumption in the 21st. Diamond: biogeographic endowments 13,000 years ago still shape the global distribution of wealth and power. Acemoglu-Robinson: extractive institutions imposed in 1600 still shape Southeast Asian absence from global industrialisation. Dell-Olken: infrastructure built in 1850 still channels economic activity in 2010. Stubbs: the war sequence of 1941–1989 still determines which states can run coherent industrial policy today. Path dependence is the silent common framework. A midterm answer that uses the phrase and names the specific mechanism (norm accumulation, infrastructure lock-in, institutional persistence, civic-capital compounding) will signal to Ravanilla that the student understands what binds the readings together.

## Part III. Consolidated Concept Glossary (Index-Card Candidates)

Concepts most likely to appear on multiple-choice questions or function as the axis of a short essay:

- **Mandala / cakravartin.** Borderless ritual-centric Indic state form, patron-client ties, no institutionalised village layer. Srivijaya, Funan, Champa, Angkor, Majapahit.
- **Sinic versus Indic states.** Administrative difference: centralised bureaucracy with village as unit (Dai Viet, Japan, Korea) versus personalised patronage with temple-based finance (Khmer, Srivijaya).
- **Nusantao.** Solheim's term for the multi-ethnic South China Sea maritime trading network that pre-dates Indic or Sinic political forms.
- **Extractive institutions (Acemoglu-Robinson).** Political-economic arrangements transferring wealth to a narrow elite; insecure property rights for most; forced labour; monopoly rights.
- **Inclusive institutions.** Pluralistic politics, secure property rights, open markets; the counterfactual path Acemoglu-Robinson say SEA was on before Dutch intervention.
- **Reversal of development.** Colonial intervention that halted and undid existing commercialisation; distinctive to Acemoglu-Robinson Ch. 9.
- **Autarky response.** Non-colonised states retreating from trade to avoid attracting colonial aggression (Banten 1620, Burma 1635, Maguindanao).
- **Dutch Cultivation System (Cultuurstelsel).** 1830s–1870s forced sugarcane cultivation in Java with Dutch-owned processing. One-third of Dutch government revenue at peak.
- **Catchment area.** 4–7 km radius around each Cultivation System sugar factory; unit of coerced reorganisation and the spatial RD boundary in Dell-Olken.
- **Input-output linkages / agglomeration.** Downstream food-processing industries clustering around former sugar factories; the main mechanism of positive persistence in Dell-Olken.
- **Export-oriented industrialisation (EOI).** Manufacturing for overseas markets; the miracle economies' strategy from the 1960s.
- **Import-substitution industrialisation (ISI).** Predecessor strategy of replacing imports with domestically produced goods behind tariffs.
- **Cold War capital injection.** Specific U.S. military procurement and aid flows (Japan $600M/yr in Korean War, Korea $4.2B 1953–1969, Taiwan $1.7B 1949–mid-1960s).
- **Strong state / weak society.** War-forged state autonomy from traditional social elites; necessary condition for EOI per Stubbs.
- **Regression discontinuity (RD).** Dell-Lane-Querubin's and Dell-Olken's identification strategy; compare units just on either side of an arbitrary historical boundary.
- **Institutionalised village governance.** Dai Viet institutional arrangement where villages held tax, conscription, and public-goods quotas; mechanism for long-run civic capital per Dell-Lane-Querubin.
- **Crowd in / crowd out.** Dell-Lane-Querubin's contribution: the *right kind* of strong state crowds *in* civic capital, contra Putnam and Fukuyama.
- **Proximate versus ultimate causes.** Diamond's framing. Guns, germs, and steel are proximate; biogeography and continental axes are ultimate.
- **Path dependence.** Common framework across all readings: early conditions shape long-run outcomes via accumulating norms, infrastructure, or institutional lock-in.
