**Purpose.** The 37-page Midterm 1 study guide (`Midterm_1_Study_Guide.md`, written 2026-04-17) was built from the six required readings, the syllabus, and the sample short-essay PDF. ## Lecture Day 1 (Mar 30) — Course Introduction / "What is Southeast Asia?"

**What the study guide covers.** Almost nothing from this lecture. The guide assumes the regional definition is settled and goes straight to the Hayton/mandala/Sinic-Indic content.

**Gaps from the lecture.**

1. **Southeast Asia as a political invention.** Ravanilla is explicit: "Southeast Asia" is a post-WWII strategic-military label, a "leftover category" defined by what it is *not* (not India, not China, not Pacific). Few spoke of it before the 1930s. The category persisted because of (a) geographic proximity, (b) shared historical interactions, (c) Cold War strategic thinking, and (d) institutionalisation through ASEAN. **Likely MC stem:** "When did the term 'Southeast Asia' come into common use, and why did it stick?"
2. **"Shared experience in diversity" as the unifying frame.** The lecture's own answer to "what holds the region together" is not geography or culture but a *shared experience of diversity*: ethnolinguistic variation (Austronesian, Tai-Kadai, Mon-Khmer, Vietnamese, Burmese, Tamil, Chinese), religious diversity (Islam, Buddhism, Christianity, traditional/folk), variation in political institutions (presidential, parliamentary, monarchy, single-party), and divergent colonial experiences (all but Thailand colonised, no common coloniser).
3. **The course's three central puzzles.** Ravanilla frames the entire course around (a) divergent HDI/GDP/Corruption outcomes shown side-by-side on slide 32, (b) divergent political institutions (slide 31), and (c) the question of why these vary so much across and within nation-states. The midterm short essays will likely echo these framings.
4. **Four learning goals** (slide 33): understand the world through SEA, learn political economy, develop empirical skills, strengthen voice. The "political economy toolkit" framing is especially load-bearing for what counts as a good answer.
5. **Concrete comparative numbers worth memorising for MC:** SEA land area 1.737M mi² vs US 3.120M mi²; SEA population ~700M vs China 1.412B, India 1.406B, US 331M; SEA total GDP ~$3.6T (smaller than UK alone). These are exactly the kind of factoids that fit MC question stems.

## Lecture Day 2 (Apr 1) — Pre-Colonial History / Rise and Fall of Mandalas

**What the study guide covers.** Mandala definition, Hayton readings, Funan/Srivijaya/Champa, Nusantao, Out-of-Taiwan model. Strong on the descriptive content.

**Gaps from the lecture.**

1. **The political-economy framework as the course's master analytical move.** This is the single biggest gap. Ravanilla spends roughly a third of LD2 building a *rational-choice institutional* framework: institutions are equilibrium responses to actors optimising under constraints. He explicitly applies it to the mandala (the mandala is "an optimal solution" to ecology + population + security + legitimacy + limited capacity constraints). This framework is the theoretical lens for the whole course and almost certainly the framing for any conceptual MC question. The study guide names "institutionalised village governance" and "extractive institutions" but does not teach the underlying constraints-and-optimisation logic.
2. **Five named constraints on premodern rulers** (slide 35–37): ecology, population, security, legitimacy, limited monitoring/enforcement capacity. Each maps to an "institutional solution":
   - Ecology → hydraulic infrastructure (barays, canals)
   - Population → agricultural surplus
   - Legitimacy → divine kingship + monumental religion (temples)
   - Security → organised but localised warfare
   - Limited capacity → mandala (concentric authority, patron-client)
   This table appears in lecture (slide 48). It is the single most testable framework Ravanilla introduces. The study guide does not use it.
3. **Self-undermining institutions / "the same features that enable rise generate vulnerability."** Recurring lecture motif (slide 11, 51, 67). The mandala's strengths (flexibility, decentralisation) are also its weaknesses (fragmentation, defection). Ravanilla uses the **Helm's Deep / Two Towers analogy** (slide 60) — a fortress's drainage culvert is also its point of attack. **Likely SAQ:** "Why did the mandalas decline?" Answer must invoke the self-undermining logic, not just external shocks.
4. **The fall of Angkor as a multi-channel breakdown** (slide 57–58). Lecture lists four sources of vulnerability (hydraulic fragility, patron-client defection, legitimacy shocks, weak peripheral control), maps each to historical evidence (14th–15th c. droughts/floods, Hindu-to-Theravada shift, Ayutthaya raids, succession disputes). The study guide treats Angkor only in passing.
5. **Srivijaya's collapse mechanism.** Lecture frames it as a *technological-shock* story: Chinese junk-ship advances (slide 63–64) made Srivijaya redundant as an intermediary, China traded directly with smaller polities, Srivijaya's monopoly rents evaporated, then Chola raids and Majapahit displacement finished it. The study guide mentions the Chola raid of 1025 but not the underlying technological displacement story.
6. **"Catalogue of institutions" comparative table** (slide 10). Ravanilla puts mandala alongside antebellum slavery, sharecropping, traffic norms, academic publishing, bureaucratic corruption, and grading curves to show all are equilibrium institutional responses. This is the "generalise beyond a single case" learning goal in action and could appear as an MC stem ("which of the following is *not* an example of an institution as equilibrium response?").
7. **Standard-narrative critique of Indianisation.** Lecture explicitly trains students to push back: why do Southeast Asians appear as passive recipients? Why would India spread culture but not conquer? Why selective adoption (no caste system)? "This is not an explanation. It is a perspective." The study guide notes this in passing under Hayton but doesn't drill it.
8. **The "Out of Taiwan" model and its critique.** Robert Blust + Peter Bellwood's proto-Austronesian Taiwan-origin migration model is named and then challenged: rice genetics suggest south-to-north travel, Pacific pig and rat came from Indochina not Taiwan. The study guide names Nusantao but doesn't pose Out-of-Taiwan as the contrasting hypothesis Ravanilla wants students to weigh.

## Lecture Day 3 (Apr 6) — Sinicized vs Indianised Southeast Asia

**What the study guide covers.** Sinic-Indic distinction, Dell/Lane/Querubin paper in depth, 1698 boundary, 33% consumption gap, civic-capital mechanism. Strong coverage.

**Gaps from the lecture.**

1. **Recap-as-curriculum.** The first 13 slides are a *recap* of the political-economy framework from LD2. Ravanilla repeats the rational-choice + optimisation + self-undermining-institutions logic. This signals he treats it as foundational. The study guide should treat it as foundational too.
2. **Why state *type* matters for development** (slide 16). Ravanilla lists four state functions: protect property rights, collect taxes, provide public goods/redistribute, wage wars. A good SAQ on Sinic vs Indic should explicitly invoke these four functions, not just "civic capital."
3. **The Dai Viet vs Khmer table** (slide 21, Table I from DLQ). Six paired contrasts: colonial outpost vs Indic patron-client; bureaucratic continuity since 939 CE vs decline after Siam invasion 1430; centralised impersonal bureaucracy vs decentralised personalistic; uniform territorial admin vs personalistic political appointments; institutionalised village chiefs (elected since 1461) vs personalistic land distribution; bureaucratic local taxation/military recruitment vs temple-based public finance. The study guide has most of these but the **1430 Siam invasion of Khmer** is an underused date worth indexing.
4. **Beyond-SEA comparative cases** (slide 15). Ravanilla extends the same Sinic/Indic logic to Europe vs China, North vs South America, Korea vs Japan, Rwanda vs Somalia. **Likely MC stem:** "Which of the following pairs illustrates the same institutions-cause-development logic as Dai Viet vs Khmer?"
5. **The causal chain spelled out as a single sentence** (slide 31): "Dai Viet State → institutionalized village governance → collective action (civic participation, paying taxes) → public goods provision → long-run economic development." And the mirror: "Mandala-style polity → diffuse, personalized authority → weaker institutionalized village governance → more fragmented collective action → thinner local public goods provision → weaker long-run economic development." Memorise this verbatim — it is the canonical answer template.
6. **Pointer to *Why Nations Fail*** on slide 32 with the Nogales US/Mexico image — Ravanilla is explicitly bridging DLQ to Acemoglu-Robinson here. The study guide doesn't flag this bridge.

## Lecture Day 4 (Apr 8) — The Early Colonial Era / Why Did Europe Win?

**What the study guide covers.** Diamond's Yali question, guns-germs-steel, proximate-vs-ultimate. Acemoglu/Robinson's reversal-of-development applied to Moluccas.

**Gaps from the lecture.** This is the lecture with the largest delta from the study guide.

1. **The whole "European Diversion" arc on the Fall of Rome → Feudalism → Towns → Nation-States** (slides 13–22). Ravanilla spends 10+ slides on European institutional history because he wants students to argue Europe won not by geography or race but by *institutional development sequence*. The chain runs: Roman fiscal/military overreach + barbarian invasions → local autarky needed → feudalism (lord-vassal-serf, military-protection-for-land) → trade fairs and merchant coalitions (Maghribi, Champagne, Hanseatic) → Italian/French/Rhine/Baltic free towns → centralised nation-states with capital markets → caravels and great expeditions. **This is THE answer template for "Why did Europe win?"** and the study guide does not lay it out. The sample A/A+ essay implicitly uses this arc — Ravanilla wants you to make it explicit.
2. **Gallup-Sachs-Mellinger (1999) "Geography and Economic Development" + Rodrik-Subramanian-Trebbi (2004) "Institutions Rule"** are named-and-cited on slide 24. These are not in the assigned readings but are the academic anchors for the geography-vs-institutions debate. **Likely MC pairing:** author → claim.
3. **Feudalism mechanism diagrams** (slides 17–19). Lord–serf economic exchange (land + protection ↔ labour). Lord–vassal political-military exchange (fiefdom ↔ military protection). Hierarchical structure (Emperor → Kings/Dukes/Margraves/Counts → Knights). Feudalism as a *decentralised solution to security* — exact mirror of mandala as decentralised solution to limited capacity. Ravanilla wants students to see the structural parallel between European feudalism and the SEA mandala.
4. **Treaty of Tordesillas (1494)** — divides the non-Christian world between Castile and Portugal (slide 28). Treaty of Saragossa (1529) extends it. **Likely MC chronology anchor.**
5. **Phase I Early Colonial actors with optimisation logic** (slides 31–37). For both Portugal and Spain, Ravanilla decomposes: King's motive → Opportunity → Constraint → Optimal strategy → Did they want to settle/colonise? → Colony duration. This is the political-economy framework applied to colonisers, mirror image of the mandala application. The study guide has the colonisation facts but not this framework.
   - **Portugal:** glory + gold; spice monopoly; silk road taxed/deep seas hard/Tordesillas; navigate, find new routes, build trading posts, fortress (**Fort Kastela in Maluku**); 1511–1641. Dates: Dias 1488 Cape of Good Hope, da Gama 1498 India, Cabral 1500 Brazil, Albuquerque 1511 Malacca.
   - **Spain:** God + glory + gold; spice monopoly + Manila-Acapulco trade; same constraints; circumnavigate, Galleon trade, **Intramuros** fortress; 333-year colony. Dates: Columbus 1492, Balboa 1513, Magellan 1520 (dies in Philippines), Elcano 1522 circumnavigation.
6. **The Caravel and Galleon as technology shifts** (slides 27, 30, 36). "From coastal barge to Caravel (Portuguese 1450s, Prince Henry the Navigator)." Technology is the missing variable that makes "Why Europe?" tractable.
7. **Mercantilism as the dominant economic doctrine** of the 1500s — trade as zero-sum, gold/silver accumulation, protectionism. Named on slide 27. Useful concept for any answer about colonial *motivation*.
8. **Douglass North's institutions definition** (slide 4 of the appended LD5/colonial-legacies block): "Institutions are humanly devised constraints that shape human interaction." **Formal vs informal** institutions (laws/constitutions vs norms/customs). "Rules of the game" vs "players" (firms/governments). **Highly likely MC definition question** — Ravanilla cites the Nobel laureate by name.
9. **Inclusive vs Extractive institutions full taxonomy** (slides 7–8). The study guide has the binary. The lecture has the four-quadrant 2x2 (political × economic, inclusive × extractive) with worked examples: USA/UK/South Korea inclusive-inclusive; South Africa post-1994 inclusive political + extractive economic (apartheid wealth concentration); China extractive political + post-1978 inclusive economic; North Korea fully extractive. **Memorise the 2x2; it is highly testable.**
10. **South Africa and China as worked AJR cases** (slides 10–11). Lecture spends real time on non-SEA AJR applications. The study guide treats AJR purely through the Moluccas. Ravanilla may ask an MC about why South Africa or post-1978 China matter for AJR's framework.

## Lecture Day 5 (Apr 13) — Colonial Institutional Legacies / Dell-Olken

**What the study guide covers.** Dell-Olken Cultivation System, catchment area, infrastructure persistence, two-Dutch-colonialisms reconciliation.

**Gaps from the lecture.**

1. **"Two phases of colonialism: Who"** (slide 22). Early presence 1500s–late 1700s (Portuguese Malacca/Spice Islands 1511–1641) vs High Imperialism (1800s–WWII). The study guide treats colonialism as a single story; the lecture explicitly separates phases.
2. **The Dutch optimisation problem framed as a decision tree** (slides 16–18). Dutch problem: monopolise spice supply → constraints: Portuguese still around, native attacks, spices grown across many islands → optimal response: defeat Portuguese (co-opt natives), stop attacks (co-opt natives), monopolise (extractive institutions). Then *which* extractive institutions vary by island:
   - **Ambon:** intensify pre-existing extractive institutions, retain Ambonese elite as middle-men, forced labour on cloves.
   - **Banda Islands:** no central authority to coerce → genocide, eliminate pre-existing institutions, plantation society with Dutch landlords + imported Indian and native slaves; **build Batavia as capital/army base**.
   - **Tidore, Ternate, Bacan (absolutist monarch islands):** suppress production, **burn all spice trees and plants**.
   This island-by-island menu of extractive institutions is more granular than the study guide. **Likely SAQ:** "Why did the Dutch use different extractive institutions across the Moluccas?"
3. **AJR's "Reversal of Fortune"** as a named, generalisable empirical pattern (slide 20). Societies wealthy in 1500 are often poorer today; sparsely populated/less developed regions (North America, Australia) got inclusive institutions; densely populated wealthy regions (Latin America, India) got extractive ones. Includes the famous AJR scatter plot of log GDP/cap 1995 vs log population density 1500. **Likely MC stem on the inverse correlation.**
4. **The 60% spice supply cut is in lecture too** (slide 19) but the lecture also lists *consequences* as a five-bullet sequence: prices jacked up; polities turned inward and abandoned trade; small city-states destroyed their own spice production; Mandala capitals moved inland away from ports; rest of world advanced while SEA economic activity halted. The study guide has these scattered; lecture has them as a clean sequence.

## Lecture Day 6 (Apr 15) — Struggle for Independence & War Years

**What the study guide covers.** Stubbs, three effects of war (destructive/formative/redistributive), Korean War procurement numbers, Philippine negative case, Cold War aid totals.

**Gaps from the lecture.** Significant. Lecture covers the *colonial-to-postwar transition* in much more country-by-country detail than the study guide.

1. **Country-by-country colonial taxonomy with institutionalisation strength** (slides 4–14). Ravanilla evaluates each colonial regime by *how strong its institutionalisation was*. This is testable as MC and as comparative SAQ material.
   - **British Malaya:** Three stages (Straits Settlements 1786/1819/1824 → Federated Malay States 1896 → Unfederated 1914 + Sarawak/Sabah White Rajah Brooke). **Strong institutionalisation:** centralised bureaucracy, legal systems, infrastructure, efficient tax collection. Tin and rubber the economic engines. World's third-largest railroad network.
   - **British Burma:** annexed 1826–1885 over three Anglo-Burmese wars. Incorporated into British India. **Divide-and-rule** tactics deepened ethnic divisions; legacy is today's ongoing insurgencies and fragmented society. Important: the study guide says little about Burma.
   - **French Indochina:** piecemeal Vietnam conquest 1859–1885 in 25 years. **Did not dismantle Dai Viet — turned it into a puppet regime.** Cambodia annexed 1863 for survival; Laos 1893 as buffer.
   - **Dutch Indonesia:** VOC 1602; most territory annexed in a 16-year span late 19th c. **Weak institutionalisation despite 300+ years of presence:** indirect rule via local elites, focus on extraction not governance. **Aceh War 1873–1904, 100,000 casualties.** Bali only fell early 20th c.
   - **Spanish + American Philippines:** Spanish 1565–1898, Philippine Revolution 1896–98, Philippine-American War 1899–1901, American 1901–1935, Commonwealth 1935–41.
   - **Thailand:** never formally colonised; buffer between French and British. But "extra-territoriality, land concessions, terms of trade = colonisation by proxy." Important nuance.
2. **Modern borders as a colonial creation** (slide 16) and **modern bureaucratic states layered on top of patron-client/mandala** (slide 19). The new patron-client relationships are "single-stranded to multi-stranded" with "entrenchment of elites" and "impact on elections" — Ravanilla is bridging colonial legacy directly to contemporary clientelism.
3. **Population/immigration consequence:** Java 1800 5M → 1900 42M; ~50M ethnic Chinese in SEA today; varying integration (strong in VN/CAM/TH/PH, weaker in IN/MY/SG). Concrete MC-style numbers.
4. **Benedict Anderson's "imagined communities" definition** (slide 30). Four features: imagined, limited, sovereign, deep horizontal comradeship. Cited by name. **Highly likely MC definition question.** The study guide does not have this at all.
5. **Variation in nationalist movements** (slide 31). Philippines earliest (1896 Revolution); Indonesia 1920s–30s (Hatta, Sukarno); Vietnam Communist late 1930s; Burma Saya San uprising 1930–31; Malaya little; Cambodia/Laos very little. Why this variation? Communication, education levels, intelligentsia, political change in Europe, international pressures (facilitators); native inferiority assumptions, elite incentives to keep status quo, colonial suppression (inhibitors). **Likely SAQ:** "Why did nationalism take such different forms across SEA before WWII?"
6. **Philippines deep-dive (slides 33–55)** — this is the *case study* Ravanilla emphasises in LD6 even though Stubbs is the assigned reading. The lecture takes the Stubbs-Philippines-as-negative-case point and builds it out:
   - Pre-Spanish: petty states, mandalas, Sulu/Maguindanao Muslim sultanates, Luzon chieftainships.
   - Spanish (1565–1898): forcibly unified, named after King Philip II, central government in Manila, common religion (Catholicism). **Encomienda system** — encomiendas granted by King in exchange for protecting natives, converting them, promoting education. Encomenderos exacted onerous taxes, used forced labour, expropriated property, sired *mestizos*. **Principalia**: former chiefs/sultans turned into noble class; tribute and forced labour exacted in exchange for continued local rule. *This is the mechanism by which patron-client survived Spanish rule.*
   - **Ilustrados:** Rizal, the *Noli Me Tangere*, the *El Filibusterismo*, Rizal executed by firing squad. 1896 Revolution → 1897 cease-fire → 1898 Spanish-American War → Battle of Manila Bay → Treaty of Paris (Spain cedes to US) → June 12, 1898 declaration of independence (US doesn't recognise) → 1899 Philippine-American War, US wins.
   - American period: "benevolent assimilation," compromise with the existing principalia, granted them political power first locally then nationally. **No plan to build a centralised state or undermine local patrons.** US in a hurry to implant American-style democracy mostly to protect US interests.
   - **Consequence for today:** dominance of oligarchy (Aquinos/Cojuangcos, Marcoses/Romualdezes); patron-client politics; weak parties/ideology; weak state (poor tax capacity, corruptible military, can't govern fringes); insecure property rights; "booty/crony capitalism." This is the contemporary endpoint of the mandala→Spanish→American institutional trajectory.
   **The Philippines walkthrough is almost certainly testable as a long SAQ** — it ties together every analytical move the course has made (mandala persistence, encomienda extractive institution, principalia patron-client, weak American state-building, no Cold War strong-state outcome per Stubbs). The study guide treats Philippines briefly as a Stubbs negative case; the lecture treats it as a 23-slide capstone.
7. **"Years of illusion" framing for the 1920s–30s** (slides 27–29) — colonial powers complacent on the surface, anti-colonial nationalism forming beneath. Useful conceptual frame.

## Verdict on the Existing 37-Page Guide

The reading-content portion of the guide (Parts I and II) holds up well — Ravanilla is testing the readings closely and the per-reading entries are the right depth. But the guide *systematically under-weights the lectures' theoretical scaffolding*: Ravanilla teaches a rational-choice institutional framework (constraints → institutions → equilibrium → self-undermining) and uses it as the master analytical move from LD2 onward. The lectures also contain large blocks of material the readings do not cover at all (the European Diversion arc, the Dutch optimisation menu, the Philippines case walkthrough, Anderson's imagined communities, the Inclusive/Extractive 2x2 with non-SEA worked cases).

A v2 is not strictly required, but Edgar should at minimum add a one-page "Lecture-Only Frameworks" supplement to the index card or to the morning-of review, anchored on the five highest-priority items above. That gets him from "I know the readings" to "I can answer in Ravanilla's vocabulary."
