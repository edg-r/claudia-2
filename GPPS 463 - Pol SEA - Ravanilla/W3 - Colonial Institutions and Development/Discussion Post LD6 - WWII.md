# Discussion Post — Lecture Day 6
## How did World War II impact Southeast Asia?
**Due: April 14, 2026 by 5:00 PM**

---

## Question

Stubbs says war and Cold War pressure built the strong states that drove Asia's export boom. If that is true, why did the Philippines, which went through the same wars, end up weak and poor?

**Why this matters:** If war alone cannot explain the Philippine failure, then colonial institutions, not geostrategy, may be doing the real work in shaping who develops and who does not.

---

## Notes on the argument

Stubbs's core claim: the sequence of wars from WWII through the Cold War drove export-oriented industrialization in the seven successful East and Southeast Asian economies (Japan, South Korea, Taiwan, Hong Kong, Singapore, Malaysia, Thailand) through three mechanisms:

1. **Strong state:** WWII destroyed the old social order and weakened entrenched social forces, allowing new institutional states to emerge relatively unconstrained. Cold War security threats then expanded state capacity (both coercive and civil) as governments mobilized against Communist insurgencies. States that are strong relative to society can implement export-oriented policy reforms without being captured by import-substitution interests.

2. **Capital:** U.S. military procurement (Korean War, Vietnam War), direct aid, and Japanese foreign direct investment injected the capital needed to build infrastructure and develop export manufacturing capacity. War-generated revenues funded bureaucracies capable of planning and implementing industrial policy.

3. **Markets:** The Korean and Vietnam wars opened the U.S. market to Asian exports and created regional procurement markets. The booming U.S. economy could absorb enormous quantities of cheap manufactured goods from the region.

**The Philippine exception:** Stubbs acknowledges the Philippines as the obvious counterexample. Spanish and American colonial rule left a legacy of weak central government and powerful regional strongmen. Neither WWII nor the Huk rebellion produced centralization; instead, martial law under Marcos devolved power back to regional autocrats after a brief period of centralization. No Cold War threat was sufficient to build a strong developmental state because the institutional substrate — created by colonial rule — was too fragmented.

**The tension with W3 readings:** This is where Stubbs connects directly to Acemoglu & Robinson and Dell & Olken from Lecture Day 5. Those readings establish that colonial institutions have very long-run effects on development through path dependence. Stubbs implicitly assumes that WWII disruption can break institutional path dependence (by destroying the old social order), but the Philippine case suggests that the colonial institutional legacy can survive even major shocks. The question worth raising in class: does geostrategic context operate through institutions (by reshaping them) or independently of them?
