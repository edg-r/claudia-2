## Notes on the argument

Hicken's central comparison is counterintuitive: Thailand suffered a much deeper immediate crisis, but the shock created political pressure for institutional reform; the Philippines suffered less, but that relative success reduced the urgency for reform. Thailand's crisis helped push through the 1997 constitution, weakened old parties, drew business elites more directly into politics, and created the conditions for Thai Rak Thai and Thaksin's unusually centralized majority government. That consolidation helped address some crisis-era vulnerabilities, especially non-performing loans and stalled credit, but it also created new risks around politicized state resources and executive overreach.

The Philippines is the opposite case. Ramos-era reforms made the financial sector more resilient before 1997, so the downturn was milder. But because the crisis did not become a deeper political crisis, post-Ramos governments lacked both the urgency and the coalition capacity to tackle structural problems like public debt, weak tax collection, and government-owned corporation liabilities. Hicken's punchline is that a milder crisis can be politically dangerous because it lets governments avoid costly reforms while vulnerabilities keep accumulating.

The link back to the course arc is institutions and political incentives. Hicken is not saying crisis severity mechanically causes reform. He is saying crisis effects are filtered through domestic political architecture: party systems, executive capacity, business-state relations, and coalition incentives determine whether a shock becomes reform, paralysis, or predation.
