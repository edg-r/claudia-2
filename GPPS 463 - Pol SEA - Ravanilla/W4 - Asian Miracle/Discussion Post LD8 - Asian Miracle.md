# Discussion Post — Lecture Day 8
## Why did only some countries experience the Asian Miracle?
**Due: April 21, 2026 by 5:00 PM**

---

## Question

Jansen says Thailand grew fast because of a dynamic Chinese-Thai entrepreneurial class and a cautious, pro-business state, not a developmental state like Korea's. If the miracle can run on a non-interventionist state, what is actually doing the work across the East Asian cases, and why did countries with stronger planning apparatuses like the Philippines still fall behind?

**Why this matters:** If Thailand and Korea both made the miracle with very different states, the common ingredient is probably an entrepreneurial class the state does not capture or crowd out, which pushes the explanation back toward W3's institutions story rather than W4's industrial policy story.

---

## Notes on the argument

Jansen's core claim: Thailand posted 6.6% real GDP growth from 1950 to 2000 through heavy factor accumulation (investment/GDP went from 13.6% in the 1950s to 40.7% in 1990–96, savings 11.5% to 34.1%) plus real but modest TFP growth (around 1.1%/yr, higher than most of the developing world but low by Young/Krugman's skeptical benchmark). The deeper driver was an ethnic-Chinese entrepreneurial class fully assimilated by the 1950s, operating inside a cautious pro-business state that ran low inflation (~5% over 1970–97) and kept government expenditure at 16.4% of GDP. Jansen is explicit: "the Thai state has been one of the least interventionist" (p. 356).

**The four East Asian commonalities (Jansen §III):** dynamic private entrepreneurs, macroeconomic stability, openness to trade and capital, and human capital investment. These show up in Thailand, Korea, Taiwan, Singapore, and Malaysia despite very different state architectures.

**What is unique to Thailand:** no direct colonial rule (the Bowring Treaty in 1855 came closer to indirect colonial integration through unequal treaties), a large agricultural land frontier that absorbed labor for a century, a small non-interventionist state, and a very rapid demographic transition.

**The tension with Ravanilla's lecture frame:** The lecture title asks why only *some* countries experienced the miracle. Jansen's four commonalities list mechanisms, but it does not cleanly explain the scope restriction. Countries with weaker entrepreneurs and more extractive colonial legacies, like the Philippines and Indonesia, did not make the cut. This is where W3 reaches in: Dell & Olken's extractive/rent-seeking institutions and Acemoglu's reversal-of-fortune argument predict exactly the pattern Jansen's four-feature checklist cannot.

**The Krugman/Young vs. Jansen debate:** Young (1994) and Krugman (1994) argued that East Asian growth was "perspiration not inspiration" — all factor accumulation, little TFP, therefore doomed to diminishing returns. Jansen concedes that factor accumulation dominates but pushes back on three counts: (1) East Asian TFP is still large in comparative terms (~1.1%/yr vs. 0.2% Latin America, negative Middle East/Africa); (2) catch-up technology adoption counts as real TFP; (3) Thailand's TFP ranks 2nd or 3rd within East Asia, which signals that open small-state arrangements can be efficient even without industrial planning. The 1997 crisis, in Jansen's read, was a policy-timing failure (financial liberalization before the banking system could handle it), not structural rot.

**Connection to LD6 Philippine exception:** The LD6 post argued that if Stubbs's war-and-geostrategy story cannot explain Philippine failure, colonial institutions are doing the real work. Jansen extends the same logic: if the miracle can run on a non-interventionist state (Thailand) *or* a developmental state (Korea), then the variable that differentiates miracle from non-miracle countries is not state architecture. It is whether the state sits on institutions that let an entrepreneurial class form and compound — an institutions story, not an industrial policy story.

**Why this is worth raising in class:** Ravanilla's lecture question invites a developmental state answer. Jansen undermines that reflex with the Thai case. The seminar payoff is forcing the group to say what, precisely, differentiates Thailand, Korea, and Malaysia (miracle) from the Philippines and Indonesia (not), given that the four common features and institutional lens both travel and neither alone is sufficient.
