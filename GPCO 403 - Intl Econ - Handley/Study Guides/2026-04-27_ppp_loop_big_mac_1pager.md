# GPCO 403 - Apr. 27: PPP, LOOP, and the Big Mac Index

## Bottom Line
The Apr. 27 class applies the law of one price (LOOP) and purchasing power parity (PPP) to exchange rates. The core move is simple: convert prices into a common currency, compare them, and ask whether goods-market arbitrage should push exchange rates or prices back toward parity. The Big Mac Index is useful because it makes that logic visible, but it also shows why PPP is a long-run benchmark rather than a tight short-run rule.

## Core Logic
LOOP applies to one good: the same product should sell for the same price in different countries once prices are expressed in one currency. If a sweater is cheaper in London than New York after exchange-rate conversion and shipping costs, arbitrageurs should buy in London and sell in New York until the price gap closes. With trade costs, the no-arbitrage condition becomes a band, not a single equal price: price gaps can persist as long as they are smaller than transport, tariff, tax, and transaction costs.

PPP generalizes LOOP from one good to a whole consumption basket. Absolute PPP says the real exchange rate should equal 1:

`q = E * P_foreign / P_home = 1`

If `q > 1`, the foreign basket is expensive relative to the home basket, so home has experienced a real depreciation. If `q < 1`, home has experienced a real appreciation. Relative PPP turns the same idea into a growth-rate prediction: the rate of nominal depreciation should approximately equal the home inflation rate minus the foreign inflation rate.

## Big Mac Application
The Economist's Big Mac Index compares the dollar price of a Big Mac across countries. A currency looks overvalued when the local Big Mac costs more than the U.S. Big Mac after conversion into dollars; it looks undervalued when it costs less. The exam-relevant intuition is:

- Expensive local Big Mac in dollar terms -> local currency appears overvalued -> PPP predicts depreciation.
- Cheap local Big Mac in dollar terms -> local currency appears undervalued -> PPP predicts appreciation.
- Equal dollar prices -> LOOP holds for this good.

The index is deliberately imperfect. A Big Mac contains many non-traded inputs, especially labor, rent, electricity, distribution, and local taxes. That is exactly why it is pedagogically useful: it shows both the appeal of price-based exchange-rate benchmarks and the reasons those benchmarks fail in practice.

## Why PPP Fails
PPP fails most often because the assumptions behind arbitrage are too strong. Goods are not always identical; markets are not perfectly competitive; transport costs, tariffs, quotas, VAT, and other regulations create wedges; and many components of consumer prices are non-traded. Richer countries also tend to have higher non-traded service costs, so simple PPP comparisons can misread income-level differences as currency misalignment.

The dollar-denominated debt reading adds the policy risk: when governments borrow in U.S. dollars, a local-currency depreciation makes repayment more expensive in domestic-currency terms. PPP and exchange-rate movements are therefore not just textbook price comparisons; they affect fiscal stress, capital flows, and crisis vulnerability.

## What to Know for Class and the Midterm
Be able to compute a common-currency price comparison, interpret overvaluation or undervaluation, explain why trade costs create an arbitrage band, define the real exchange rate, and state relative PPP as depreciation equals the inflation differential. For short answers, do not just say "PPP fails"; name the wedge: non-traded inputs, transport costs, tariffs/taxes, market power, quality differences, or measurement problems.
