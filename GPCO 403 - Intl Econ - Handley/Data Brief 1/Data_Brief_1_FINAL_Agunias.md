## Part B. Short Interpretation (220 words)

### Q1. Best measure of living standards

GDP per capita in current USD is the best first-pass measure of average living standards because it normalizes aggregate income by population and expresses it in a common currency for cross-country comparison. By this measure, Australia (US$65,373) is poorer than the United States (US$86,601), a gap of roughly 25%. The key limitation is that current-USD figures ignore price-level differences across countries; a dollar buys a different basket in Sydney than in New York. A PPP-adjusted comparison would narrow this gap.

### Q2. Sectoral balances

The sectoral balances identity states that CA = (S − I) + (T − G), or current account = private net lending + government net lending. Rearranging: Private = CA − Government. For Australia in 2024: Private = −2.1% − (−1.8%) = −0.3% of GDP. The private sector (households and firms combined) is therefore a small net borrower, investing slightly more than it saves. The current account deficit is driven almost entirely by the government fiscal deficit, with private saving-investment roughly in balance.

### Q3. 10% AUD depreciation

A 10% AUD depreciation raises the AUD/USD rate. Using the WEO-implied 2024 rate of 1.5146, the new rate is 1.5146 × 1.10 = 1.6661. Holding nominal GDP in AUD constant at A$2,693B, new GDP in USD = 2,693 / 1.6661 = US$1,616.4B, a fall of 9.09% from US$1,778B. Real output is unchanged; only the USD valuation falls.
